Consensus in Practice: Chubby & ZooKeeper

Where the clean theory of Paxos meets the messy real world — how Google and the open-source community turned consensus into coordination services that thousands of applications actually use.

Part I

From Theory to Practice — Two Mindsets

For the last two lectures, this course lived in the world of foundations: replicated state machines, logical clocks, consistency models, and the Paxos protocol that lets a group of machines agree on a single value even when some of them fail. Those were beautiful, provable objects. Today the class crosses a line that the professor explicitly marked — we are now officially in the second half of the course, the half about real systems. From here on, the running question is not "can we prove this is correct?" but "what did engineers actually build, and why did they make the trade-offs they made?"

Before touching any real system, it is worth pausing on why the goalposts move. The reason we are learning this is that the skills you need to evaluate a real system are genuinely different from the skills you need to design a protocol — and confusing the two is a classic mistake.

Why the Goals Change

When you study a foundation or protocol (like Paxos), your job is to take a messy real problem and abstract it away until what remains is clean and elegant. You want a protocol that is explainable, easy to understand, and — crucially — provably correct. The entire payoff is intuition: a crisp mental model of the problem and its solution.

When you study a real system (like the ones today), the job inverts. Now the most important and hardest work is figuring out what assumptions actually hold in the real world — how often do machines fail, how skewed are the clocks, what does the workload look like? Given those assumptions, you might deliberately strengthen or weaken a textbook protocol so it fits reality better. You are thinking like a system architect deciding on a contract: what guarantees will I promise my users, and what will I demand from them in return?

The Real Insight of Systems Work

In practice, the biggest difficulty in building a system is rarely the code. It is agreeing on the contract — deciding precisely what your service promises and what it expects from its users. Once the specification is nailed down, the implementation usually "just follows." That is why so many of today's questions are open-ended judgement calls, not theorems.

Where We Are in the Course

Here is the map of everything covered so far, so today's material has somewhere to attach. The course has built up, in order: replicated state machines (run the same operations in the same order on many machines so they stay identical); ordering with logical clocks (deciding "what happened before what" without synchronized time); data consistency models (linearizability, sequential, causal — different promises about what reads can see); and fault tolerance via primary-backup replication and the Paxos consensus protocol.

Key Term — Coordination Service

A coordination service is a small, highly reliable distributed system whose entire job is to help other distributed systems coordinate — electing a leader, taking turns writing to shared storage, agreeing on configuration. It does not store your application's bulk data; it stores tiny amounts of metadata and hands out locks. Today's two systems, Chubby (Google's internal service) and ZooKeeper (open source), are both coordination services.

This Builds On Paxos

Everything today sits on top of Paxos from the previous lectures. Chubby and ZooKeeper both run a leader-based consensus protocol internally to keep their replicas identical. The new material is the packaging around that core: what interface to expose, how to handle client failures, and how to make it fast. Keep Paxos in your back pocket — it is the engine inside both systems.

Part II

Chubby — The Client's Contract

Chubby is an internal service at Google built to be a highly available coordination service. One quote from the Chubby paper sets the tone for the whole lecture: building Chubby was not meant to be a novel, research-worthy contribution. It was a real thing built for very real reasons, and the paper's job is to explain why the design decisions were made. That humility is the point — real systems are about good engineering judgement, not new theorems.

We will look at Chubby from two angles, in order. First, the client perspective: the contract, what a user can assume. Then, later, the implementation: how that contract is actually delivered. Always understand the promise before the mechanism.

Chubby Is a Lock Service

From the client's point of view, Chubby makes exactly two promises:

Chubby's Contract to Clients

1. Availability. As long as some replica is alive, the service does not fail — it will always be there to answer your requests.
2. Locking. At most one client can hold a given lock at any point in time. This is the mutual-exclusion guarantee that lets clients take turns.

That second promise — mutual exclusion — is the whole reason Chubby exists. If you can guarantee that only one machine holds "the lock" at a time, you can use that machine as the leader, the single writer, the one allowed to touch some shared resource. Everyone else waits.

The File System Abstraction

Here is a design decision that looks almost cosmetic but is deeply practical. Chubby does not invent a strange new API. Instead, it presents itself as a tiny Unix-like file system. There are files arranged in directories, with paths like /ls/cell/myapp/leader. And the trick: each file is associated with a lock. You acquire the lock by "acquiring" the file; you communicate by writing the file's contents.

Why a File System?

Every developer already understands files, paths, and directories. By dressing the lock service up as a file system, Chubby makes itself instantly familiar — you "open" a path, "acquire" it, "write" to it. This lowers the barrier to adoption enormously. The familiar shape is a feature.

Worked Example: Electing a Leader

Let's make this concrete with the prototypical use case from lecture. Suppose you have a database and a pool of worker processes, and you want only one worker at a time to write to the database — so the workers do not clobber each other's data. This is exactly what Chubby is for. Every worker runs the same snippet:

// every worker runs this same code
open("/ls/cell/myapp/leader")        // open the lock file in Chubby

if acquire("/ls/cell/myapp/leader") {   // try to grab the lock
    // SUCCESS — we are the leader
    setContents("/ls/cell/myapp/leader", myName)  // announce ourselves
    writeToDatabase(...)                // do the leader's work
    release("/ls/cell/myapp/leader")      // give up the lock
} else {
    // FAILED — we are NOT the leader
    leader = readContents("/ls/cell/myapp/leader")  // find out who is
}
1

Every worker opens the same lock file — a path inside Chubby that looks just like a file system path.

2

Every worker tries to acquire the lock on that file. Because of Chubby's mutual-exclusion guarantee, exactly one of them succeeds.

3

The winner now knows it is the leader. It writes its own name into the file's contents, both to announce itself and to record who is in charge.

4

The winner does the actual work — writing to the database — then releases the lock when done.

5

Every loser takes the else branch. It can discover who the current leader is simply by reading the lock file's contents. No central registry needed — the lock file is the registry.

The Lock and the Bulletin Board Are the Same File

Notice the elegance: a single Chubby file does double duty. Acquiring it provides mutual exclusion (only the leader can hold it), and its contents provide a shared bulletin board (everyone can read "who is the leader right now"). One primitive, two jobs.

Why a Lock Service, Not a Paxos Library?

This is the lecture's first big design question, and it has two independent axes worth separating carefully:

  • Lock vs. Paxos — do you hand the user a high-level lock, or the raw consensus primitive?
  • Service vs. Library — does it run as a separate service on its own machines, or as a library linked into the client's own program?
Key Distinction — Service vs. Library

A library is code you import directly into your program; it runs on your machine, in your process, and you are responsible for deploying and operating it. A service runs elsewhere — on different machines, deployed and operated by a different team — and you reach it over the network via requests and responses. The client and service do not share code or a deployment.

Chubby chose a lock service. Here is why, consolidated from the class discussion:

BenefitWhy It Matters
Easier to adoptApps start small, then realize they need availability. Adding a service is a few network calls; threading a library through your codebase means invasive changes everywhere.
Deployment offloadedThe whole burden of running replicas, checking they're alive, keeping them networked — all handed to the Chubby team. The client just talks to "one server."
Locks are easier than PaxosTelling a developer "whoever grabs the lock may proceed" is far simpler than "here's a protocol that decides on a value." Locks match the developer's intent directly.
Language-agnosticA service is reached over the network, so it doesn't matter what language it's written in. A library is tied to a specific language.
Feels centralizedFrom the client's view, Chubby is a single entity — even though it's secretly many replicas. The replication is hidden.
The Big Idea — Hide the Hard Part Behind a Simple Abstraction

Without Chubby, every team at Google needing leader election would have to build their own fault-tolerant locking on top of Paxos. Chubby's core value is that it hides all of the fault tolerance and just says: "the lock is alive for you, always." A genuinely hard distributed-systems problem becomes a one-line acquire() call. That packaging — not a new algorithm — is the contribution.

The professor noted that the first two benefits (easier adoption, deployment offloaded) are really benefits of the service architecture in general, not anything specific to Chubby. They apply any time you turn a library into a service.

Part III

Sessions, Leases & the Lost-Lock Problem

Chubby's contract has a dangerous gap, and finding it is the motivation for this whole part. Chubby promises that it (the service) never dies. But it promises nothing about the client. So a frightening question arises:

What If the Lock-Holder Dies?

Imagine a client acquires a lock — it is now the leader — and then it crashes without ever calling release(). The lock was supposed to be handed back. But the client that held it is gone. Is the lock now stuck forever? Is the whole system doomed, with every other worker waiting eternally for a lock that will never be released?

The Footgun — A Crashed Client Holding a Lock

A naive lock implementation deadlocks here. If holding a lock requires the holder to release it, but the holder can crash, then a single crash can freeze the entire application. Any real locking service must have a way to reclaim locks from dead clients.

The Fix: Sessions and Leases

Chubby solves this with sessions backed by leases. The idea is that a lock is not held forever by default — it is held only as long as the client keeps proving it is still alive.

Key Term — Lease

A lease is a lock granted for a bounded window of time. During that window, the service guarantees the lease is valid (the holder keeps the lock). To keep the lease, the client must periodically send keep-alive messages before it expires. If the lease window passes with no keep-alive — because the client crashed, or the network broke — the session is declared dead and the lock is released automatically by the service.

1

The client library opens a session with Chubby and acquires a lock. The lease has a fixed duration — say, 10 seconds.

2

Well before the 10 seconds expire — say at the 9-second mark — the client sends a keep-alive. Chubby extends the lease by another 10 seconds.

3

This repeats indefinitely while the client is healthy: keep-alive, extend, keep-alive, extend.

4

If the client crashes, the keep-alives stop. The lease runs out. Chubby sees no keep-alive arrived, declares the session invalid, and automatically releases the lock so other clients can grab it.

A healthy client refreshes its lease before each expiry. When it crashes, the next lease expires with no keep-alive, and Chubby reclaims the lock.

But Clocks Are Unsynchronized — How Does This Even Work?

An alarm bell should be ringing. Earlier lectures hammered home that clock synchronization is hard — different machines' clocks drift apart and you cannot trust them to agree. So how can a client and the server possibly agree on when a lease "expires"?

The answer is a beautifully pragmatic trick: make the lease so long that clock drift becomes irrelevant. Leases last seconds or tens of seconds. Clock drift between two machines, even when bad, is on the order of milliseconds — it is not going to exceed a second. So the client uses a conservative under-approximation: with a 10-second lease, it might start sending keep-alives at the 9-second mark, leaving a huge safety margin. The error from clock drift is tiny compared to that margin.

"Foundations Clean, Systems Dirty — But They Work"

This is the lecture's recurring motto. You can never get a hard timing guarantee out of a lease — if time stretched arbitrarily, or clocks drifted catastrophically, you could still be wrong. But in practice, real systems measure their clock skew (e.g. "the 99.9999th percentile of skew is 35 ms"), pick a conservative bound well above it (e.g. 100 ms), and move on with their lives. It is not provably perfect. It works.

A Subtler Bug: The Delayed Write

There is one more problem, and it is the deepest one in the Chubby section. Picture the sequence: a client grabs the lock, then issues a write to the database. But the database is across a network. The write message gets arbitrarily delayed in flight. While it is delayed, the client's lease expires, the lock is released, and a different client grabs it and starts writing. Now the original, delayed write finally arrives — applied by a client that no longer holds the lock. Two writers have effectively collided.

The Core Problem — Effect Without the Lock

The lock was meant to make writes mutually exclusive. But the effect of a write (it landing in the database) can happen well after the request was sent. So an effect can land on the database at a moment when the sender no longer holds the lock. The lock was held when you asked, but not when the write happened.

Chubby's proposed fix is a lock ID (sometimes called a sequencer). When you hold the lock, you get an ID. You pass this ID along with any operation that depends on holding the lock. The resource being protected — or the actor doing the effect — then re-checks with Chubby: "I'm carrying lock ID X; do I actually still hold this lock right now?" Only if Chubby confirms the ID is current does the operation proceed.

1

Client acquires the lock and receives a lock ID — a token proving it was the holder.

2

The client passes this lock ID along with the write request to whoever performs the effect.

3

Before the effect is actually applied, the actor asks Chubby: "Is lock ID X still valid?"

4

If yes, the write proceeds. If the lease had expired and someone else took the lock, the ID is stale, Chubby says no, and the dangerous delayed write is rejected.

A Note on What's Really Guaranteed

Even with lock IDs, there is no perfect guarantee that "the effect happened strictly inside the lock period" — there is always a network in between and some estimation involved. The practical defense is to check how much lease time is left before starting: if only 100 ms remain, maybe don't start a risky write; if 5 seconds remain, you can probably finish. As always: conservative over-approximations, not certainty.

Part IV

Chubby Under the Hood — Implementation

We now flip from the contract to the machinery. The client saw Chubby as a single, immortal lock service. Underneath, that illusion is delivered by replication and consensus. Before any code, the engineers wrote down the requirements — because, as Part I argued, agreeing on the spec is where the real work is.

Requirements and the Paxos Cell

Chubby's System Requirements

Scale: thousands of clients interacting with a very small number of very small files (the files mostly hold locks, not data). High availability: the service must essentially never go down. Scalability: Chubby cannot become the bottleneck of the applications that depend on it. Event notification: a way to notify clients of changes to reduce wasteful polling.

A Chubby deployment is called a cell, and a cell is typically five replicas. The replicas run a leader-based Paxos protocol so that the file system's contents are identical across all of them — a replicated state machine offering strong consistency, tolerating up to two faults (recall: 2F+1 replicas tolerate F failures, so 5 replicas tolerate 2). On top of that, Chubby ships a thin client library that merely wraps the RPC calls to the service, so developers call a function instead of hand-rolling network requests.

A Chubby cell: five replicas run leader-based Paxos. The client talks through a thin library; the leader coordinates consensus to keep all replicas' files identical.

Coarse-Grained vs. Fine-Grained Locks

A pivotal design choice: should Chubby's locks be fine-grained (grab a lock per tiny action and immediately drop it) or coarse-grained (grab a lock once and hold it for a long stretch of work)? This choice ripples through performance, availability, and how hard Chubby is to build. Weigh both sides from the client's and the implementer's view:

Fine-Grained LocksCoarse-Grained Locks
Lots of acquire/release overhead — you pay more for locking relative to real workGrab once, do all your work, release once — overhead amortized
Demands very high availability from Chubby; a brief outage strands thousands of frequent lock requestsA few-second Chubby outage is survivable — most clients already hold their lock and keep working
Implementer's burden: huge scalability pressure (Chubby must handle massive lock traffic)Scaling responsibility shifts to clients; Chubby relaxes
Locks lost frequently → apps need aggressive defensive programmingLost locks are rare; only worry near lease expiry
If an app replica crashes, little is stranded (short leases)If an app replica crashes, the system can stall until the long (e.g. 10s) lease expires
The Big Idea — Chubby Chooses Coarse-Grained, On Purpose

Recall the top requirement: Chubby must not be the bottleneck. Fine-grained locks would put Chubby under relentless load and force near-perfect availability. Coarse-grained locks let Chubby relinquish scalability pressure to the clients — they hold their locks and keep working even if Chubby blinks offline for a few seconds. The cost is that a crashed app replica can stall the system until its long lease expires, but Chubby decides that trade is worth it. Every design choice is a trade-off; here the requirement dictated the choice.

Serving Reads: Linearizability vs. Performance

Reads are an extremely common part of Chubby's workload, and we want two things at once: linearizability and good performance. These pull against each other.

Key Term — Linearizability (for a read)

A linearizable read must reflect the last completed write. If a write finished before your read started, your read has to see its effect (it may also see in-progress writes, but never miss a completed one). It's the strongest, most intuitive consistency: the system behaves as if there were a single copy and operations happened one at a time.

The slow but obviously-correct way: put every read into the Paxos log, just like writes. Then reads and writes share one global order and linearizability is trivially satisfied. The problem? It is really slow — under a read-heavy workload the consensus log fills with reads that change nothing, paying full consensus cost just to enforce ordering.

Expand: why "serve reads at all replicas" doesn't quite work

A tempting optimization: store a (value, version) per key on every replica, and to read, ask a majority of replicas and take the highest version among them. For key foo you might get versions V1, V1, V2, V3, V2 — a majority gives you V3, so you return that.

The catch: there is a gap between acceptance and learning. Different replicas can legitimately have different versions at the same instant, and two reads hitting different majorities can disagree about the latest value. So "read a majority of replicas" does not reliably give linearizability. That is precisely why Chubby falls back to the leader-only approach below.

Chubby's actual choice — leader reads. Designate one replica as the leader. All replicas hold the database, but only the leader serves reads (and all writes). Why is that linearizable? Because the leader is the single replica guaranteed to have the latest committed state — there is no stale-majority gap to worry about. Clients find the leader by asking any replica, then talk to the leader until it dies or steps down.

1

A read needs no consensus — the leader already has the latest state, so it answers immediately. Fast.

2

A write still runs full consensus: the leader gets it accepted by a majority and appended to the log. Correct.

3

If the leader fails, the replicas run an election (using consensus) to pick a new one. During that election Chubby may be briefly unavailable — but that's fine: elections take seconds, and leases last much longer, so clients ride it out.

Caching With Invalidation

Routing every read through the single leader still concentrates load. So Chubby lets clients cache the data they read. But caching threatens linearizability: a cached copy might be stale, so a client could read an old value while the leader has a newer one.

The Risk — Stale Cache Breaks Linearizability

If a client reads from its own cache first, the cache may not reflect the latest state at the leader. Returning that stale value violates linearizability — the client misses a completed write. A cache is useless if it can lie to you about completed writes.

The fix is leader-driven invalidation. The leader tracks, for each data item, which clients hold a cached copy. When that item is modified, the leader sends invalidations to exactly those clients, telling them to drop the stale entry. And critically — the leader must wait to hear back from the clients (confirming they invalidated) before it considers the write done.

Why This Is Acceptable Despite the Sync Cost

Waiting for every caching client to acknowledge before completing a write adds a synchronization step to every write — expensive! Chubby gets away with it for one reason: its traffic is overwhelmingly read-heavy, and there is no strict latency requirement on writes. Reads must be fast; writes are allowed to be slower. The workload assumption (Part I!) is what makes the design valid.

Scaling the Real Bottleneck: Keep-Alives

Now a genuine surprise. What is the single most frequent request type in Chubby? Not writes. Not even reads. It is keep-alives — clients constantly refreshing their leases. From a real production trace: about 93% of all requests are keep-alives. Reads are a distant second; writes are rare.

Optimize What's Actually Frequent

It would be natural to pour optimization effort into reads or writes. But the trace says the dominant cost is keep-alives. The lesson: measure your workload, then optimize the thing that's actually frequent — not the thing that feels important. This is Part I's "discover the real assumptions" in action.

Chubby scales keep-alives with proxies. A proxy sits between many clients and the Chubby cell. Clients send their keep-alives to the proxy; the proxy batches them and forwards a single combined keep-alive on behalf of, say, 10 clients at once — turning 100 keep-alives into 10, or even 1. Think of it like a Nagle-style server: gather requests for a moment, then send them together and fan out the responses. If the proxy hears from 9 of 10 clients, it keeps-alive those 9.

The Trade-Off of Proxies

Proxies add a new potential failure point — if the proxy or the proxy↔client link fails, Chubby thinks a lock is alive while the client has actually lost it. The mitigation: run proxies on the same network as Chubby so they're no more failure-prone than a direct connection, and be a bit more conservative with timing to absorb the extra synchronization skew. Why not batch at the client instead? Because clients don't know about each other — only a proxy can see many clients and combine them.

Part V

ZooKeeper — Weaker Guarantees, Better Performance

ZooKeeper is the open-source cousin of Chubby — one of the most popular coordination services in the world. It shares Chubby's DNA: a small hierarchical file system, a service rather than a library. But it deliberately differs in two ways, and those differences are the whole point of this part.

Why Offer a Lower-Level API?

Chubby forces a specific abstraction on you: locks. ZooKeeper's bet is that this is too prescriptive. Instead of handing you locks, it hands you a lower-level primitive and lets you build whatever coordination pattern you need on top — locks, yes, but also configuration watches, process-group membership, double barriers (synchronize everyone before and after a computation), and more.

And there's a performance motive. Recall the most expensive thing Chubby does: on every write, it must run consensus and invalidate every client's cache, waiting to hear back from all of them. That blocking is slow and can cause arbitrary delays. ZooKeeper's escape hatch is to offer a weaker consistency guarantee that avoids this blocking entirely.

This Connects Back to Caching

The reason Chubby blocks on writes is the cache-invalidation handshake from Part IV — the leader must wait for every caching client to acknowledge. ZooKeeper sidesteps that by weakening consistency so it never needs the immediate, synchronous invalidation. The cost paid in Part IV is exactly the cost ZooKeeper is trying to avoid.

"Almost" Linearizable

ZooKeeper offers what the professor calls almost-linearizability, defined by two rules:

ZooKeeper's Consistency Guarantee

1. Writes are linearizable. All writes are strongly consistent and globally ordered — everyone agrees on the single order in which writes happened.
2. Per-client FIFO. All requests from a single client are processed in the order that client issued them (first-in, first-out). But requests from different clients have no ordering relationship beyond the global write order.

That second rule is what makes it weaker than full linearizability. Consider this execution (a sequence of operations on a register X):

set X = 1      // write, finishes first
set X = 2      // write, finishes second
get X  -> 1    // a read returns 1
get X  -> 2    // a read returns 2

Is this linearizable? No. Under linearizability you cannot reorder these events freely; get X → 1 would have to come before set X = 2 in real time, but it appears after it. A single-copy register can never return the old value 1 after a newer write 2 has completed. So this is not a valid linearizable execution.

But is it a valid ZooKeeper execution? Yes. Here's why: ZooKeeper only guarantees that the two writes are globally ordered (set 1 before set 2 — everyone agrees) and that each single client's requests are FIFO. If get X → 1 and get X → 2 come from different clients, there is no required ordering between them. ZooKeeper is free to let one client still observe the older value 1 for a while. That extra freedom is precisely what lets it avoid the immediate cache invalidation — it doesn't have to make every client see the newest write instantly.

ZooKeeper fixes a single global order for writes, but reads from different clients carry no mutual ordering — so one client may still see the old value 1. Allowed under ZooKeeper, forbidden under linearizability.
Consistency Models Are Defined Ad-Hoc

The professor flags a real pitfall: there are dozens of named consistency conditions, each defined in its own ad-hoc way ("your reads see your writes, but writes don't conflict with other writes, and there's a FIFO order on your reads…"). They are genuinely hard to compare. ZooKeeper's guarantee is "almost" linearizable precisely because it's defined by these patchwork rules rather than one clean property — don't expect a neat lattice of strength.

Building a Lock From Watches

So if ZooKeeper doesn't give you locks directly, how do you build one? The key realization — which ties back to Chubby's read story — is that you do not need full linearizability to implement a lock. You only need linearizable writes. ZooKeeper provides exactly that.

The mechanism is a watch: a client can register a watch on a file, and ZooKeeper will notify it when that file is updated (it tells you that it changed, not the new data). A lock built on watches works like this:

1

Try to acquire the lock by creating a file (a write operation).

2

If the file already exists, someone else holds the lock. So set a watch on that file instead of busy-waiting.

3

When the holder releases (the file changes/disappears), the watch fires a notification.

4

On notification, try to re-acquire — attempt the create again.

Why Linearizable Writes Are Enough

What if two clients try to create the same file at the exact same moment — who wins? Because writes are strongly consistent and globally ordered, ZooKeeper imposes some order on them: one create genuinely succeeds first, the other comes after and fails. There's no ambiguity about who holds the lock. The reads (watches) can be a little stale and it still works — that's the deep payoff: locks need write-linearizability, not read-linearizability.

The Herd Effect and the Sequencer

The simple watch-based lock has a nasty flaw. When a lock is released, every client watching it wakes up simultaneously and stampedes to re-acquire — potentially a hundred clients hammering the service at once. This is the herd effect.

The Herd Effect

If 100 clients all watch the same lock file, releasing it triggers 100 simultaneous notifications and 100 simultaneous re-acquire attempts — a thundering herd that can overload the service. Only one will win; the other 99 just generated load and go back to waiting.

The fix from the ZooKeeper paper is a sequencer: instead of everyone watching one file, build a small lock tree. Each waiting client creates a sequentially-numbered child node. Then:

1

Create a child node under the lock's parent. ZooKeeper assigns it a sequence number — you become, say, node #2.

2

Look at your siblings. If you have the lowest sequence number, you hold the lock — proceed.

3

If someone is ahead of you (a smaller number), watch only the node immediately before you — not the whole lock.

4

When your predecessor releases, only you are notified. You become the new lowest, acquire the lock, and the chain continues one client at a time.

The sequencer: waiting clients form a numbered queue. Each watches only the node ahead of it, so a release wakes exactly one client — no herd.

This turns the stampede into an orderly single-file queue, eliminating the herd effect. The professor noted this is essentially just a queue — but the point of the paper was to show that you can build such patterns yourself on top of ZooKeeper's minimal file-system primitive, including a better lock than the naive one.

Performance Tip — Pipelining

If a client must do 5,000 updates after grabbing a lock, doing them one-at-a-time (update → consensus → update → consensus…) at ~2ms each takes ~10 seconds. ZooKeeper lets the client pipeline them: fire all updates into a FIFO queue at once, and the service applies them in order. The per-client FIFO guarantee is exactly what makes this safe — and it slashes the wall-clock time dramatically.

Expand: ZooKeeper's implementation in one breath

All replicas serve reads. This is allowed precisely because ZooKeeper offers weaker-than-linearizable consistency — a slightly stale read from any replica is acceptable, so no need to funnel reads through one leader.

All writes go to the leader. The leader runs a consensus protocol called ZAB (ZooKeeper Atomic Broadcast) — a leader-based, Paxos-like protocol internal to ZooKeeper with its own proofs. There is also a garbage/keep-alive mechanism analogous to Chubby's sessions.

Does ZooKeeper block? Reads: no (served from any replica). Writes: yes, because consensus for strongly-consistent ordered writes is unavoidable — but writes do not have to wait to invalidate caches (the savings vs. Chubby). So ZooKeeper blocks less overall, with a different performance profile.

Putting It All Together

This lecture was the bridge between two halves of the course. Part I drew the line: foundations chase clean, provable protocols, while real systems chase the right contract for real assumptions. Everything after was that principle in motion.

Chubby and ZooKeeper both wrap the Paxos engine from earlier lectures inside a friendly file-system, lock-flavored service — because, as Part II argued, a service hides deployment and a lock matches developer intent better than raw consensus. Part III exposed the hard reality that clients crash, so locks became leases with keep-alives, made workable by conservative timing despite unsynchronized clocks, and protected against delayed writes by lock IDs. Part IV pulled back the curtain: a five-replica Paxos cell, the deliberate choice of coarse-grained locks, leader-only reads for linearizability, cache invalidation made affordable by a read-heavy workload, and proxies to tame the surprising 93%-keep-alive traffic. Part V then showed the road not taken: ZooKeeper weakens consistency to "almost-linearizable," serves reads from any replica, and hands you a lower-level primitive so you can build watch-based locks and a herd-free sequencer yourself.

The Big Idea — System Design Is Trade-Offs, All the Way Down

The single most important takeaway: you never get something that works perfectly in every dimension. Chubby trades the risk of post-crash stalls for the simplicity and resilience of coarse locks. ZooKeeper trades linearizability for read scalability and less blocking. Neither is "better" — each picked the trade-off that fit its assumptions. As you meet more systems this term, always ask: what did this system give up, and what did it buy?

Zooming out, the lecture's closing recap on consensus in practice distills to a handful of patterns you'll see again and again: real systems implement consensus as a service, not a library; the file-system abstraction is popular because it's familiar; production systems use a leader for performance; and they lean on real physical time (leases, keep-alives) with conservative approximations, plus optimizations like caching, proxying, and pipelining.
