Weak Consistency: CAP, Eventual Consistency & CRDTs

An interactive guide to Lecture 11 — why strong consistency is sometimes too expensive, what we trade for availability, and how to build systems that still behave correctly.

Part I

Why Would We Ever Weaken Consistency?

Everything in this course up to now has chased a single ideal: make a distributed system behave as if it were a single, reliable machine. We built primary–backup replication, Paxos, and ZooKeeper precisely so that no client could ever observe a stale or contradictory value. That ideal is called strong consistency. This lecture asks a heretical question: what if we deliberately give some of that up — and why on earth would we?

The short answer is cost. Strong consistency is slow, and in the real world of data centers spread across continents, "slow" can mean "unusable." To see why, we first need to be precise about what strong consistency gives us, and what it demands in return.

What Strong Consistency Buys — and Costs

Throughout the course we have met several flavors of strong consistency. It helps to name them before we trade them away.

Key Concept — Strong Consistency

Strong consistency means every client sees a single, agreed-upon, up-to-date view of the data, as if all operations happened one at a time in some global order. The strongest form is linearizability (every operation appears to take effect instantaneously at some point between its start and end). Weaker-but-still-"strong" forms include sequential consistency and causal consistency, which fix an order on operations even if it isn't exactly real time.

We saw linearizability inside primary–backup replication, Paxos, and Raft, and a near-linearizable form in ZooKeeper. The catch is that all of these guarantees have to be paid for, and the bill has three line items:

1

It needs consensus. If you want strong consistency and fault tolerance, replicas must agree on an order of updates — and agreement requires a consensus protocol like Paxos.

2

Consensus means blocking. A replica often cannot answer until it has heard from a majority of its peers. If those peers are slow or unreachable, the replica waits.

3

Order is expensive. Forcing every update through a single agreed sequence serializes work that could otherwise have run in parallel.

Add these up and the conclusion is blunt: strong consistency is very expensive. The rest of the lecture is about deciding when that price is worth paying — and what we can do when it isn't.

The Problem That Forces the Issue

Here is the concrete scenario the professor uses to set everything up. Picture two replicas and two clients. Each client fires off updates, and — because the network delivers messages at different speeds — those updates arrive at the two replicas in different orders.

Two clients, two replicas. R1 may see C1's update first; R2 may see C2's first. Which update should each replica commit first?

The professor asks: which update should the replicas commit first? To keep them consistent, they must agree — and agreeing is exactly the expensive consensus we just described. Hold onto this picture. In Part IV we will turn the question on its head and ask something far more productive: what if the order doesn't matter at all?

Where This Is Headed

This part is the "why." The CAP theorem (Part II) will prove that under network partitions you literally cannot keep strong consistency without sacrificing availability. PACELC (Part III) shows the tradeoff persists even without partitions. Those two results justify everything after them: eventual consistency, the Facebook study, and CRDTs.

Part II

The CAP Theorem

Before we can decide what to give up, we need to know whether we even have a choice. The CAP theorem, introduced by Eric Brewer in 2000, is the foundational result that tells us: no, you can't have it all. It is the distributed-systems analog of "you can't have your cake and eat it too" — but made precise.

Three Properties, Pick Two

Key Concept — The CAP Theorem

In a distributed system you cannot simultaneously guarantee all three of:

Consistency (C) — every read returns the correct, up-to-date result. This is a safety property ("nothing bad happens").

Availability (A) — every request gets a (non-error) response. This is a liveness property ("something good eventually happens").

Partition tolerance (P) — the system keeps working even when the network drops messages between groups of replicas (unreliability).

You may guarantee at most two of the three at the same time.

It's the Same Safety-vs-Liveness Story

CAP isn't a brand-new idea — it crystallizes the safety-versus-liveness tension we already met in the FLP impossibility result (consensus can't be both safe and guaranteed-to-terminate in an asynchronous network) and in primary–backup. CAP just focuses that tension specifically on consistency in an unreliable system. Seeing the recurring pattern is the whole point: distributed systems keep forcing the same fundamental trade.

Why is partition tolerance always in the picture? Because partitions are real. Imagine a hyperscaler with data centers across the world: for some stretch of time, two of them genuinely cannot exchange a single message. Worse, think of mobile and IoT devices — a phone going through a tunnel has intermittent connectivity, and such devices are far more failure-prone. Partitions are transient and shift over time, but in a real system you must assume they will happen.

The Proof Sketch — Two Indistinguishable Worlds

The argument is beautifully short. Let's walk it step by step.

1

Set the stage. Replicas R1, R2, …, Rn all implement a single register X. The network splits into two partitions: partition 1 holds only R1; partition 2 holds everyone else. Every message between R1 and the others is lost.

2

A client reads. A client asks R2 (in partition 2) to get X.

3

Two worlds exist. In one world, some client already told R1 to set X = v1. In another world, a client told R1 to set X = v2. R2 has heard about neither, because R1 is cut off.

4

The trap. R2 cannot distinguish these two worlds — no matter how long it waits. So it must either respond immediately with a possibly-wrong value (sacrificing consistency) or wait arbitrarily long for the partition to heal (sacrificing availability). There is no third option.

R2 is asked to read X, but R1 (which holds the latest write) is partitioned away. R2 cannot tell whether X was set to v1 or v2 — so it must choose: answer fast and maybe wrong (drop C), or wait forever (drop A).

CAP in Systems We've Already Seen

The most useful way to internalize CAP is to ask, for each system we've studied, which two did it pick?

SystemPicksWhat it gives up
PaxosC + PLiveness — it can't respond outside a majority partition
FLP (consensus)C + PTermination is not guaranteed in an async network
Primary–backup (split brain)C + PA partitioned client may get no response, but never a wrong one

Notice the pattern: the systems we've built so far almost all chose CP — they sacrifice liveness (availability) to keep consistency and tolerate partitions. The "split brain" example is the sharpest: the view service and backup advance to View 3 while the client and primary still believe they're in View 2. The partitioned primary refuses to violate correctness even though it may stall — that's CP in action.

Don't Drop Partition Tolerance

In practice, systems choose between CP (slow, consensus-based, sacrifices availability) and AP (drops consistency to always answer). What they almost never drop is P. Giving up partition tolerance means your system simply stops working whenever the network partitions — and partitions are common in the real world. A "CA" system is mostly a theoretical curiosity, not a real distributed system.

This leaves us with two crisp design rules the lecture states explicitly:

  • When can a replica answer a read/write without violating linearizability? Only if it is in a majority partition (this is exactly Paxos's rule).
  • How can a replica always serve clients? Only by dropping consistency — the AP choice we explore in Part IV.

Part III

PACELC — The Tradeoff That Survives Even Without Partitions

CAP has a subtle blind spot: it only talks about what happens during a partition. But partitions are the rare case — most of the time the network is fine. Does that mean strong consistency is free when there's no partition? No. PACELC, introduced by Daniel Abadi in 2012, extends CAP to capture the cost that persists in normal operation.

If Partition, then A vs C — Else, L vs C

Key Concept — PACELC

Read it as a two-branch rule:

If there is a Partition, choose between Availability and Consistency (this is just CAP).

Else (no partition), choose between Latency and Consistency.

The pronunciation: "PAC-ELC." The new insight lives entirely in the second branch.

Why split the world into "partition" and "no partition"? Because one describes the rare case of execution and the other describes the common case. This is a recurring principle in systems design: build for the common case to perform optimally, and make sure the rare case merely degrades gracefully rather than collapsing. Partitions happen — but they are not the common case. The common case is "no partition," and even there, strong consistency still costs you latency, because keeping replicas in lockstep means round-trips.

The Real Takeaway of PACELC

CAP makes it sound like the consistency tradeoff only bites during failures. PACELC corrects that: strong consistency means sacrificing latency even outside of partitions. You can have low latency or consistency, but not both — partition or no partition. That's why so many high-scale systems weaken consistency: they're not afraid of partitions so much as everyday round-trip latency.

The Four Kinds of System

Crossing the two branches gives four categories. The naming convention lists the partition choice (PA or PC) and the else-choice (EL or EC).

ClassPartition → / Else →Real systems
PA / ELAvailability / Latency — never blocks for consistencyEarly Dynamo, Cassandra, Riak
PC / ECConsistency / Consistency — always strongMySQL, Postgres, Bigtable
PA / ECAvailable during partition, but consistent otherwiseMongoDB
PC / ELConsistent during partition, fast otherwise (rare)(uncommon in practice)

The two extremes are the most common: PA/EL systems (like Cassandra) prioritize availability and low latency over consistency at all times, while PC/EC systems (like Postgres) are strongly consistent no matter what. The middle rows are the interesting hybrids that adapt their behavior to whether a partition is present.

The Big Idea

Consistency is not free — and not just during failures. CAP shows you must sacrifice consistency or availability to tolerate partitions; PACELC adds that even in the happy path, strong consistency costs you latency. Every distributed database you'll ever use sits somewhere on this map, and its position is a deliberate engineering choice — not an accident.

Part IV

Eventual Consistency & the Strong Eventual Refinement

We've established that to stay available under partitions, we must drop consistency. But what does "drop consistency" actually mean? It can't mean "the application returns garbage and we shrug" — that's useless. We need a disciplined, weaker guarantee. The key, the professor stresses, is order.

The Reframe: When Does Order Not Matter?

Back in Part I we asked "which update should the replicas commit first?" That question assumed order matters. The more productive question is: what if the order of updates doesn't matter at all? If two updates commute — produce the same result regardless of which goes first — then there's nothing to agree on, and no expensive consensus is needed.

A clean example: split the set of registers across replicas. Put register X on one replica and register Y on another. Now updates to X only ever land on one replica and updates to Y on the other, so across replicas there is never a conflicting global order to resolve. The updates are order-independent by construction.

From Eventual to Strong Eventual Consistency

Key Concept — Eventual Consistency

Eventual consistency: if no new updates arrive, all replicas will eventually converge to the same state. This is a liveness property — it promises something good eventually happens, and it can never be violated by a finite execution trace (you can always say "convergence just hasn't happened yet").

But eventual consistency alone is too weak to distinguish good systems from bad ones. Consider two executions: one where we cleanly split the register state (order-independent), and one with arbitrary conflicting updates. Both can eventually converge, so both satisfy eventual consistency — yet they clearly behave differently. What separates them?

The distinguishing property is about finite traces: replicas that have delivered the same set of updates must have equivalent state. That's a safety property — it can be violated at a finite point. The lecture gives it a name.

Key Concept — Strong Eventual Consistency (SEC)

SEC = Strong Convergence (safety) + Eventual Consistency (liveness).

Strong convergence: any two replicas that have delivered the same set of updates have equivalent state — immediately, not just eventually.

Eventual consistency: absent new updates, all replicas eventually deliver the same set of updates and converge.

Why Industry Cares

Over the last ~15 years SEC has been heavily researched, and many industry systems adopt it instead of strong consistency — especially in the geo-replicated setting, where partitions are frequent and cross-data-center latency is brutally expensive. SEC is the disciplined version of "AP" that Part II said we'd need. CRDTs (Part VI) are the tool that makes SEC implementable.

A Concrete Hazard: The S3 Photo App

SEC sounds great, but it is heavily application-dependent: the application must be able to tolerate disorder. The lecture screens candidates:

ApplicationTolerates disorder?Why
Bank (transfer + interest)NoReordering could let you spend a balance you don't actually have
Social network (post + get-friends)YesSeeing a friend before their posts is harmless

Now make it concrete. You build a photo-sharing service on AWS using an eventually consistent Amazon S3 (a key–value store with just get and set). You store three kinds of data:

// three key-value mappings in S3
photoURL   -> image binary
albumName  -> [photoURL, photoURL, ...]
userName   -> { numPhotos, [albumName, ...] }

Because the store is only eventually consistent, your application must defend against two classes of bug:

Hazard 1 — Lack of Referential Integrity

A user's stored numPhotos may not equal the actual photo count across their albums. If you update the album then the user record, and a reader observes them in between (or out of order), they'll temporarily see an inconsistent total. The two pieces of data reference each other but aren't updated atomically.

Hazard 2 — Updates Appear Lost

Suppose two clients concurrently do "read album, append a photo, write album." Client A reads [photo1], appends photo2. Client B also reads [photo1], appends photo2′. Both write back — and one append silently overwrites the other. The album ends up with only photo1 plus one of the two new photos. An update vanished.

So why is S3 wildly successful despite this? Two reasons the lecture highlights:

1

Read-after-write for the initial put. The first time you write a key, you get strong consistency for that data forever after — everyone will read it.

2

Write-once workloads. Most S3 data is written once and then read many times, almost never edited. For "write once, read often," eventual consistency's hazards rarely fire, so the cheap, available store is exactly what you want.

Part V

Checking Correctness — and What Facebook Found

We've argued that eventual consistency is cheaper but riskier. A natural question follows: in practice, how often does an eventually-consistent system actually return a wrong (non-linearizable) result? Facebook (now Meta) answered this empirically in a 2015 SOSP paper studying TAO, their eventually-consistent social-graph database (nodes are people/pages/groups; edges are "friend of," "authored by," "tagged," etc.). But to understand how they checked it, we need a side quest into system correctness in general.

Four Ways to Check a System Is Correct

The oldest method is simply: run the system on some inputs and inspect the outputs — like tasting a bit of everything in a chemistry lab and going "seems fine." We've come a long way since. The four methods below form a spectrum from automatic & black-box & weak-guarantee to manual & white-box & strong-guarantee.

MethodHow it worksProsCons
TestingDefine checks, generate inputs/configs, run, see if checks passLightweight, black-box, fully automaticLimited guarantees; inputs are synthetic, not production
Monitoring (runtime verification)A monitor reads logs/traces of the running system in production and checks a propertyBlack-box, automatic, uses real production dataLimited guarantees; production overhead
Model checkingModel the system as a state machine; exhaustively explore transitions vs. propertiesReal (systematic, exhaustive) guarantees; automaticWhite-box; expensive; guarantees are finite (bounded)
Deductive verificationBuild a model + properties, construct a manual proof the model satisfies themVery strong guaranteesHeavy manual effort; deeply white-box
The Spectrum Has a Shape

As you move from testing toward deductive verification, the methods become more manual, more white-box, and more expensive — but the guarantees get stronger. Testing says "every test I ran passed." Model checking says "no reachable state in 100,000 rounds violates the property." Deductive verification says "no execution ever violates it." You pay for certainty with effort.

Real tools sit along this line: TLA+ and P for modeling/checking distributed systems, and Dafny, Coq, and Lean for (semi-)deductive verification. The professor notes that LLMs are lowering the bar for deductive verification, so expect to see more of it in distributed systems going forward.

How Facebook Checked TAO: Monitoring

Which method did Facebook pick? Monitoring — because it runs on real-world executions, not synthetic inputs. The mechanics:

1

For each processed request, log its start time, end time, arguments, and result.

2

Knowing start/end times tells you which operations overlap in real time and which don't — and therefore which you're allowed to reorder.

3

Search for a linearization: a sequential ordering of operations that (a) respects non-overlapping real-time order and (b) explains every observed result. If one exists, the execution was linearizable.

The cost of logging everything for 12 days would be crushing for both storage and performance. So they sampled — roughly 1 in a million events — and still counted 2.7 billion requests (≈900M about vertices, ≈1.8B about edges; mostly reads).

The Catch: You Can't Trust the Clocks

Footgun — Clock Synchronization

The start/end timestamps come from different machines, and it is known to be impossible to perfectly synchronize physical clocks across machines. So when the monitor compares "did op A finish before op B started?", it might be comparing two skewed clocks and reach the wrong conclusion.

Facebook's fix: they measured the 99.9th-percentile clock skew across replicas at 35 ms and used that as a near-worst-case bound. They then over-approximated by treating comparisons as uncertain within ±35 ms on each side. This produces a careful asymmetry:

1

Lower bound (expand by 35 ms). Stretch each request's interval outward by 35 ms. This creates more overlaps, making it easier to find a linearization — so any violation that survives is certainly real. (They might miss some real violations, but every violation they report is true.)

2

Upper bound (shrink by 35 ms). Squeeze intervals inward, as if all clocks were skewed the opposite way. This removes overlaps and asks "what is the worst possible violation count if clocks were maximally adversarial?"

One more subtlety mattered for the results: only objects with both reads and writes can possibly exhibit a violation. An object that is only written but never read can't reveal a stale read — there's no read to observe it. (In the data, ~75% of objects had no writes, and ~30% had no reads, so a large fraction simply couldn't show a violation.)

The Big Idea

The punchline: even though TAO is eventually consistent, only 0.0004% of reads violated linearizability (lower bound), and even the pessimistic upper bound — assuming every clock is maximally skewed the wrong way every time — was just 0.003%. An eventually-consistent system is, in practice, almost always strongly consistent. Weak consistency is a worst-case label, not an everyday experience.

Is 0.003% "good enough"? It depends on the application — the same lesson as SEC. For a social network, perfectly fine (we never even needed linearizability). For a bank, unacceptable — a single wrongly-allowed transaction is one too many.

Part VI

CRDTs — Making Strong Eventual Consistency Practical

We now know what we want (strong eventual consistency) and that it's worth wanting (the Facebook study shows it's usually as good as strong consistency). The last piece is how to build it. The answer is a family of data structures called CRDTs — but first we have to see why strong convergence alone isn't enough.

Why Strong Convergence Isn't the Whole Story

Recall strong convergence: replicas with the same set of updates have equivalent state. The gap is that it says nothing about what that state should be. A degenerate "solution" could order all updates by, say, the hash of their contents, and apply them in hash order. That technically converges — every replica computes the same hash order — but the resulting value is meaningless. We need genuine conflict resolution, not just any deterministic agreement.

Two example data types make the distinction vivid.

A counter: operations that commute

A counter supports increment(x), decrement(x), and get. (From here on we care only about writes and omit reads.) These operations commute fully: incrementing then decrementing yields the same value as decrementing then incrementing. So the state can simply be the integer, and each replica just applies operations as they arrive. Same set of operations ⟹ same value — strong convergence for free.

A register: operations that don't commute

A register supports set(v) and get. Now set(1) and set(2) do not commute — the final value depends on order. What does "converge" even mean? One clean answer: a multi-value register whose state is the set of all concurrent writes. Both set(1) and set(2) are stored as {1, 2}. Every replica that saw both writes holds the same set, so strong convergence holds — and the client applies a custom resolution policy (e.g. "largest write wins") to pick a final value.

What a CRDT Is

Key Concept — Conflict-Free Replicated Data Type (CRDT)

A CRDT is a data type engineered so that concurrent updates always merge into a consistent state without coordination — generalizing the multi-value register into a reusable framework that provides strong eventual consistency. You can use off-the-shelf CRDTs (registers, counters, sets, lists) or design your own following CRDT principles. They're popular at Apple, Facebook, SoundCloud, and beyond; the original motivating use case was collaborative text editing (a document many people type into at once).

CRDTs come in two equivalent implementation styles — same behavior, different ergonomics:

StyleHow replicas syncThe one rule you must satisfy
Operation-basedBroadcast every update to all replicas; each applies themUpdates must commute
State-basedPeriodically broadcast your whole state S; replicas merge statesThe merge function must combine two states correctly

For a state-based CRDT, merge runs every time a replica receives another replica's state. What's the merge for the multi-value register? Set union. Simple. The counter is where it gets subtle.

The Increment-Only Counter, Done Right

Naïve Merge = Wrong Count

For a state-based counter, you might guess merge = addition. It doesn't work — it double counts. If replica 1 holds 1 and merges into replica 2 (now 1), then replica 1 merges again, replica 2 jumps to 3. Re-merging the same state keeps re-adding replica 1's contribution. Addition is not idempotent, so repeated merges inflate the count.

The fix borrows the intuition from vector clocks: instead of one shared integer, keep one counter per replica.

1

State: a vector of counters, one slot per replica. Replica i only ever increments its own slot.

2

get: return the sum of all slots.

3

merge: take the element-wise max of the two vectors.

Increment-only counter as a per-replica vector. Merge takes the max of each slot; get() sums the slots. Because max is idempotent, re-merging never double counts — the bug that plagued naïve addition.

Why does max fix double-counting? Each slot records the true, monotonically growing count contributed by one replica. Merging by max just adopts the larger known value per replica — re-merging the same state is a no-op, so nothing is ever counted twice. (The full increment-and-decrement counter is more involved and the lecture points to a blog post rather than covering it; the increment-only version is enough to grasp the principle.)

The Big Idea

CRDTs deliver strong eventual consistency without consensus by demanding one of two algebraic properties: operations that commute (op-based) or a merge that is commutative, associative, and idempotent (state-based, like element-wise max). Get the algebra right, and replicas converge automatically — no coordination, no blocking, no Paxos. That is precisely the cheap-but-correct AP system Part II told us we'd need.

Putting It All Together

This lecture is a single argument, told in six moves. It starts from a cost and ends with a cure.

We began (Part I) by naming the bill for strong consistency: consensus, blocking, and forced ordering make it expensive. The CAP theorem (Part II) then proved this isn't just an engineering inconvenience but a fundamental limit — under a partition, two replicas become genuinely indistinguishable, so you must drop either consistency or availability. Crucially, you essentially never drop partition tolerance, because partitions are real. PACELC (Part III) sharpened the knife: even with no partition, strong consistency still costs latency, which is why high-scale, geo-replicated systems so often weaken it.

Having justified weakening consistency, we made it disciplined. Eventual consistency (Part IV) is the liveness promise that replicas converge; strong convergence adds the safety promise that the same updates yield the same state; together they form strong eventual consistency. But SEC is application-dependent — a bank can't tolerate disorder, a social feed can — and the S3 photo app showed the real bugs (lost updates, broken referential integrity) you must defend against.

Then we got empirical. The detour into correctness checking (Part V) — testing, monitoring, model checking, deductive verification — gave us the vocabulary to read Facebook's TAO study, which used monitoring to discover that an eventually-consistent system violates linearizability only ~0.0004% of the time. Weak consistency is a worst-case label, not an everyday reality. Finally, CRDTs (Part VI) gave us the construction: pick operations that commute or a merge that's idempotent (per-replica max for counters, union for registers), and replicas converge with no consensus at all — exactly the cheap, available, correct AP system CAP told us to build.

The throughline connecting all six parts is order. Strong consistency is expensive precisely because it forces a global order; weak consistency becomes safe precisely when we engineer updates whose order doesn't matter.

What's Next

The next lecture turns to scalability and the various solutions for it — how to grow a system to handle more load, which is the natural counterpart to today's consistency tradeoffs. Keep one more thread in mind too: the professor flagged a recent reversal. As networks get faster and more reliable, the critical path shrinks to a single machine/data center, and strong consistency is coming back into favor (S3 itself is now strongly consistent) — because developers found reasoning about inconsistency simply too error-prone. The pendulum between strong and weak consistency is still swinging; this lecture gives you the framework to understand why it moves.
