Google Spanner — Transactions Across the Planet

How a database that spans the globe pulls off the seemingly impossible: strictly-ordered, distributed transactions made efficient by knowing exactly how wrong its clocks are.

Part I

The Spanner Problem

Every system we have studied so far in this course made a trade-off. Earlier we saw Azure Storage, which could give you transactions — but only within a single partition. Dynamo gave us scale and availability but only eventual consistency. The recurring lesson has been that you cannot have everything: pick scale, pick strong guarantees, pick global reach — but not all three at once.

Today's system, Google Spanner, is interesting precisely because it refuses that trade-off. It is a database that is replicated across the entire planet, scales to enormous size, and offers full transactions that touch data in different parts of the world. To a student who has internalized the usual trade-offs, the natural reaction — which a student in lecture voiced directly — is "isn't that impossible?" Answering that is the whole point of this lecture.

What Is Spanner?

Spanner is Google's geo-distributed database. "Geo-distributed" means its data lives in data centers scattered around the globe — different cities, different continents — so that the data is close to users everywhere and survives even the total loss of a data center. It began as an internal system to power Google's advertising backend, and is now also sold as a cloud product to outside customers.

The single most important thing Spanner offers, the thing that makes the paper famous, is geo-distributed transactions: a transaction can read and write data that is spread across shards living in different regions of the world, and the database still behaves as if all transactions happened one at a time in a sensible order.

Key Concept — A Transaction

A transaction is a group of reads and writes that the database must treat as a single, indivisible unit: either all of its effects happen, or none of them do, and no other transaction can observe a half-finished state. Spanner's claim to fame is supporting transactions that span shards and continents — not just data sitting on one machine.

The Goals (and the Paradox)

As always, start with what the system is trying to achieve. Spanner sets out to be all of the following at once:

GoalWhat it means
ScalableSharded across many machines so it can grow without limit.
Multi-versionEach key stores many versions, tagged by time — just like Dynamo kept multiple versions of a value. This is what makes lock-free reads possible later.
Globally distributedReplicas live in different data centers and regions across the world.
Synchronously replicatedReplication happens on the critical path — a write is not "done" until replicas agree.
Distributed transactionsA single transaction can atomically touch many shards in many regions.

Here is the tension. To be scalable, Spanner shards its data — it chops the key space into pieces and spreads them across machines. (You will also see these pieces called tablets or partitions; all three words mean the same thing — a subset of the data living together.) Each shard is independently replicated with Paxos, the consensus protocol we studied that lets a group of replicas agree on a sequence of operations even when some of them fail.

The Paradox

Transactions that cross regions require multiple rounds of communication across the world, and the speed of light makes that slow — tens to hundreds of milliseconds per round trip. Worse, to order transactions correctly across regions you seemingly need synchronized clocks, and a recurring theme of this entire course has been that physical clock synchronization is hard and you generally can't trust clocks at all. So how can Spanner be fast and correctly ordered across the globe?

The Key Insight: Bound the Uncertainty

Throughout the course we have taken a pessimistic stance toward physical clocks: "we know nothing — two machines' clocks could disagree by an arbitrary, unknowable amount." Spanner makes one small but powerful change to that stance.

The Big Idea

Instead of saying "I know nothing about how out-of-sync the clocks are," Spanner says "I know exactly one thing: a conservative bound on how out-of-sync they could possibly be." Knowing that bound — never the exact time, just a guaranteed-correct error range — is enough to order transactions across the planet. When clocks are well-synced (the common case), the bound is tiny and Spanner is fast; when the bound grows, Spanner simply slows down and waits the uncertainty out, but stays correct.

This is the heart of everything that follows. In practice Google measured that the skew between their clocks is almost always below 10 milliseconds, which is small enough for Spanner to perform very well. The mechanism that exposes this bound to the rest of the system is an API called TrueTime — our subject for Part II.

Where We're Headed

The rest of the lecture builds in layers: TrueTime (Part II) gives us a trustworthy notion of "now." We use it to keep Paxos leaders' leases disjoint (Part III), which lets us assign monotonic timestamps across leaders (Part IV), which gives us strict serializability, and finally lock-free reads (Part V). Each piece depends on the one before it — keep that chain in mind.

Part II

TrueTime — Honest Clocks

We just claimed Spanner relies on a known bound on clock uncertainty. TrueTime is the concrete API that delivers that bound to the rest of the system. Before we can use it, we need to understand precisely what it tells us — and, just as importantly, what it does not.

TT.now() — Time as an Interval

An ordinary clock call returns a single number: "it is 12:00:00.000." TrueTime refuses to lie like that. Instead, the core method TT.now() returns an interval with two endpoints, earliest and latest:

struct TTinterval { earliest; latest; }

// The true physical time at this instant is guaranteed
// to lie somewhere inside [earliest, latest].
TTinterval now()

The promise is simple and absolute: the real physical time, right now, is somewhere inside that interval. It can never be earlier than earliest, and never later than latest. The width of the interval is the clock uncertainty — a narrow interval means the clocks are well-synced, a wide interval means we are unsure.

Key Concept — TT.now()

TT.now() returns [earliest, latest], a conservative window guaranteed to contain the true time. We never learn the exact instant; we only learn a range we can completely trust. Correctness depends on this interval being conservative — if TrueTime ever reported an interval that did not actually contain the real time, the whole protocol breaks.

Where does the bound actually come from?

Google implements TrueTime with GPS receivers and atomic clocks in each data center, plus careful knowledge of the deployment — even the lengths of the cables, which determine signal propagation delays. From the GPS error bounds and these physical measurements, they derive a guaranteed-conservative uncertainty interval. The professor was candid that the exact engineering is fiddly; for this course we simply assume TrueTime's interval is correct and conservative, and reason from there.

One student asked how conservative — "is it 99.something percent?" The paper doesn't state the exact confidence, but it is assumed very high; it cannot be a literal 100% guarantee, but it is engineered to be effectively certain.

after() and before() — Asking About a Specific Time

Two helper methods are built on top of TT.now(). Both take a timestamp t you care about and answer a yes/no question — but conservatively, so they only say "yes" when they are certain.

// Has time t DEFINITELY already passed?
after(t)  = true iff TT.now().earliest > t

// Has time t DEFINITELY not yet arrived?
before(t) = true iff TT.now().latest < t

The logic is worth dwelling on. after(t) says "t has passed" only when even the earliest possible real time is already past t — so there is no doubt. before(t) says "t hasn't arrived" only when even the latest possible real time is still before t. If t falls inside the current interval, both answer false: we genuinely don't know yet.

The board example. t1 is left of the whole interval, so it has definitely passed. t2 is right of it, so it definitely hasn't arrived. t3 falls inside, so we cannot tell — both after and before return false.
Why before() Is Almost Useless — and after() Is the Workhorse

A student noticed something sharp: before(t) is only true at the instant you call it. As soon as a little time passes, the latest endpoint marches forward and the answer can flip. So the protocol almost never makes a decision based on before(). Instead it repeatedly calls after() and waits until after(t) becomes true — because once a time has definitely passed, it stays passed. This "wait until after() is true" pattern, called commit-wait, is the single most important move in the entire protocol; you will see it again in Parts III, IV, and V.

The One Thing We Know Across Machines

Now the payoff. Recall the classic two-process picture from earlier in the course: process P1 takes a timestamp, then sends a message; process P2 takes a timestamp the moment it receives the message.

Without TrueTime, comparing P1's timestamp and P2's timestamp tells us nothing. They are readings from two different physical clocks that may drift arbitrarily — P2's reading could be smaller, equal, or larger than P1's. The only thing we know is the obvious causal fact that the receive happened after the send in real time; the numbers tell us nothing.

With TrueTime, each process records not a number but a TrueTime interval — call them tt1 = [e1, L1] and tt2 = [e2, L2]. Now we can state exactly one solid relationship between their four endpoints:

The TrueTime Causality Guarantee

If event 1 happened before event 2 in real time, then tt1.earliest < tt2.latest. That is the only relationship we can rely on — and it is enough to recover causality from physical timestamps.

Even when tt2's interval is huge and overlaps tt1, the guarantee e1 < L2 still holds. We know nothing else — not e1 vs e2, not L1 vs L2. If real event 1 truly came first, TrueTime's honesty makes it impossible for tt2 to sit entirely before tt1.

Why must this hold? If event 2 really happened after event 1 in physical time, and both intervals are honest (each truly contains its real moment), then the real time of event 2 is past the real time of event 1. The latest endpoint L2 sits at or beyond event 2's real time, which is beyond event 1's real time, which is at or beyond e1. So e1 < L2 is forced. If TrueTime ever let tt2 sit entirely before tt1 in this situation, one of the two intervals would have to be lying — violating the conservatism promise.

Part III

Architecture & Lease Disjointness

We now have an honest clock. But to use it for transactions we first need the lay of the land: how Spanner's data and replicas are organized, and one subtle correctness problem about leaders that TrueTime turns out to solve elegantly.

Shards, Paxos, and Leaders

Spanner's data type is not the usual key→value. It is (key, timestamp) → value. The same key can have many entries, one per timestamp — that is exactly what "multi-version" means, and it is why a read can ask for the value "as of" some past moment. The data is chopped into tablets (= partitions = shards), each a subset of these (key, timestamp)→value entries. Many tablets can live on one server, and underneath sits a distributed file system we will ignore today.

Take one vertical slice of the stack — a single shard:

1

The shard is replicated across zones (regions), so it survives the loss of an entire data center. The replicas run Paxos together to agree on the shard's sequence of operations.

2

One replica is elected leader via Paxos, using long-lived leases (time-bounded "I am the leader" grants). All writes funnel through the leader, which proposes them to the others.

3

Reads may be served by any up-to-date replica, not just the leader — a key to performance and locality. (How a replica decides it is "up-to-date" is the subject of Part V.)

Cross-Shard Transactions: Two-Phase Commit

A single shard's Paxos log handles transactions that stay inside that shard. But Spanner's headline feature is transactions that span many shards across many zones. For those, Spanner layers two-phase commit (2PC) on top of the per-shard Paxos groups.

Concretely: a transaction touching several shards involves the leader of each shard. One of those leaders is designated the coordinator of the transaction; the rest are participants. Each leader runs a transaction manager data structure to play its part in the 2PC.

Key Concept — The Locking Rule

Any operation that is a transaction — touching multiple partitions or bundling several sub-operations — must acquire locks on all the partitions it touches before writing. Operations that are not transactions bypass the lock table entirely. Locks are what serialize conflicting transactions on the same data.

Why store the transaction manager's state in Paxos?

Here is a subtle but vital design choice. The transaction manager's state — "we are in the middle of committing transaction X" — is itself written into Paxos, i.e. replicated, not just kept in the leader's memory. The professor asked the class why. The answer:

Surviving a Mid-Transaction Crash

Imagine the coordinator (or any leader) crashes partway through the two-phase commit. If its transaction state lived only in memory, the replica promoted to replace it would have no idea where the transaction was — was it committed? aborted? still preparing? By writing every intermediate transaction step into Paxos, the state is replicated, so the new leader can resume the transaction exactly where it stopped. A transaction is an effectful, durable process, so its progress must be made durable just like any other write.

A student raised the obvious cost: every transactional write now pays for a Paxos round across all the data centers holding replicas of every shard it touches. That is expensive, and Spanner doesn't pretend otherwise. The professor's response: the genuinely clever part of Spanner is using physical time to eliminate other coordination overhead. The Paxos round-trip itself is the irreducible price of agreement — at some point, machines must communicate to be sure everyone knows the same thing.

The Disjointness Invariant

Now the problem TrueTime solves here. Leadership is granted by a lease — a window of time during which a replica believes it is the leader. For correctness, Spanner requires:

Leases Must Be Disjoint

Each leader's lease interval must be disjoint from every past and future leader's lease — they may never overlap. If two replicas both believed they were leader at the same moment, they could each accept conflicting writes and assign conflicting timestamps, corrupting the system. (This is the classic split-brain danger.)

Consecutive leases must not overlap. The whole challenge: different replicas, in different regions, must coordinate this without a god's-eye clock telling them the real time.

Enforcing Disjointness with TrueTime + Quorums

The class brainstormed this, and it combines two ideas we already have. The clever result is that Spanner enforces disjointness without running Paxos for the lease itself — it borrows just one ingredient from Paxos: the majority quorum.

Idea 1 — The single-vote rule (each replica self-polices via TrueTime)

Every replica obeys a personal rule about granting lease votes:

The Single-Vote Rule

If a replica grants a lease vote at time t, it will not grant another vote until TT.after(t.latest + lease_length) is true — i.e. until it is certain the previous lease it voted for has fully expired. By waiting for the conservative latest endpoint (plus the lease length) to definitely pass, each replica single-handedly guarantees it never double-votes into overlapping leases.

Idea 2 — Quorum overlap (the magic that ties replicas together)

But disjointness must hold across all replicas, not just one. Here the quorum does the work. A leader needs lease votes from a majority of replicas. Between any two consecutive leaders' majorities, at least one replica is common to both — because two majorities of the same set must overlap.

The Insight

That one shared replica — whichever it happens to be — forces disjointness through its own single-vote rule. It voted for the old leader, so by its personal rule it refuses to vote for the new leader until the old lease has definitely expired. No replica needs to know it is the pivotal one; the math of quorum overlap guarantees that some replica always plays this role.

Let's trace it. Suppose R1 becomes leader i, R3 becomes leader i+1, and R2 is in both quorums:

1

R1 requests a lease, sampling TrueTime as T1. A majority (including R2) votes yes. R1 holds its lease from T1.earliest for the lease length — using earliest keeps it conservative, so R1 never overstays.

2

Later, R3 wants to become leader and asks for votes. R2, having voted for R1, applies its single-vote rule: it will not vote for R3 until after(T2.latest + lease_length) — where T2 was R2's reading when it voted for R1.

3

So R3's lease can only begin (T3.earliest) after that gap. The arithmetic guarantees R1's lease ends before R3's lease begins. Disjoint.

A Note on the Cost

A student asked: what if network delay means R1 only hears its acceptances near the end of the window? Then R1's lease is simply shorter — measured from when it sent its request, not when replies arrived. Congestion shrinks leases but never breaks correctness. Conservatism always errs toward safety, paying in performance, never in correctness. This same "err conservative, lose a little speed" tradeoff is the through-line of the whole lecture.

Part IV

Strict Serializability & the Commit Protocol

Disjoint leases were not the goal — they are a tool. The goal is strict serializability for read-write transactions, and we will now see how disjointness makes it achievable. First, what exactly are we promising?

Serializability, Then Strict Serializability

Serializability means the database may interleave transactions however it likes, as long as the result is equivalent to running them one at a time in some order — as if single-threaded. Each transaction's sub-operations happen atomically with respect to others.

Consider two transactions doing a get-then-set increment. An interleaving like get→2, set→3, get→1, set→2 is still serializable, because we can reorder it to be equivalent to "transaction 2, then transaction 1" run back-to-back. Serializability lets us reorder freely, as long as atomicity is preserved.

Key Concept — Strict Serializability

Strict serializability adds one requirement to serializability: if transaction T1 completes before T2 starts (in real, wall-clock time), then the chosen serial order must also place T1 before T2. This is the real-time ordering guarantee. You can still freely reorder transactions whose lifetimes overlap; you may only not reorder ones separated in real time. Spanner also calls this external consistency or strict visibility.

A Familiar Analogy

Strict serializability is to serializability exactly what linearizability is to sequential consistency — both add the real-time ordering requirement on top of "behaves like some sequential order." If you understood linearizability earlier in the course, you already understand the shape of this guarantee; it's the same idea lifted from single objects to whole multi-key transactions.

Spanner lets a developer choose per transaction between full strict serializability (with locks, stronger but slower) and snapshot isolation (lock-free reads at a chosen timestamp, weaker but faster — our Part V). The choice is a knob trading guarantees against efficiency.

Monotonic Timestamps as the Serial Order

How does Spanner realize a serial order? It stamps every transaction with a monotonically increasing commit timestamp. Because the timestamps strictly increase and each transaction gets a distinct one, the order of timestamps is the serialization order — and Spanner engineers it so that order also respects real time.

For a single leader this is trivial: it just hands out locally increasing numbers. The hard part is making timestamps monotonic across leaders, where a dying leader may never have talked to its successor. This is where Part III pays off:

Disjointness → Monotonicity

Each leader assigns timestamps only inside its own lease interval. Because leases are disjoint and ordered in time, the intervals don't overlap — so a later leader's timestamps are automatically larger than any earlier leader's. Within its own interval each leader trivially keeps its numbers increasing. The disjointness from Part III is precisely what makes timestamps monotonic globally without leaders ever coordinating directly.

The Commit Protocol, Step by Step

Now the full read-write transaction across shards. Writes are buffered at the client until commit — the client does not push individual writes as it goes. Instead it reads everything it needs, computes all its writes, and then sends them together. (One student misheard "buffered" as batching for efficiency; the professor clarified it means the client holds all the writes and ships them in one commit message, not that it delays to batch many transactions.)

1

Reads. The client issues reads to the leader of each shard it needs. Each leader acquires read locks and returns the most recent data. (Locks have timeouts and need client keep-alives, so a crashed client doesn't hold them forever — that's how Spanner avoids stuck locks.)

2

Commit request. When all reads are done, the client sends one message to every participating leader containing all its writes, and designates one leader as the coordinator.

3

Prepare (non-coordinator leaders). Each participant acquires write locks, picks a prepare timestamp T larger than any timestamp it has used before, logs it via Paxos (so it survives a crash), and sends a prepared message to the coordinator.

4

Choose commit timestamp (coordinator). The coordinator acquires its write locks and picks a commit timestamp S that is greater than all three of: (a) every participant's prepared T; (b) its own TT.now().latest at the moment it received the commit message; (c) every timestamp it has previously assigned (for monotonicity).

5

Log & commit-wait. The coordinator logs S via Paxos (or aborts on timeout). Then it waits until TT.after(S) is true — until S is definitely in the past everywhere.

6

Apply. Only after the wait does the coordinator tell the participants and the client to commit, and the writes actually take effect, stamped with S.

Why does the prepare phase exist at all? Because each participant leader might also be coordinating other transactions. Its prepared T announces "this is a timestamp I consider safe for this transaction — it's after everything I'm already in the middle of." The coordinator then takes the max over all those, plus its own clock, guaranteeing the final S sits after every concurrent transaction's claim.

Why We Wait Before Writing — Commit-Wait

Step 5's wait is the crux, so let's see what breaks without it. Strict serializability demands: if T1 finishes before T2 starts in real time, then commit_ts(T2) > commit_ts(T1). Picking S larger than TT.now().latest guarantees S is past real-time-now. But that's not enough on its own.

The Bug Commit-Wait Prevents

If we applied the write the instant we chose S, the write could become visible before real time actually reached S. Then a later transaction that started after we "finished" might still get a timestamp below S — or another transaction supposed to be ordered after us could read state inconsistent with our timestamp. By waiting until TT.after(S) before making the write visible, Spanner guarantees that by the time anyone can see the effect, real time has genuinely passed S — so any later transaction is forced to get a strictly larger timestamp. Timestamp order then truly matches real-time order.

This Is Where the Clock Bound Buys Speed

The length of the commit-wait is exactly the TrueTime uncertainty — you wait out the interval width. When clocks are tightly synced (sub-10ms skew, the common case), the wait is tiny and throughput is high. When uncertainty spikes, the wait grows and Spanner slows — but never becomes incorrect. That is the entire bargain: trade a small, bounded wait for planet-scale strict serializability without endless coordination rounds.

Part V

Lock-Free Snapshot Reads

We have strict serializability for read-write transactions — but it costs locks and coordination. Many workloads are read-heavy and don't need that. Spanner's other guarantee, snapshot isolation, lets any replica serve reads with no locks at all, by reading a consistent snapshot of the database at a chosen timestamp. This is where the multi-version design from Part I finally pays off.

Reading at a Timestamp

Because every value is stored as (key, timestamp) → value, "the database as of time T" is well-defined: for each key, take the newest version with timestamp ≤ T. That frozen view is a snapshot, and reading it requires no locks — you're reading immutable past versions, so no concurrent writer can interfere.

The client specifies a read timestamp T meaning "give me a snapshot no older than T." Any replica can answer — but it must first make sure it is safe to do so.

Why a Replica Can't Just Answer Immediately

A replica might still be processing transactions that will write values at or before T but haven't landed yet. If it answered now, it could return a snapshot that later turns out to be wrong — missing a write that belongs before T. The replica must wait until it is certain no future write can change the answer at timestamp T.

Computing T-safe

Each replica maintains a value T-safe: the largest timestamp at which it can correctly serve a snapshot read right now. It serves a read for T only once T-safe ≥ T; otherwise it waits for T-safe to climb. T-safe is the minimum of two components, because two different kinds of pending work could still affect old timestamps:

Component 1 — T-paxos (committed writes)

T-paxos is the timestamp of the highest Paxos write already applied at this replica. It increases monotonically because writes are applied in timestamp order. The payoff of strict serializability: if write at timestamp X has been applied, then every write with a smaller timestamp has also been applied — so the replica's view at any time ≤ T-paxos is complete and final.

Component 2 — T-TM (in-flight prepared transactions)

Some transactions may be prepared but not yet committed. From the commit protocol we know a prepared transaction's final timestamp will be at least its prepare timestamp — never below it. So define T-TM = (min over all prepared transactions of their prepare timestamps) − 1, or ∞ if none are prepared. Below T-TM, no pending transaction can still insert a write.

// A replica may serve a snapshot read at T only when T ≤ T-safe
T_safe = min( T_paxos , T_TM )

// T_paxos = timestamp of highest applied Paxos write
// T_TM    = (min prepared-timestamp) - 1, or +infinity if none prepared

The reasoning behind T-TM deserves a careful look. When a transaction is prepared with timestamp p, we know with certainty its eventual commit timestamp will be ≥ p — it can land at p, or later, but never before p. So for any read timestamp strictly below the smallest such p, that pending transaction is irrelevant: it cannot retroactively change history that old. For read timestamps at or above p, though, we genuinely don't yet know where the transaction will commit — so we must hold off.

A replica freely serves reads at any timestamp ≤ T-safe. Above T-safe lies the recent horizon where committed and prepared transactions might still alter the answer — so the replica waits until T-safe advances past the requested timestamp.
How the Pieces Connect

T-paxos works because of Part IV's strict serializability (writes apply in monotonic timestamp order). T-TM works because of Part IV's commit protocol (a commit timestamp is never below its prepare timestamp). And both rest on Part II's TrueTime giving meaningful, monotonic timestamps in the first place. Snapshot reads are the capstone that consumes everything built before it.

Putting It All Together

Spanner began as an apparent contradiction: a database that is globally distributed, synchronously replicated, scalable, and offers strictly-ordered transactions spanning the world — everything the course's earlier trade-offs said you couldn't have at once. The entire lecture was the unwinding of that contradiction, and every piece depended on the one before it.

It starts with humility about clocks. We've always said "we know nothing about physical time." Spanner relaxes that to "we know one bound on the uncertainty," and packages it as TrueTime — honest intervals instead of dishonest single numbers, with the workhorse pattern of waiting until after(t) is certain.

That honest clock lets Spanner keep Paxos leader leases disjoint, fusing TrueTime's single-vote rule with quorum overlap — no central clock, no extra consensus, just a guaranteed shared voter forcing the gap. Disjoint leases hand each leader a private slice of the timeline, which makes commit timestamps monotonic across leaders for free. Monotonic timestamps are the serialization order; the two-phase commit with commit-wait pins that order to real time, delivering strict serializability. And the same timestamps, plus the multi-version store, let any replica serve lock-free snapshot reads by tracking T-safe = min(T-paxos, T-TM).

The Through-Line

One bargain recurs at every layer: be conservative, and pay for uncertainty in time, never in correctness. Leases that shrink under congestion, commit-waits that lengthen when clocks drift, reads that stall until T-safe climbs — in every case Spanner trades a little speed to stay correct, and because real clock skew is almost always under 10 ms, it stays fast in practice. Spanner doesn't beat the speed of light or perfectly sync clocks; it just refuses to lie about what it knows, and squeezes maximum performance out of an honest bound.

Since the 2012 paper, Spanner has steadily grown a full SQL layer and ever more use cases. The professor's closing observation is itself a systems lesson: the genuinely hard part of building a database isn't the query language — it's reliability, monitoring, and resilience. Spanner nails those, so it makes sense to keep piling features onto a foundation that is already battle-tested.
