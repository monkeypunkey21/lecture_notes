Part I

What Is a Distributed System?

This is the very first lecture of the course, so we start at zero. Before we can talk about why distributed systems are interesting or hard, we need a precise picture of what one even is. The word "distributed" gets thrown around loosely in everyday tech talk, so let's pin it down.

A Working Definition

When you use Google, Amazon, or TikTok, it feels like you are talking to one program on one computer. You type a query, you get a result. But behind that single, smooth experience is not one computer — it is a vast collection of separate machines, sitting in different buildings, sometimes on different continents, all passing messages to one another to produce your answer. That collection is the distributed system.

Key Concept — Distributed System

A distributed system is a collection of computers (or software elements) that cooperate over a network to provide some service to users. The defining features are (1) multiple independent machines and (2) they coordinate by sending messages, because they do not share memory the way threads on one computer do.

Two words in that definition are doing the heavy lifting. Cooperate means the machines are not just sitting side by side — they actively work together on the same task. Network means the only way they can talk is by sending messages across wires and fiber, which (as we will see) can be slow, can be dropped, and gives no shared clock or shared memory. Almost everything difficult about this course flows from that second word.

Reason #1 to Study Them: They Are Everywhere

So why devote a whole course to this? The first answer is simply that distributed systems run the modern world. Nearly every large software company — Google, Amazon, Netflix, Cloudflare — serves thousands or millions of clients across the globe using distributed systems. You cannot serve a planet's worth of users from a single machine in a garage.

The professor showed two telling images. The first was Google in 1997: a couple of computers in a garage. The second was Google today: hundreds of data centers spread across the world, each holding hundreds of thousands of machines, with users and data centers all interacting with one another. The same is true for Amazon, and for "edge" companies like Cloudflare that push servers even closer to users instead of concentrating them in big central data centers.

Distributed in Infrastructure and Software

Distribution shows up at two levels. Infrastructure is distributed: physical machines spread across the globe. But software is distributed too. Amazon's internal service-dependency graph is so dense its own engineers nicknamed it the "death star" — thousands of tiny microservices, each doing one job, all calling one another. Netflix and others have the same. A modern app is a web of small services talking over the network, not one big program.

What Are We Actually Trying to Achieve?

The lecture's first in-class discussion asked: imagine you are a Google engineer building search. What are your goals when serving results to users? The class brainstormed, and the answers cluster into a short list that, conveniently, is exactly the list of things distributed systems are for:

1

Resilience to failures — also called reliability, uptime, or availability. The service should keep working even when individual parts break.

2

Low latency — responses should be fast. And not just fast for one query: latency matters amortized across many queries and many users, since a real user fires off a whole sequence of requests.

3

Support for large load — this is a second, distinct kind of speed. One axis is "how fast does a request respond?" The other is "how many requests can I handle at once?" Handling more requests on the same hardware means lower cost and the ability to absorb real traffic.

4

Accuracy / relevance — easy access to the most relevant results. (A real systems goal, even if it overlaps with the search-quality side of things.)

Why This Matters Later

Keep this list — resilience, low latency, large load, accuracy — in your head. The entire rest of the course is about how distributed systems achieve these goals, and Part II is about why achieving them is so painfully hard. These four words are the "what"; everything else is the "how."

Notice why these goals push you toward distribution specifically. You want to handle geographic separation (customers worldwide, but still fast). You want to build reliable systems out of unreliable components (more on this below). You want to aggregate machines for capacity — and crucially, the old strategy of "just buy a bigger computer" (scaling up) is long gone. The modern strategy is to scale horizontally: buy a bunch of small, cheap machines and wire them together. And you want to customize services for specific tasks — the microservices pattern.

Part II

Why They Are Hard to Reason About

If distributed systems just meant "more computers," they would not deserve a course. The second — and arguably more important — reason to study them is that they are genuinely hard to reason about and hard to develop. Several deep challenges make them unlike ordinary single-machine programming. We'll take the three core ones in turn, then survey the rest.

Challenge 1: Partial Failure

On one computer, failure is usually all-or-nothing: the program crashes, or it doesn't. In a distributed system, parts fail while the rest keeps running. This is partial failure, and it is the defining headache of the field.

Key Concept — Partial Failure

Partial failure is when some components of a distributed system fail while others keep working. There is a famous line, attributed to Leslie Lamport, that captures it: "A distributed system is one where you can't get your work done because a machine you've never heard of is broken."

Here is why this is unavoidable, not just unlucky. Suppose a single server fails only once a month — very reliable. Now suppose you run hundreds of thousands of servers (Facebook's Prineville data center alone has on the order of hundreds of thousands of servers, half a million disks, thousands of network switches). When you aggregate that many "reliable" components, something is failing every day, every hour, maybe every minute. Rare events become constant events at scale.

The Big Idea

You must design assuming failures will happen — not as an edge case, but as the normal state of the world. The job is to build a system that tolerates failures gracefully and still presents users the illusion that everything is working, with "many nines" of reliability (down only seconds per year), all on top of hardware that is constantly breaking somewhere.

Challenge 2: Failures Are Ambiguous

It gets worse. Not only do parts fail — when something goes wrong, you often cannot even tell what went wrong. The class was asked: you send a request (say, to load your next reel on TikTok) and nothing comes back. Is it the server that failed? The network? Or are both just slow? Students proposed clever ideas — heartbeat signals, trying a different server, distinct status codes for different failure layers. These help sometimes, but the professor revealed it as a trick question.

The Fundamental Limit — You Cannot Always Tell

If you send a request and get a response, great — you learned something. But if you send a request and no response comes back, in the general case you cannot distinguish among: the server crashed, the network dropped your message, the network dropped the reply, or everything is just slow. You have no access to the other side of the world, so you can make no assumptions about delays. This ambiguity is fundamental to distribution, not a bug you can engineer away.

Challenge 3: Concurrency Over Shared State

A tempting "easy fix" came up: why not just partition users across machines — give each ZIP code or county its own server — and run them independently? Two problems sink this. The first is load: splits are hard to balance, load is dynamic (people send more requests after work, etc.), and you can't perfectly assign users to machines. But the deeper problem is shared state.

Why You Can't Just Split Everyone Up

If each county were truly independent, you'd only ever see the Instagram posts of people in your own county. The whole point of these applications is that users share state — they see and affect each other's data. So the machines must constantly communicate and synchronize across the split. You cannot simply partition the problem away; the state is inherently shared.

Sharing state across machines means you are running many users, many requests, and many operations concurrently against the same distributed state. If you've taken an operating systems course, you've met concurrency tools — locks, mutexes, semaphores. The natural question: why not just use those here?

Why Locks & Mutexes Don't Just Carry Over

Locks, mutexes, and semaphores are designed for one machine managing multiple threads in a single process. Here you have multiple machines. You'd need locks that span servers — and that's dangerous: the network or machines may be unreliable, so if one server holds a cross-server lock and then slows down or fails, it can freeze the system everywhere across the globe. We need ways to synchronize without holding locks that make everything unavailable when one cable somewhere is slow.

The Remaining Challenges: Scale, Testing, Security

Beyond those three core challenges, the lecture flagged several more that make distributed systems hard:

ChallengeWhy It's Painful
Design & evaluation at scaleYou may have to completely redesign your software for different magnitudes of load. It's very hard to predict how a system behaves at huge scale before you're there.
Testing & correctnessWith many concurrent/distributed threads, the number of possible execution orderings grows exponentially. To truly verify correctness you'd have to check all the interleavings — infeasible by brute force.
SecurityNetworks plus services with different trust levels create their own class of problems.

That exponential blowup in testing deserves a picture. Imagine just two threads, A and B, each taking a couple of small steps. The system's behavior can depend on the order in which those steps interleave — and there are many possible orders even for two threads. Scale to hundreds or thousands of threads and the number of orderings explodes.

Even two threads of two steps each fan out into many interleavings. Each leaf is a distinct execution the system might take — and to prove correctness you'd have to check all of them. With thousands of threads, this tree is astronomically large.

None of this is new. Distributed systems have been recognized as important for over 40 years, and many Turing Award winners worked on them — directly (Leslie Lamport, Barbara Liskov, Butler Lampson) or on the formal-methods and correctness side (Clarke, Emerson, Sifakis, Pnueli).

Part III

The Goal of the Class

We've now seen the carrot (distributed systems are everywhere and achieve goals no single machine can) and the stick (they fail partially, ambiguously, and concurrently). So what is this course actually trying to teach you to do about all of that? It comes down to one unifying goal.

Building One Machine Out of Many

The Central Goal of This Course

Build an abstraction that unifies several machines so that, from the user's perspective, they act as a single, coherent, unified thing. This abstraction should (1) enable concurrent use of shared state and resources, and (2) hide all the failures happening underneath. The point is to automate the heavy lifting so that application developers don't each have to re-solve partial failure, ambiguity, and concurrency from scratch.

This reframes everything. Distributed-systems engineering is the discipline of building better abstractions — for developers or end users — so that a messy pile of unreliable, geographically scattered machines can be used as a clean building block inside some larger system. You don't want every team that needs a distributed service to rediscover consensus and failure handling on their own. You want to package that hard work once, behind a tidy interface.

The Catch: Abstraction vs. Performance

If we could provide a perfect abstraction for free, the course would be short. We can't. There is a fundamental tension we'll fight all term.

The Core Trade-off

The stronger the abstraction you provide, the worse the performance you tend to get. If you want an abstraction that looks totally sequential, with no faults, where everything runs perfectly, then something becomes slow. Much of the course is learning to navigate this trade-off — and to understand its fundamental limits, which show up as "impossibility results" (theorems saying certain perfect guarantees simply cannot be achieved).

Contrast With Operating Systems

A clean way to lock in what makes distributed systems distinctive is to compare them with operating systems, which solve a similar-sounding but opposite problem. Both fields are about illusions — but they create mirror-image illusions.

Operating SystemsDistributed Systems
Give every application the illusion that it runs on its own machine…Give one application the illusion that it runs on a single machine…
…even though they all share one machine.…even though it runs across many machines.
One physical machine → many virtual ones.Many physical machines → one virtual one.
Two Illusions, Pointing Opposite Ways

An OS takes one machine and makes it look like many (each process thinks it owns the computer). A distributed system takes many machines and makes them look like one (the app thinks it runs on a single, reliable computer). Holding these two side by side is the cleanest way to remember what distributed systems are for.

From this goal, the lecture stated the concrete class outcomes. As a developer of distributed systems, you should be able to reason about the uncertainty inside them. As a user of distributed systems, you should be able to identify the guarantees and properties a system offers — when it will work well, when it will break, and what that means for whatever higher-level goal you're using it for.

Part IV

The Fireworks Problem & Consensus

Abstract challenges land harder when you feel them in a concrete puzzle. The lecture used a deliberately old-fashioned story to make one of the deepest results in the field tangible — and to give you a first taste of an impossibility result before the course formalizes it later.

Two Towns Across a Foggy Lake

Picture two towns on opposite shores of a lake. They want to launch a joint fireworks show at exactly the same time — if one starts and the other doesn't, it's a flop. So they must agree on an exact time. The complication: this is before phones, so they can only communicate by sending boat messengers across the lake. And the messengers are unreliable — fog, incompetence, whatever — so a boat might get lost and never arrive. The goal: find a protocol that lets both towns agree on the exact firing time.

Map the Story to the System

The two towns are two computers. The lake with fog is an unreliable network that can drop messages. The boat messengers are network packets that may never arrive. "Agree on the exact time" is "agree on a shared value." Hold this mapping in mind — the puzzle is the systems problem in costume.

The Endless Acknowledgement

The class worked toward the natural idea of acknowledgements (acks). Let's trace it and watch it fail.

1

Town 1 sends Town 2: "Fireworks on April 10th, 10:42 PM." Is that enough? No — if the boat is lost, T1 can't know T2 heard it. T1 needs an acknowledgement before it can safely fire.

2

So Town 2 sends back an ack: "Got it — April 10th, 10:42." Now is it safe? Still no. T2 has no idea whether that ack arrived. If it was lost, T1 is still waiting and might never fire — while T2 sits there assuming the show is on.

3

So T1 must ack the ack: "Confirmed, I got your confirmation." But now that message might be lost, and T1 can't be sure T2 received it… so T1 is unsure again.

4

You can see the trap. Every ack needs an ack. No matter how many you send, the last sender is never sure their message arrived. The chain never terminates.

The Aha Moment

No finite number of acknowledgements can ever make both sides certain they agree, when messages can be lost. There is always one more message whose delivery is unconfirmed. This isn't a failure of cleverness — it's a structural impossibility baked into "unreliable channel + need for mutual certainty."

The Real Name: The Consensus Problem

The systems version drops the fireworks: imagine two database replicas on opposite sides of the world that must agree on a single value — say, the value in one row of a table. The network between them is unreliable and can drop messages. Can they always agree on a common value?

Key Concept — Consensus

The consensus problem is getting multiple machines to agree on a shared value. The lecture's headline result: when the network connecting them is unreliable (messages can be dropped), perfect consensus is impossible — you cannot achieve it with all the guarantees you'd want. Something must be sacrificed. This is one of the foundational impossibility results of the field, and the course will formalize it later.

Where the Course Goes From Here

Consensus is just the opening example. The course will cover a connected set of topics, all variations on "coordinate unreliable machines over an unreliable network":

TopicThe Question It Answers
Clock synchronization & logical clocksHow do machines agree on time and ordering when there's no shared clock?
Failure recoveryHow does the system get back to a good state after parts fail?
ConsistencyHow do we make shared state look consistent from an outside observer's view?
ConsensusHow (and how well) can machines agree on a value? (the fireworks problem)
Distributed transactionsHow do we provide locking-style guarantees over distributed state?
Real systemsHow do production systems balance all these theoretical trade-offs in practice?

Part V

Course Logistics

The rest of the lecture was practical — but several pieces are worth pinning down because they shape how you should approach the term. The course is CS134 Distributed Systems, taught in Spring 2026 by Professor Kristen Dinos (who goes by Kai), with TAs Kaelin and Sebastian and LAs Alec and Peter. Discussion sections are on Fridays.

Two Halves: Foundations and Real Systems

The class is split roughly in two, and the professor was emphatic that you need both:

Theory and Practice — Not Either/Or

One half is beautiful foundations: clean, elegant theory with crisp labels, letting you draw big conclusions about what can and cannot work (the impossibility results). The other half is real-world systems: messier, but functional and able to actually do the job. With only one half you get nowhere in this field — the theory tells you the limits, the systems show you how to live within them.

Assignments, Grading & the Exam

There are four programming assignments, all written in Go (syntactically close to a cleaner C++; easy to pick up if you know a lower-level language). The first is an introductory MapReduce assignment and is done individually. Assignments 2–4 build on each other, may be done in groups of two, and are much harder.

Heed the Difficulty Warning

The professor stressed this directly: assignment 2 is at least 2× (for them, 3×) the effort of assignment 1, and 2–4 stack on each other. Start early. The assignments ship with test cases you must deeply understand to debug — you cannot brute-force them. Late penalties are exponential by day: 1%, then 2%, 4%, 8%… (max 99% credit). Deadlines are 10 PM, and a "late day" is a 24-hour window.

Grade breakdown: labs ≈ 57% (weighted more as they get harder), final exam 40%, and discussion attendance 3% (1% for each of the first three discussions). The final exam is June 12th with no makeups except dire circumstances. The professor strongly urged attending discussion sections — even more than lecture — because that's where you get hands-on help with the hard assignments.

The AI Policy (Read This Carefully)

The course takes a notably open but strict stance on AI, and the details matter for your grade.

The Rule — Use AI Freely, But Disclose It

You may use AI as much as you want. The only requirement: submit an AI_usage.txt per assignment precisely describing what you did. Using AI will not affect your grade — even "I had an LLM write the whole thing" earns full credit if disclosed. But if you're found to have used AI without disclosing it, you are heavily penalized. Also: AI output is held to academic-integrity standards — if five people submit identical AI-generated code, that counts as copying. If you don't use AI, just write that you didn't.

The Professor's Honest Advice

Beyond the rules, a personal nudge: "The less you write, the less you understand." If you let an LLM write the whole assignment, you won't learn anything. Use it for syntax or scaffolding, but engage with the genuinely hard parts yourself — that's the entire point of being in the class.

Logistics housekeeping: announcements go on Bruin Learn; all communication should go through Piazza (public or private posts), since email is easy to lose. Lectures are recorded, but recordings are explicitly not a replacement — last year, students who skipped lecture did worse.

Putting It All Together

This first lecture is really one continuous argument, and the parts lock together into a single line of reasoning. Part I set the stage: a distributed system is many machines cooperating over a network, and we can't avoid them because the goals of any planet-scale service — resilience, low latency, large load, accuracy — are exactly the goals that force you to spread work across cheap machines.

But that move has a price, which is Part II. The instant you have many machines on an unreliable network, you inherit partial failure (parts break constantly at scale), failure ambiguity (you often can't even tell what broke), and concurrency over shared state (you can't just partition the problem away, and single-machine locks don't transfer). Those three challenges are the "stick" that makes the field hard — and the reason it's worth a whole course.

Part III gave the response to that difficulty: the course's one big goal is to build an abstraction that turns many unreliable machines into one coherent, reliable-looking machine — the mirror image of what an operating system does. But you can't get this for free; the deeper the illusion, the more performance you pay, and some perfect guarantees are flatly impossible. Part IV made that last claim concrete: the fireworks-across-a-foggy-lake puzzle is the consensus problem in disguise, and the never-ending chain of acknowledgements is your first encounter with an impossibility result — you cannot achieve perfect agreement over an unreliable channel. Finally, Part V grounded all of it in how the class will actually run: theory plus real systems, four hard Go assignments, and a refreshingly honest "use AI but disclose it" policy.

The One Sentence to Remember

Distributed systems exist to make many unreliable machines behave like one reliable machine — and the whole discipline is the art of doing that as well as possible while respecting the hard limits (like consensus impossibility) on how good "as well as possible" can be.

What's Next

From here the course leaves the motivation behind and dives into the machinery. Expect to start formalizing the ideas this lecture only sketched: how machines agree on time and ordering (clock synchronization and logical clocks), and then consensus, consistency, failure recovery, and distributed transactions, before circling back to how real production systems balance these trade-offs. The fireworks/consensus impossibility you met today will keep reappearing — it's the boundary against which all of those topics are designed. Practically: start Assignment 1 (MapReduce) early, go to Discussion 1 (it includes a tutorial for getting started), and find a partner for assignments 2–4.
