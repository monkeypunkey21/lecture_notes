Part I

Properties, Traces & What "Correct" Even Means

Before we can talk about whether Paxos "works," we need a precise vocabulary for what working means. In distributed systems we never reason about a single run of a program; we reason about all the runs it could ever have. The lecture opened by recapping exactly this idea from a quiz, and it's the foundation everything else rests on, so we'll start here.

What It Means for a System to Satisfy a Property

Imagine you've built a system — a protocol, a server, a cluster of machines passing messages. You want to know: does it behave the way I want? To answer that crisply, we do two things. First we describe a property: the set of behaviors we consider acceptable. Then we look at the traces the system can actually produce — a trace is one possible recorded history of everything that happened, step by step.

Key Concept — Trace

A trace is a single observable execution of the system: the ordered sequence of events (messages sent, values accepted, processes crashing) that one particular run produces. A system has many possible traces because messages can be delayed, reordered, or lost, and processes can fail at different moments.

With that vocabulary, satisfying a property has a sharp definition: every trace the system can produce must lie inside the property. Think of the property as a big set of "allowed histories." If even one possible run escapes that set, the system does not satisfy the property.

One Trace Disproves, But Cannot Prove

This asymmetry is the most important takeaway of the recap. To disprove a property you only need a single bad trace — a counterexample. But to prove a property you must show all traces are good, which is far harder. Showing one good run tells you nothing, because the next run might be the bad one.

The quiz question that opened the lecture asked: how do you prove a system satisfies a safety property? "Show one finite trace satisfies it" and "show one infinite trace satisfies it" are both wrong — a single trace can only ever disprove. The correct answers were "show all finite traces satisfy it" and "show all infinite traces satisfy it." And there's a subtle bonus: every finite trace is just a prefix of some infinite trace, so the set of finite traces is a subset of the infinite ones. If you prove all infinite traces are good, the finite ones come along for free.

Two Flavors of Property: Safety vs Liveness

Properties come in two fundamentally different shapes, and the whole back half of the lecture hinges on the distinction.

SafetyLiveness
"Something bad never happens.""Something good eventually happens."
Violated by a finite trace — you can point to the exact moment it broke.Violated only by an infinite trace — the good thing is forever delayed, never outright contradicted.
Example: two processes never decide different values.Example: every process eventually decides some value.
Why This Distinction Matters Later

Hold onto this. In Part IV we'll prove that Paxos guarantees its safety properties unconditionally but cannot guarantee its liveness property — and the counterexample we build will be an infinite trace, exactly as the definition above predicts. The safety/liveness split is the lens through which the FLP impossibility result becomes intuitive.

Part II

The Paxos Protocol — Agreement Among the Unreliable

Now the central object of the lecture. Consensus is the problem of getting a collection of machines to agree on a single value, even though messages get lost and machines crash. Paxos is a fault-tolerant protocol that solves it. Why do we care? Because almost every serious distributed system — databases, configuration stores, lock services — needs a way for a cluster to agree on "what happened next," and Paxos (or a descendant of it) is how they do it.

Key Concept — Consensus

Given a set of proposed values from different machines, a consensus protocol drives the whole system to decide on one of those values, such that everyone who decides agrees on the same one. Consensus is the abstract problem; Paxos is one concrete solution to it. (The lecture stressed this split — last time, a quiz "trick question" mixed properties of consensus-in-general with properties specific to Paxos.)

Three Roles, Two Phases

Paxos cleanly separates three roles. In a real deployment one physical machine usually plays all three at once, but keeping them conceptually separate is what makes the protocol comprehensible.

The Three Roles

Proposers suggest values and drive the protocol forward. Acceptors are the voting members — they accept proposals, and a majority of them agreeing is what constitutes a decision. Learners have one job: to find out what value was finally chosen. Crucially, only learners ever know consensus has been reached.

And there are two phases: a prepare round and an accept round. The whole reason consensus is subtle — and the source of both its safety and its liveness troubles — is this two-phase structure. We'll see why two phases are necessary, and what breaks if you try to collapse them into one.

Phase 1 — Prepare

A proposer wanting to push a value first asks permission. It picks a proposal number N and sends prepare(N) to all acceptors.

The Proposal Number N

N must be unique across proposers and higher than any N this proposer used before. Uniqueness is cheap: just partition the number space among proposers (proposer 1 uses 1, 4, 7…; proposer 2 uses 2, 5, 8…). Notably we do not require N to be higher than every other proposer's numbers — that would need global coordination, which is exactly what we're trying to avoid. Each proposer only outpaces its own past numbers.

When an acceptor receives prepare(N), one of three things happens:

1

It already promised a higher number. The acceptor has committed not to accept anything below some N' > N. It rejects: "no, that's stale."

2

It has already accepted some proposal. It replies promise(N) but attaches the proposal it previously accepted — both the accepted number and its value. This attachment is the secret sauce of safety: it propagates already-chosen values forward.

3

It has promised nothing higher and accepted nothing. It simply replies promise(N), committing to ignore anything numbered below N from now on.

Phase 2 — Accept

Once a proposer hears promise back from a majority of acceptors, it may proceed to phase two. But it doesn't blindly push its own value. It first inspects all the promises: if any acceptor reported a previously-accepted value, the proposer must adopt the value attached to the highest accepted number it saw. Only if nobody had accepted anything is the proposer free to use its own value.

Why "Adopt the Highest Accepted Value"?

This single rule is what makes Paxos safe. If some value might already have been chosen by a majority earlier, a new proposer will see it in the promises and re-propose that same value rather than overwriting it. Newer proposers carry old decisions forward instead of clobbering them — that's how the system "never changes its mind" once consensus is reached, even while individual acceptors are free to.

The proposer then sends accept(N, value) to a majority (or all) acceptors. An acceptor receiving this checks one thing: have I promised a higher number to anyone? If yes, ignore it (failure). If no, accept it, record (N, value) locally, and notify the learners.

// Acceptor logic, the whole thing in pseudocode
on prepare(n):
    if n < promised_n:        // already promised something higher
        reply "reject"
    else:
        promised_n = n
        reply promise(n, accepted_n, accepted_val)  // attach prior accept, if any

on accept(n, val):
    if n < promised_n:        // someone newer promised higher — stale
        reply "fail"
    else:
        accepted_n, accepted_val = n, val
        notify learners(n, val)
The One Check an Acceptor Makes — And It's Global

When deciding whether to accept, an acceptor asks only: "Have I promised a higher number to anyone?" — not "to this particular proposer." It's a single local variable. An acceptor does not care what value it previously accepted; if no higher promise is outstanding, it happily overwrites its old accepted value with the new one. Misreading this as per-proposer state is a classic error.

A Full Execution, Step by Step

Let's trace the example the professor walked through: two proposers (P1, P2) and three acceptors (A1, A2, A3). Watch how a value gets locked in.

P1 drives value X to consensus. The red circle marks the instant a majority has accepted — after this, X is locked.
1

P1 sends prepare(5) to all three acceptors. Two of them currently hold an old accepted number 4. Since 5 > 4, they don't accept yet — they promise. (A common slip is to say "5 > 4 so they accept." No: prepare only ever yields a promise, never an accept.)

2

P1 hears promise(5) from a majority. Now it may run phase 2. Suppose, at the same time, P2 sends prepare(4). A3 can promise 4, but A2 — having already promised 5 — cannot promise the lower 4. P2 is now behind.

3

P1 sends accept(5, X). A majority accept it and reply with acceptance messages. This is the moment consensus is reached — a majority now holds value X. From here, nothing can dislodge X.

4

P2, still in the dark, sends prepare(6) to A2 and A3. A3 — having accepted nothing — replies promise(6). A2 replies promise(6) too, but attaches its accepted value X. A2 isn't doing this because it "knows consensus happened" — it has no idea. It attaches X simply because that's what it accepted.

5

P2 wanted to propose Y, but the rules force its hand: it saw an accepted value X in a promise, so it must re-propose the highest one. P2 sends accept(6, X). Everyone accepts X. The system happily converges on X — P2's intended Y never had a chance once consensus on X was reached.

The Big Idea — Majority + Carry-Forward

Two mechanisms together make Paxos safe: (1) nothing is ever accepted without a majority behind it, and (2) any future proposer is forced to carry forward the highest already-accepted value. Because any two majorities of acceptors must overlap in at least one acceptor, a new proposer is guaranteed to see any value that might already have been chosen — and is then compelled to re-propose it. That overlap is the mathematical heart of why Paxos is correct.

A Practical Anchor — Counting the Majority

How does a proposer know when it has "a majority"? Because every node knows the total number of acceptors up front — it's a fixed, agreed-upon assumption of the system. With 3 acceptors, the moment you hear from 2, you know you have a majority. No node ever needs to discover the cluster size at runtime.

Part III

Why Paxos Is Correct — Thinking About What Goes Wrong

Here's a confession the professor made out loud: even the inventor, Leslie Lamport, found Paxos so hard to explain that he published a follow-up paper titled "Paxos Made Simple" whose abstract dryly insists the algorithm "when presented in plain English, is very simple." It isn't, quite. So rather than a full proof, the lecture used the standard distributed-systems move: what-if analysis.

The Method — Enumerate How It Could Break

Key Concept — What-If Analysis

Before you can prove a protocol correct, you must enumerate every way it could go wrong. A real proof is a case analysis over all those failure modes, showing each one is actually fine. Even short of a full proof, hunting for what could go wrong builds the intuition for why the protocol holds. In this class, that hunt is the main tool.

The Execution That Paxos Forbids

Consider three proposer/acceptor pairs where each proposer only ever reaches one acceptor: P1→A1, P2→A2, P3→A3. Each prepares, gets a single promise back, then sends an accept — and ends up with three acceptors each holding a different accepted value. No majority anywhere. The system is wedged: there's no agreed value and no obvious way to reconcile.

This Execution Is Impossible — And That's the Point

The escape is the majority rule. A proposer may only send an accept after hearing promises from a majority of acceptors. Hearing from a single acceptor is not enough. So this "everyone accepted something different" state can never arise — Paxos simply does not permit those accept messages to be sent. The protocol design rules out the disaster by construction.

This directly answers a student's earlier worry: "what if we have 5 acceptors and each proposer only contacts 2?" Then no proposer ever gets a majority (you need 3 of 5), so no accept is ever legal, and the system makes no incorrect progress — it just stalls. Stalling is a liveness problem (Part IV), not a safety violation. Safety is preserved.

Acceptors Are Allowed to Change Their Mind

Now the genuinely counterintuitive part. Consider a run where a value X has been accepted by a majority — consensus is reached — but one acceptor, A1, accepted X while a later proposer is trying to get value Y chosen on the side. For the system to function, can A1 ever switch to a different value?

Yes — Single Acceptors May Change Their Mind

The protocol never forbade an acceptor from accepting a new value. And it must allow this — otherwise the system could deadlock in a state with no way to make progress. An acceptor that already accepted something will gladly accept a new proposal, as long as it hasn't promised a higher number. It does not cling to its old value. This flexibility is what lets the system claw its way out of stuck configurations.

But — and this is the crucial guardrail — once consensus has been reached (a majority holds the same value), the system as a whole can never change its mind. Individual acceptors flipping is fine; the collective decision is permanent. How are these reconciled? Through the carry-forward rule from Part II: because any new majority overlaps the old one, any new proposer is forced to re-propose the already-chosen value. Individual acceptors may wobble, but they can only ever wobble toward the chosen value, never away from it.

The Central Dissonance

Stack the three facts the lecture arrived at, and feel the tension:

1

A single acceptor can change its mind.

2

No acceptor ever knows when consensus has been reached — only learners find that out, and only by hearing from a majority.

3

Yet once consensus is reached, the system can never change its mind.

The Big Idea — Correctness Without Knowledge

Paxos achieves an irrevocable collective decision even though no acceptor knows the decision was made and every acceptor is free to flip individually. The system enforces the outcome structurally — via majority overlap and carry-forward — not by any node being aware of it. A node never gets a memo saying "consensus reached, stop changing." It works anyway. That's why decoupling proposer / acceptor / learner roles matters: it forces you to confront that correctness is a global property, not something any single participant can observe.

Part IV

The Limits — Livelock and the FLP Impossibility

We've seen Paxos is safe. The natural next question: is it also live — does it always eventually decide? The answer is a hard no, and that "no" turns out to be not a bug in Paxos but a fundamental law of distributed computing.

The Three Properties We Want from Consensus

Consensus has three target properties. The lecture went through whether Paxos satisfies each.

PropertyMeaningPaxos?
ValidityThe agreed value must be one that was actually proposed.✓ Trivially — nobody proposes a value out of thin air.
AgreementAll correct processes decide the same value.✓ This is the safety we proved in Part III.
TerminationEvery correct process eventually decides.✗ This is liveness — and Paxos cannot guarantee it.
Validity Isn't Just a Technicality

Validity was added precisely to rule out a cheating "consensus" protocol that always decides, say, 0 regardless of inputs. Such a protocol would satisfy agreement and termination for free but be useless. Validity forces the decision to reflect a real proposal.

Agreement and validity are safety properties — "nothing bad happens." Termination is a liveness property — "something good eventually happens." And per Part I, to disprove liveness we need an infinite trace where the good thing never occurs. Let's build one.

The Livelock Counterexample

Two proposers can keep each other out forever, even with no crashes and a perfectly connected network:

1

Proposer P1 completes phase 1 with number N0 — it has a majority's promises and is about to do its accept.

2

Right then, P2 completes phase 1 with a higher number N1 > N0. The acceptors have now promised N1.

3

P1 tries its accept(N0, …) — but the acceptors promised the higher N1, so they reject it. P1 retries with a number higher than N1.

4

Now P2 tries its accept — and gets rejected, because P1's new prepare bumped the promised number again. P2 retries higher. And this repeats forever.

Livelock — Busy Forever, Deciding Never

This is a livelock (not a deadlock — everyone is actively working). The trace is genuinely infinite: the two proposers leapfrog each other's proposal numbers indefinitely, and neither ever completes phase 2. Because we exhibited an infinite trace in which no value is ever chosen, we have disproved termination. Note the root cause: it's the two-phase structure that lets a second proposer "swoop in" and invalidate the first proposer's prepare.

A student protested: "isn't this impractical / virtually impossible?" In practice, yes — it's astronomically unlikely. But at the level of proofs we must be honest: a counterexample exists, so termination is not satisfied. Rarity doesn't make a property hold.

Expand: why can't we just merge the two phases to kill the livelock?

The livelock comes from having two phases, so a tempting fix is one phase: proposer sends propose-and-accept(5, X) directly, retrying with a higher number if it doesn't hear a majority. But this trades a liveness bug for a safety bug. Suppose two proposers propose different values with the same number to different acceptors:

  • If acceptors may change their mind: a majority could accept X, then later flip to Y — the system would change its mind after consensus, violating agreement.
  • If acceptors may not change their mind: they can get permanently stuck holding different values with no majority and no way to reconcile — worse than before, because now nobody can ever fix it, and no node even knows the system is stuck.

Either way, collapsing the phases destroys safety. The prepare phase exists precisely so a proposer can discover and carry forward any value that might already be chosen before it commits. Two phases is not an accident — it's load-bearing.

The FLP Impossibility Result

Is this just a Paxos weakness? No — and here's the deep result. Recall the fireworks story from early in the course: two towns must agree on a show time, but messengers crossing the fog can be lost, so no finite exchange of confirmations ever lets both sides be certain they agree. That intuition was made into a theorem.

Key Concept — The FLP Impossibility (Fischer, Lynch, Paterson)

In an asynchronous network (no bound on message delay) with even one process that may fail, it is impossible to design a consensus protocol that guarantees all three of validity, agreement, and termination. The asynchrony is the crux: with no timing bound, you can never distinguish "slow" from "crashed," so termination must be sacrificed.

The Big Idea — You Cannot Have It All

FLP says safety and liveness for consensus are fundamentally incompatible under realistic (asynchronous, faulty) conditions. Paxos makes the principled choice: it never sacrifices safety, and accepts that liveness can theoretically fail. No amount of clever engineering escapes this — it's a theorem, not an implementation gap. Every consensus system you'll ever use lives under FLP's shadow.

Can we do better in practice? Yes — we can make livelock vanishingly rare without ever truly guaranteeing termination:

  • Randomized timeouts / exponential backoff: proposers wait a random interval before retrying, so they stop colliding at the same instant.
  • Predetermined proposer order: a rejection can include the identity of the proposer the acceptor is committed to, helping the loser back off.
  • Leader election: elect one proposer to drive proposals, so there's no contention to livelock on (we'll develop this in Part VI).

The professor's framing: Paxos is cooperative. These tricks improve the common case, but the FLP-mandated worst case remains.

Fault Tolerance — How Many Nodes?

To tolerate F acceptor failures, how many acceptors do you need? The answer falls straight out of the majority rule.

2F + 1 Acceptors, F + 1 Proposers

With 2F + 1 acceptors, if F fail, F + 1 survive — still a majority — so the system keeps deciding. You can lose nearly half your acceptors and carry on. Proposers are easier: you only need F + 1 to tolerate F failures, because a single surviving proposer that keeps trying is enough to make progress. There's no "majority of proposers" requirement — proposers don't vote, they just drive.

The "2" in 2F+1 traces to the two points in the protocol — prepare and accept — where a proposer must hear back from a majority. Lose the ability to form a majority and the proposer can do nothing, so you must keep enough acceptors alive to always assemble one.

Part V

From One Decision to a Whole Service — The Replicated State Machine

Paxos agrees on one value. But a real service — a bank, a key-value store — processes a never-ending stream of requests. We want a cluster of replicas that all stay in perfect sync. This is the replicated state machine (RSM), and it's where Paxos becomes genuinely useful. (Fully working this out is Homework 3, which the professor warned is very hard — so we'll build the high-level scaffold.)

Key Concept — Replicated State Machine

Run identical copies of a service on several machines. If every replica starts in the same state and applies the same updates in the same order, they remain identical forever. The entire problem therefore reduces to one thing: get all replicas to agree on the order of updates. And agreeing is exactly what Paxos does.

The Shared-Log Abstraction

So how do we go from "agree on one value" to "agree on an ordered stream"? The key insight a student offered: don't run one Paxos instance — run many, one per position in a log.

1

Every replica plays all three roles — proposer, acceptor, learner — at once.

2

Picture a shared log: an array of slots, slot 0, slot 1, slot 2, … Each replica holds its own copy of this log.

3

Run a separate, independent consensus instance for each slot. Slot i gets its own Paxos round to decide which request goes in slot i.

4

Once consensus fills the slots in order — "deposit here, interest there, withdrawal next" — every replica applies the requests in log order and stays identical.

Solid slots have reached consensus and can be applied in order; dashed slots are still being agreed on.
Two Ways to Replicate — and Why We Pick the Log

A student suggested an alternative: agree on the entire state of the machine at each step rather than the next update. That's correct too! But it costs enormously more memory and bandwidth — you'd ship the whole state every round. Agreeing on just the next update in an ordered log is the classic, lean approach: order the items, replay them, done.

When Is a Replica Ready to Apply Slot i?

A replica can apply the request in slot i to its actual memory only once that slot's decision is final — once it won't change. And how does a replica learn the decision is final? Through its learner role:

The Apply Condition

A replica may apply slot i when, as a learner, it has heard from a majority of replicas that they accepted the same request for slot i. Majority acceptance = consensus = the decision is permanent = safe to apply. This is the learner's whole purpose: detecting that a majority converged.

What about requests arriving while slot i is still being agreed? Queue them. Each replica buffers incoming requests and always proposes the head of its queue for the next open slot, retrying until its request lands somewhere. Nothing is dropped; everything eventually gets a slot.

Two Real Problems With the Log

Throughput: because everything funnels through one sequential log, requests can't run concurrently — the single log is a bottleneck under load. Unbounded growth: a service running for weeks accumulates a gigantic log, so you need a garbage-collection strategy to discard slots that are safely applied everywhere and will never be revisited. Part VI tackles the throughput side.

Paxos vs Primary-Backup — What Did We Buy?

We already had primary-backup replication, so why bother with all this Paxos machinery? Here's the trade.

Primary-BackupPaxos RSM
A single primary (plus a view service) coordinates; a centralized hierarchy.No primary, no view service — replicas talk peer-to-peer and agree among themselves.
Needs the primary and the view service up; loses either and you run a view-change protocol.Needs only a majority of replicas up. Half the network can die and the service keeps working.
One round of communication — fast.Best case: one extra round (double the messages). Worst case: livelock, arbitrary delay.
The view service is a potential single point of failure.No single point of failure.
The Big Idea — Trading Latency for Resilience

Paxos's headline win is the elimination of any single point of failure: you only need a majority alive, not one specific node. The cost is communication — at best one extra round per decision, at worst unbounded delay under livelock. For a company like Facebook running thousands of servers, that trade is obvious: you cannot let one machine's failure take the whole service down. Fault tolerance is worth doubling the message cost.

Where Does the Client Connect?

With no view service, how does a client find a replica? It doesn't need a directory — it knows the static list of replica addresses up front (these are fixed, write-once IPs for the cluster) and just tries them. Any replica can propose, so whichever one responds is fine. No centralized lookup required.

D is too strong — you never need all replicas, only a majority, which is the whole point of fault tolerance.
Part VI

Making It Fast — Multi-Paxos and Leader-Based Paxos

The basic RSM works but is wasteful: two full phases per slot, one log slot at a time. The lecture closed by asking — are we solving the right problem, or restricting ourselves needlessly? The answer is the latter, and two optimizations recover most of the lost performance without sacrificing correctness or worst-case behavior.

Multi-Paxos — Skip the Prepare Phase

Here's the observation that unlocks everything. After a proposer successfully completes a round — prepare, promise, accept, accepted — what does it know?

The Streak Insight

A proposer that just succeeded knows it has a majority of acceptors already on its side — they promised to it and accepted its value. So for the next slot, why re-run phase 1 to win them over again? They already trust this proposer. It can jump straight to phase 2 (accept) and skip the prepare round entirely.

Key Concept — Multi-Paxos

Multi-Paxos: once a proposer has won a round, it keeps a "streak" and skips the prepare phase on subsequent proposals, going directly to accept. This halves the communication per decision in the steady state — one round instead of two. It's proven correct, heavily used in production (Amazon DynamoDB is one example), and the protocol is necessarily a bit different from vanilla Paxos to make the streak safe.

What if a competing proposer steals the majority? Then the streak-holder "loses the streak": its next accept gets rejected, and it must fall back to a full prepare round. So how much worse is Multi-Paxos than plain Paxos in the worst case?

The Big Idea — Better Best Case, No Worse Worst Case

In the worst case (constant contention, streaks always broken), Multi-Paxos degrades to exactly plain Paxos — never worse. In the common case (one proposer running uncontended), it's twice as fast. Improving the best case while never harming the worst case is the gold standard of optimization. That's why it's safe to deploy everywhere.

Leader-Based Paxos — Consensus on the View, Not the Value

Multi-Paxos batches by exploiting streaks. A more radical reframing: what if we use consensus for a different question entirely?

Today we run consensus on values: "agree on the value for slot 0, then agree on the value for slot 1…" Instead, use consensus once to elect a leader (agree on the view — who's in charge). After that, route every client request to the leader, which is permanently in the accept phase because there's no contention to fight. We don't need a separate view service — the replicas are the view service, agreeing on the view among themselves.

Key Concept — Leader-Based Paxos

Maintain a sequence of views instead of a sequence of values. Consensus decides who proposes, not what is proposed. With an agreed leader and no contention, every request takes a single round. The decoupling is elegant: heavy consensus machinery runs rarely (only to change leaders), while the common path is fast. This is the family that includes Raft and similar protocols.

This raises two new questions the lecture flagged:

1

What if the leader crashes? Detect that the leader has gone silent — learned from clients reporting it unreachable, or from replicas noticing no response for a while — then re-run the consensus-for-the-view to elect a new leader. (Next lecture covers a cleaner mechanism using leases / validity periods.)

2

The geo-replication drawback: all requests must travel to the one leader. If your leader sits in Brazil and your users are elsewhere, every request pays that distance. Leaderless Paxos lets a client hit any nearby replica; a leader concentrates traffic at one possibly-distant point. A genuine trade-off, not a free lunch.

Optimizations Don't Escape FLP

None of these tricks — randomized backoff, Multi-Paxos, leaders, leases — actually guarantees liveness. They make the bad executions rare and fast to recover from, but the FLP impossibility from Part IV is permanent. With "practical luck" you avoid the pathological runs, yet no execution is ever provably guaranteed to terminate. Engineering improves the odds; it cannot repeal the theorem.

The Consensus Landscape

Paxos is one star in a crowded sky. The lecture named the neighbors:

  • Multi-Paxos — the streak-skipping optimization above.
  • View-stamped replication — an early leader-based protocol.
  • ZAB — the consensus engine inside ZooKeeper (coming up next lecture).
  • Raft — similar in power to Paxos but deliberately designed to be simpler to understand.

What they share: they all agree on a sequence of values and/or use a leader-style structure — exactly the two ideas from this part. The landscape is tangled enough that a 2015 research paper exists purely to reconcile the terminology and compare which protocol does what better.

Multi-Paxos lets a proposer skip the prepare phase while it holds a "streak." Compared to plain Paxos, what is its worst-case communication cost?
A
Strictly worse — skipping prepare adds extra recovery rounds.
B
Exactly the same as plain Paxos — when streaks keep breaking, it falls back to full two-phase rounds, so it's never worse.
C
Half of plain Paxos in all cases, including the worst case.
D
Unbounded, because losing a streak causes a livelock unique to Multi-Paxos.
Correct — B. Under constant contention every streak is immediately broken, so each proposal reverts to a full prepare-plus-accept round — identical to plain Paxos, never worse. The two-rounds-into-one savings only appears in the uncontended best case. That "improve the best case without degrading the worst case" profile is exactly why Multi-Paxos is the gold-standard kind of optimization. C overstates the win (it's only best-case); D invents a Multi-Paxos-specific livelock that doesn't exist.

Putting It All Together

This lecture is one long arc from a definition to a deployed system, and every part feeds the next. We started in Part I by making "correct" precise: a system satisfies a property only if all its traces do, one bad trace disproves, and properties split into safety ("nothing bad happens," broken by a finite trace) and liveness ("something good eventually happens," broken only by an infinite one). That single distinction was the key that unlocked everything afterward.

In Part II we met Paxos: three roles (proposer, acceptor, learner), two phases (prepare, accept), and one quietly brilliant rule — a proposer must carry forward the highest already-accepted value it sees in the promises. Part III revealed why that rule makes Paxos safe: any two majorities overlap, so a new proposer always sees a chosen value and is forced to re-propose it — which is how the system can be unable to change its mind even though individual acceptors can, and even though no acceptor ever knows consensus was reached. Correctness as a global structural fact, observed by no one.

Then Part IV cashed in the safety/liveness lens from Part I: Paxos gives you agreement and validity (safety) for free but cannot guarantee termination (liveness) — and the livelock counterexample was, exactly as the definition demanded, an infinite trace. This isn't a Paxos flaw; the FLP theorem proves no asynchronous consensus protocol with a faulty process can have all three properties. Paxos's principled answer: never break safety, tolerate the rare liveness failure, and survive F failures with 2F+1 acceptors.

Finally, Parts V and VI turned one decision into a service. Run a Paxos instance per slot of a shared log, apply a slot once a majority agrees, and you have a replicated state machine that beats primary-backup on fault tolerance — no single point of failure, just "keep a majority alive." Multi-Paxos and leader-based Paxos then claw back the performance cost by skipping the prepare phase or fixing a leader, improving the common case while — crucially — never escaping FLP's worst case.

The Through-Line

Distributed agreement is governed by an unbreakable law (FLP), so every real system makes the same choice Paxos does: guard safety absolutely, and engineer liveness to be good-enough-in-practice. Everything from the shared log to Multi-Paxos to Raft is a variation on living gracefully within that constraint.
