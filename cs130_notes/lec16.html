Software Architecture: Quality Attributes & Architectural Styles

An interactive guide to Lecture 16 — what architecture really is, the drivers and quality attributes that shape it, the views we use to reason about it, and the classic styles that trade quality for quality.

Part I

What Is Software Architecture?

Why Are We Learning This?

Up to now this course has lived mostly at the level of code: classes, objects, design patterns between them. But a real system — a self-driving car, a flight-booking site, a robot — is far too big to hold in your head one class at a time. You need a way to step back and reason about the system as a whole: how its big pieces are structured, how they collaborate, and whether the system will actually be fast enough, reliable enough, and changeable enough once it is built. That zoomed-out picture is the software architecture.

The professor framed this as the highest-stakes decision-making in engineering. Architecture decisions are the ones that are brutally expensive to undo later. Get them wrong and, in safety-critical systems, the consequences can be lethal — a point we will see concretely when we reach the Toyota braking bug in Part V. So the question this whole lecture answers is: how do we make these big structural decisions deliberately, instead of by accident?

Where This Comes From

Software architecture was founded as an academic discipline by David Garlan and Mary Shaw at Carnegie Mellon — the professor's own PhD advisor was David Garlan. Many of the framings in this lecture (load-bearing walls, "constraints give you power") come straight from how Garlan teaches it.

Two Definitions of Architecture

There isn't one universally agreed definition. The lecture contrasted two influential schools.

Key Concept — The CMU (Garlan & Shaw) Definition

The software architecture of a computing system is the set of structures needed to reason about the system, comprising software elements, relations among them, and properties of both.

The phrase that does all the work here is "needed to reason about the system." Architecture, in this view, is an artifact — something concrete you can study, model, and analyze. It is a snapshot of the system that lets you predict things: how it will behave in a given environment, how maintainable it is, whether it will hit its performance targets. Anything that helps you make those predictions belongs in the architecture; anything that doesn't, doesn't.

Note the deliberate breadth: "elements and relations." This is not just a class diagram. It explicitly includes the collaboration and behavior between elements. The professor stressed that thinking of architecture as purely structural (just boxes and lines, a static class diagram) is a common and misguided view.

Key Concept — The USC / UC Irvine Definition

The software architecture is the set of all design decisions that are important — including the rationale behind them.

The contrast is the heart of this section. The CMU definition treats architecture as a snapshot: here is what we ended up with, modeled so we can analyze it. The USC/UC Irvine definition treats it as a history: here are the decisions we made and why. The professor offered a memorable analogy — the decision-based view is more like a Git log of the system (an evolving record of choices and rationale) than a single static photograph.

The Big Idea

We don't capture architecture just to document it. We capture it so we can reason about the implications of our decisions before we pay for them in code. Architecture is a prediction tool.

Part II

Architectural Drivers — The Inputs to Design

What Drives a Design Decision?

Before you can design an architecture you need to know what is pushing on it. Those forces are called architectural drivers.

Key Concept — Architectural Drivers

Also called architecturally significant requirements. They are the inputs to the design process — the wants and needs that, if they changed, would change which design you pick. A driver is anything that is both high importance and high difficulty.

That "if it changed, your decision would change" test is the operational definition. Drivers aren't every requirement — they are the small set of high-impact, hard-to-satisfy requirements that genuinely steer the shape of the system. They come in three flavors, and telling them apart is exam-worthy.

Requirements vs. Constraints vs. Quality Attributes

1. Functional Requirements

These say what the system should do. At architecture time we deliberately keep them very high level — not detailed user stories, but top-level capabilities. The professor's example: "the system shall allow users to book flights." That is the architectural altitude; the full user story about booking a flight comes later, in detailed design.

2. Constraints

Key Concept — Constraint

The difference between a requirement and a constraint: a requirement tells you what to do; a constraint has already made a decision for you. Constraints don't determine what you build — they shrink the design space by removing options.

Constraints can be technical or business in nature. Examples from lecture: "the whole organization uses MySQL, so we must use MySQL"; "the team only knows C++, so the design must be in C++"; "development cost may not exceed $100 million." None of these tell you what to build — each one just removes choices you would otherwise have had.

3. Quality Attributes

These are the extra-functional requirements — Part III is devoted entirely to them. The professor pointedly prefers "extra-functional" over the common term "non-functional," and the reasoning is worth absorbing now because it reframes the whole topic.

Why "Non-Functional" Is the Wrong Word

"Non-functional" makes it sound like quality attributes have nothing to do with functionality. They do! A quality attribute is built on top of a functional requirement by adding a measure to it. You take "the system shall handle a request" and add "…and reply within 2 seconds." The function is still there; you've layered a quality measure onto it. So they are additions to functionality, not the opposite of it — hence "extra-functional."

Part III

Quality Attributes — The Load-Bearing Walls

Why Quality Attributes Deserve Special Care

Here is the single most important metaphor in the lecture, and it comes from David Garlan. Imagine this lecture hall holds 360 students, and next quarter you need it to hold 500. What can you change? Some walls are flimsy "paper walls" you can tear down and move to expand the room. But other walls — the load-bearing walls — hold up the whole building. Move one of those and the entire structure collapses.

The Big Idea

Quality attributes are the load-bearing walls of software. If you don't plan for them early, it is incredibly hard to add them later — often you must tear the whole system down and rebuild from scratch. They define the limits of future change, so they deserve disproportionate design attention up front. Detailed features, by contrast, are the furniture: easy to rearrange later.

Key Concept — Quality Attribute

A measurable characteristic of "goodness." Where a function describes what the system does, a quality attribute describes how well it does it. Examples: extensibility, availability, security, performance, robustness, interoperability, testability, modifiability.

The Quality Attribute Catalog

The lecture walked through several of the most important quality attributes. For each, note the definition and the measure — because a quality attribute without a measure is nearly useless.

1

Modifiability — the ease with which a class of changes can be made. Measure: the cost of change (e.g., development time: "adding a feature of that type takes one week"), or scope ("and we don't have to touch that subsystem").

2

Extensibility — a subtype of modifiability: the degree to which a system minimizes the effort and error-proneness of adding new features. The more modules you must change to add a feature, the less extensible (and the more likely you introduce bugs).

3

Testability — the ease with which software can be made to demonstrate its faults through (typically execution-based) testing. The professor flagged this as frequently neglected, which is why a whole lecture (the following Tuesday) is devoted to it.

4

Availability — the ability to mask or repair faults. Measures: percentage uptime, mean time to repair (MTTR), mean time between failures (MTBF). Usually expressed as the number of nines: 99%, 99.9%, 99.99%. Each extra nine roughly doubles the cost.

5

Performance — the ability to meet timing requirements under load. Measures: average/min/max latency, throughput, jitter, deadline-miss rate. Crucially, you don't demand that everything finish in 10 s — you specify, for a particular type of request, what the response time must be. This is exactly why "extra-functional" beats "non-functional."

Writing a Good Quality Attribute Requirement

The professor ran a live workshop refining one requirement. Watch the requirement get sharper at each step — this is the skill the homework will test.

1

"The system should be extensible."  ❌ Nobody liked this. It is unmeasurable and unfalsifiable — extensible how, for whom, with respect to what change?

2

"Adding new sensors should be easy."  ⚠️ Better — now there's a change scenario — but "easy" is still vague. Easy for whom? Measured how?

3

"Adding new depth sensors should not require changing components that process depth images."  ✅ Now we're specific: a concrete change (new depth sensors) and a concrete measure of "easy" (no changes to the depth-image-processing components).

4

"Adding new depth-processing functionality should minimize changes to existing pre-processing components."  ✅ The class debated this one. The word "minimize" is a feature, not a bug: it specifies a direction to optimize.

Why "Minimize / Maximize" Is Legitimate

Quality attributes almost always trade off against each other — you usually can't get everything right at once. If every requirement is a hard binary threshold, then inside that space of trade-offs you have no way to say which solution you'd prefer. Soft goals ("minimize changes," "maximize throughput") give you a direction to optimize when there's no single hard threshold to hit. Specify a hard threshold when you have one; otherwise a direction plus a priority is perfectly good.

Exam Tip — The Two-Part Recipe

CMU's master's course uses a six-part quality-attribute scenario. The professor explicitly does not require all six here. Remember just two parts: the scenario (the situation / stimulus) and the measure (the response measure). A good QA requirement = a concrete scenario + a concrete measure.

Attribute-Driven Design (ADD)

How do you actually produce an architecture when requirements conflict and many views matter? The Software Engineering Institute at CMU developed Attribute-Driven Design, an iterative process. The key mindset shift: you don't try to conjure a perfect architecture from nothing — you improve an existing one, one quality at a time.

1

Pick an iteration goal: choose a quality attribute to improve this round (e.g., "improve maintainability a bit").

2

Choose a part to refine and sketch a candidate solution — the design decisions that would improve that quality for that part of the system.

3

Analyze the side effects: improving maintainability may have cost you performance, or raised development cost. So re-examine all the quality attributes that matter for that part.

4

Check & iterate: did you actually improve the system, or just change it? If needed, loop again with a new goal.

Part IV

Architectural Views — Different Lenses for Different Questions

Recall the CMU definition: architecture is the set of structures that let us reason about properties. No single diagram captures everything. So we draw multiple views, each a different perspective that supports a different kind of analysis. If you only ever draw the code view, you simply cannot reason about whole classes of properties.

Builds On Part III

Each view is good at reasoning about certain quality attributes and blind to others. Keep the quality-attribute catalog in mind as we go — the punchline is that the view you draw determines the questions you're allowed to ask.

The Module View — Code Before You Run It

Key Concept — Module View

A module style / module view describes the system as pieces of code. The elements are modules (packages, classes, source files); the relations are things like "imports," "depends on," "uses." This is what you see in your IDE — the system before you compile it.

The module view lets you reason about code-level design decisions: maintainability, changeability, and especially information hiding — you can literally see the modularization and the interfaces between modules. Class diagrams and entity-relationship diagrams (tables, relations, attributes, an underlined primary key — the data view) live in this family.

The Component-and-Connector View — Code While It Runs

Key Concept — Component

In software architecture a component is an independently deployable runtime unit — something that can actually execute (a process, an executable). It is not a piece of code. C++ source cannot be executed; a compiled, running executable can. Components run for a prolonged period; they are not functions that instantly return, and they are not packages of code.

The component-and-connector (C&C) view is the runtime view: elements are executing components, and connectors are runtime communication channels (exchanging messages). A client component and a server component interact through interfaces.

The Distinction Students Always Miss

The module view and the C&C view are not isomorphic. A client and a server might both be written in JavaScript and both import the same shared code library — so in the module view that library is one module, but when you ship the system it gets compiled into both running components. The mapping from modules to components is not one-to-one. "Library" is an overloaded term: a code library you import (module view) is different from a running library/server component (C&C view).

A C&C view shows that B receives from A1 and A2 and sends to C — but not whether a message from A1 causes a message to C. The view shows existing channels, not causal flow or ordering.
What a C&C View Can and Cannot Tell You

Think of a graph of students who sometimes talk to each other. You can see which channels exist, but not who initiates a conversation or how information actually propagates through the graph. In the figure above, you cannot tell whether B's messages to C are causally linked to what it received from A1 versus A2. The C&C view captures established communication channels — static interactions — not ordering or causality. For that you need the behavioral view.

Data and Behavioral Views

The data view (entity-relationship diagrams) helps you reason about how you structure your database and tables. The behavioral view (sequence diagrams, state machines) describes interactions over time, and is what you use to reason about ordering, messaging protocols, complex interactions, bottlenecks, and whether you can meet performance properties. You can even find bugs in component state machines here.

A View Limits Your Claims

A module view describes the system before it is compiled, so you cannot make claims about availability from it — the top and bottom layers of a module diagram aren't independently "up" or "down." Availability is a runtime property, judged from the C&C or dynamic views. This rule (the view determines which properties you may reason about) returns with force in the layered style.

Part V

Architectural Styles — Design Patterns at the System Scale

Constraints Give You Power

You already learned the Gang of Four design patterns — reusable solutions to recurring problems between classes and objects. An architectural style is the same idea scaled up: a recurring solution at the level of the whole architecture, the dominant shape of a system.

Key Concept — Architectural Style

A reusable pattern at architectural scale, defined by a set of elements, the relations among them, and — crucially — a set of constraints on those elements and relations. Unlike GoF patterns (which assume an object-oriented language), architectural styles assume no particular programming paradigm, so they translate to any language used to build multi-component systems.

The Big Idea — Constraints Create Power

The constraints are not red tape — they are the source of the style's value. Because you constrain a style, you can predict its quality attributes. Without constraints you could predict nothing. Garlan's analogy: putting the men's room always on the left and the women's on the right is a constraint on the building's architect — and precisely because of that constraint you can navigate any floor without looking around. Constraints buy you predictability.

Styles split along the same view divide from Part IV: pipes & filters and publish–subscribe are component-and-connector styles (about running components), while the layered style is a module style (about code). Keep track of which world each one lives in.

Pipes & Filters (a C&C style)

One of the oldest styles. Filters are components that read and process data from input streams; pipes are connectors that buffer the data and preserve its order, forwarding it to another component's input port. A pipe is not a collection of filters — it is the channel between them. (A Unix pipe is exactly this: it buffers a text stream and forwards it to the next program.)

# The canonical pipes & filters system: a Unix shell pipeline
cat access.log | grep "ERROR" | sort | uniq -c | sort -rn
#  filter      filter        filter  filter      filter
#         pipe        pipe       pipe       pipe

The professor's examples of pipes-and-filters systems: image-processing pipelines (brightness → noise reduction → edge detection → coloring), and all Unix shell scripts — they take text streams in and produce text streams out, and the whole point is that you can recombine them freely.

The Constraints

1

Strict independence: filters must not share state or memory with other filters; they interact only through pipes.

2

Agnosticism: a filter must not know the identity of its upstream or downstream neighbor. An edge-detection filter cannot assume a noise-reduction filter ran before it. This is information hiding applied to wiring — the order in which filters connect is hidden from the filters themselves.

3

Acyclic pathways: data flows from a source to a sink; genuine cycles break the style.

Promoted Quality Attributes

From those constraints, the predictions follow almost mechanically:

  • Reusability (the most interesting one): because filters make no assumptions about where data comes from and all share one interface, they slot into countless contexts. grep is endlessly reusable for exactly this reason. Fewer assumptions = more reuse.
  • Extensibility & modifiability: changing the connections is trivial since everything speaks the same data format. (Note: changing the buffer implementation is not easy — the modifiability is in the wiring, not the pipes' internals.)
  • Testability: each filter can be tested in isolation. High modularity tends to bring testability along with reusability and modifiability.
  • Throughput: independent filters can be deployed on separate processors and literally run in parallel (image pipelines across GPU cores; Unix pipes across cores).

Inhibited Quality Attributes

What Pipes & Filters Costs You

Interactivity / usability suffer — it's hard to feed user events into a pipeline or do incremental display updates and complex control. And while throughput is high, latency is hurt: because every stage must speak the unified pipe format, data is repeatedly encoded and decoded at each boundary (e.g., translating every image into the pipe's bitmap format), adding translation overhead end to end.

The Layered Style (a module style)

Now we switch worlds — into modules, i.e. code. A layer is a module that cohesively groups functionality at one level of abstraction. The relation is "allowed to use." In the strict layered style, every piece of software lives in exactly one layer, and a layer may use only modules in the same layer or the layer directly below it — call down by one, never up, and never skipping.

A strict layered OS architecture. Each layer may only call the layer directly below. Swap the hardware-abstraction layer and the layers above keep working on new hardware.

Familiar examples: the TCP/IP stack (link → internet → transport → application) and an operating system (hardware abstraction → memory management → I/O / file management → UI). Each layer owns one responsibility and can be swapped out interchangeably.

Promoted & Inhibited Qualities

  • Reusability & portability: lower layers make fewer assumptions and can be reused; isolating platform-specific code in the bottom layers means you swap the hardware-abstraction layer for new hardware and everything above still runs.
  • Modifiability: a layer can be replaced without disturbing the others — the whole reason OSes are built this way.
  • Performance is inhibited: a request is forced to traverse every intermediate layer, each packing and unpacking data and adding to the stack, even when that work isn't strictly necessary.
The Escape Hatch — Layer Bridging

Layer bridging relaxes the strict rule and permits skipping layers to gain performance — at the cost of some modifiability (more dependencies make the architecture harder to change).

Subtle but Exam-Critical

The layered style is a module style, so it describes the system before compilation. That means you cannot conclude anything about availability from it — "if the bottom layer breaks, the whole thing is down" is a runtime claim a module view can't make. You'd only get availability if your C&C architecture also happened to be layered. This is the Part IV rule biting again: module views can only support claims about properties visible in a module view.

Publish–Subscribe (a C&C style)

Back to runtime components. Publish–subscribe is the architectural-scale cousin of the Observer design pattern. Some components (publishers) produce events; others (subscribers) consume them. Instead of wiring publishers directly to subscribers, you introduce a topic — a named channel. Publishers send events to a topic; subscribers register on that topic and get notified whenever something is published to it.

# ROS (Robot Operating System) — a publish/subscribe framework
pub = node.create_publisher(Image, "depth_camera")   # topic = a string!
sub = node.create_subscription(Image, "depth_camera", on_frame)
# publisher and subscriber never reference each other directly

The Constraints

1

Publisher ignorance: publishers must not rely on the presence or identity of any subscriber. If the system needs a subscriber to exist for correctness, it isn't strict pub-sub.

2

Subscriber ignorance: subscribers must not rely on which publisher the data came from.

3

One-way propagation: information flows only publisher → subscriber.

4

Asynchronous: sends are non-blocking. Pub-sub systems are inherently asynchronous.

Real-World Footgun — The Toyota Braking Bug

Robustness is pub-sub's weak spot: if you push the brake and nobody is listening to the brake event, nothing stops the car. Yet many cars use pub-sub over a shared data bus. About a decade ago, a Toyota bug let a music CD whose audio mimicked the structure of brake events inject those events onto the bus — effectively remote-braking the car. The professor's blunt warning: choosing the wrong architectural style can literally kill people. Quality-attribute trade-offs are not academic.

Pub-Sub's Other Weak Spots — Testability & Silent Failures

Because topics are just strings, a real bug in Apollo Auto (a major open-source self-driving framework, from the professor's PhD work) arose when someone renamed a topic from line_class to line inconsistently: some components published to the new name, others still subscribed to the old one. Such components silently fail — they just never receive data and never warn you. Combined with heavy asynchrony and execution paths that only run in emergencies, this makes pub-sub systems notoriously hard to test. Understandability also drops: without a live C&C model you can't easily see who receives what.

You Inherit the Framework's Trade-offs

Frameworks like ROS, MQTT, and Apache Kafka impose the publish–subscribe style on your data. By adopting one you automatically inherit its quality-attribute trade-offs — the extensibility and the testability/robustness pain. The lesson: know which style a framework forces on you, and plan for the downsides. Many can be mitigated with the right tooling, modeling, or QA process, but only if you know they're there.

Putting It All Together

This lecture is a single argument told in five moves. Architecture (Part I) exists so we can reason about and predict a system's properties before we pay for them in code — it's a prediction tool, whether you view it as a snapshot (CMU) or a log of decisions (UC Irvine).

To design one deliberately, you start from its drivers (Part II) — the high-impact, hard requirements that would change your design if they changed — carefully separating functional requirements, constraints (decisions already made for you), and quality attributes. Among these, the quality attributes (Part III) are the load-bearing walls: plan them late and you rebuild from scratch, so you specify them precisely (scenario + measure) and improve them iteratively via Attribute-Driven Design.

But you can't reason about a quality attribute without the right lens, which is why we draw multiple views (Part IV): the module view (code, pre-compile) for maintainability and information hiding; the component-and-connector view (running components) for message flow and deployment; the behavioral view for ordering and bottlenecks. The iron rule — a view limits the claims you can make — explains why availability is invisible in a module view.

Finally, architectural styles (Part V) close the loop: each style is a bundle of elements, relations, and constraints, and it's precisely the constraints that let us predict the quality attributes from Part III. Pipes & filters trades latency and interactivity for reusability and throughput; layered trades performance for portability and modifiability; pub-sub trades robustness and testability for extensibility. There is no free lunch — only trade-offs you choose on purpose.

The One Sentence to Remember

Constraints give you power: by restricting how elements may relate, an architectural style lets you predict the quality attributes you'll get — and every choice you make trades one quality for another.
