Engineering Software the Right Way

An interactive guide to Lecture 1 — why software engineering exists, how AI changes the game, how to learn for keeps, and the first real skill of the course: turning user needs into requirements and user stories.

Part I

Why Software Engineering Exists

You already know how to program. Previous courses taught you to build complex systems just by typing words — to make seemingly enormous changes with a few quick edits. In that sense you are a kind of wizard. But this course is not about casting more spells. It is about a harder question: how do you keep the magic from blowing up the castle?

The Complexity Problem

To feel the problem, consider a number the professor opened with. The source code of the Windows operating system is roughly 50 million lines of code. Printed on normal paper, that stack would stand about twice the height of Royce Hall. Reading it for eight hours a day at a typical developer's pace would take more than ten years — just to read it, not to understand it.

The Big Idea

No single person at Microsoft has ever read all of Windows. Yet thousands of engineers still make changes that mostly work. Software engineering is the set of disciplines and principles that let people build and modify systems far too large for any one mind to hold. That ability — not raw coding speed — is what this course trains.

This reframes the whole job. The hard part of large-scale software is not writing a clever line of code; it is coordinating change across a system nobody fully comprehends, without breaking the parts you never looked at. That is why "it compiles and runs on my machine" is never the finish line.

Learning From Other Engineers

Other engineering disciplines have faced this for centuries, so we can borrow their playbook. Take London's Tower Bridge. Before anyone laid a stone, the builders went through a disciplined sequence — and the same sequence maps almost exactly onto building software.

1

Identify the requirements. What must this bridge actually do? It shall connect the roads on both banks. It shall open to let tall ships pass. If you don't understand what you need, you can't build the right thing.

2

Capture quality attributes. Beyond raw function, there are non-functional requirements — what software architects call quality attributes. Durability: survive heavy use and weather for 100+ years. Performance: open in 15 minutes, not 2 hours. These constrain how good the result must be, not just what it does.

3

Design and analyze. Architects draw many blueprints and different views, then experts in statics analyze whether it is strong, durable, and fast enough — all before construction.

4

Construct so the pieces connect. Two halves built separately must meet in the middle. Making independently-built components agree on a shared interface is hard — and is exactly the same problem in software when a team builds parts in parallel.

How Software Often Gets Built Instead

In practice, developers grab the most reusable pieces, glue them together, drive a few cars over the bridge to see if it breaks, and when it breaks, patch around the broken part with a quick fix and pray. That is the anti-pattern this course exists to replace. The professor's running joke: build software like the collapsed early pyramids, and in 5,000 years people will still see your mistake and ask, "didn't they take 130?"

Requirements Before Code

The single most important lesson of the lecture is that you must consider requirements before implementation. The cautionary tale is the Berlin Brandenburg Airport: built decades late partly because it reused designs drawn up before the A380 even existed — and the new aircraft was too large for a normal airport layout. Some escalators didn't reach the ground; on the eve of opening they realized they had no roof and no compliant fire protection because the laws had changed. They had effectively skipped the requirements stage.

What Engineers Are Bad At

Engineers are usually excellent at building the system right. They are often poor at figuring out what is actually needed. Two design principles fall out of the airport disaster: design for change (the world and the customer's needs will shift, so plan for it) and think about what is truly needed before you build. Skipping requirements is how you discover, five years and millions of dollars later, that you built the wrong thing.

Part II

AI as an Amplifier

It is reasonable to ask, in this last quarter before many of you graduate: with AI agents reportedly building C compilers for $20,000, do these software-engineering skills even matter anymore? The lecture's answer is a careful yes — and the reasoning is worth internalizing because it shapes how you should spend your time.

Amplifier, Not Equalizer

The popular story is that "now everybody can code." Large-scale studies — notably from Google — tell a different story. The developers with the strongest software-engineering foundations receive the largest productivity boost from AI. Teams that already followed a well-established engineering process got a far bigger lift than teams that did not.

Key Concept — Amplifier vs. Equalizer

An equalizer would lift everyone to the same level, erasing the gap between novice and expert. An amplifier magnifies whatever is already there. The research finding is that AI is an amplifier: it multiplies the existing strengths of high-performing individuals and teams rather than flattening differences. Strong foundations get more valuable, not less.

The reason is intuitive once stated. An experienced engineer brings knowledge, anticipates edge cases, decomposes a problem, and judges trade-offs — and AI then executes faster on top of that judgment. An empty brain cannot do analysis, and an empty brain cannot do generation. You still need knowledge in your head to direct the tool.

The Skill-Formation Trap

There is a real danger, though, and it is specifically a danger to your learning. The research the professor surveyed is sobering:

What AI boostsWhat AI can quietly harm
Short-term performance and productivity on the taskLong-term learning and skill formation
How much output you produce in a sessionHow much you actually understand afterward
Perceived effectiveness ("this feels amazing")Measured later performance on non-AI exams
The Numbers

In a study of nearly 1,000 math students, unrestricted AI use during study time decreased later performance on non-AI exams by about 17% — roughly four letter grades — and impaired critical thinking. An Anthropic study found developers who leaned on AI to solve tasks completely skipped the skill-formation stage: they got the work done but never built the skill.

The Mitigation

These negative effects can be reduced with instructor-provided pedagogical restrictions. That is why the course supplies AI tutors with guardrails built in (a user-story tutor, a teamwork tutor, a low-hallucination notebook-style assistant). The course policy: you may use any AI tool, but you must disclose the exact prompt, the result, and how you critically engaged with it — and work that AI did for you earns no credit, because the course grades your learning, not the tool's output.

The healthy mental model the professor offers: do not be "that guy" who brags about an AI that finishes his workout faster. Use AI to do more, not to do the same work in less time — the same way professional developers ship more features in the same hours rather than the same features in fewer.

Part III

How to Actually Learn

Why This Section Is Here

This builds directly on Part II. If your goal is to maximize the skills AI will amplify, then how you study is no longer a soft topic — it is the lever that determines your future value. Much of this research comes from UCLA's own Bjork Learning and Forgetting Lab.

Desirable Difficulties

The counterintuitive core idea: the harder you make the learning on yourself, the more effective it usually is. Students tend to believe that if something felt easy to learn, they learned a lot. The opposite is closer to the truth. Strategies that make your brain sweat outperform comfortable ones.

Key Concept — Desirable Difficulty

A desirable difficulty is a study technique that is cognitively more demanding in the moment — and because it is more demanding, it produces stronger, more durable learning. The discomfort is the point, not a bug.

Interleaving

Instead of studying one topic in a long block, then another in a long block, interleave them: a while on topic A, a while on topic B, switch back. The forced context-switching feels worse — students rate themselves as learning less — yet they learn more. It is a textbook desirable difficulty.

Generation

Generation means trying to produce the answer before looking it up — e.g. guessing what a command does before checking the docs. In a world where every answer is a few keystrokes away, the productive move is to struggle in your own head first; that struggle is what makes the eventual answer stick.

Decomposition

Just as software engineering decomposes a big problem into smaller subproblems, learning decomposes a tough skill into smaller component skills you can practice individually. You rarely master the whole hard skill at once.

Retrieval and Spacing

Here the professor uses a vivid computer-science metaphor. You have learned that a read operation has no side effects. That may hold for digital systems — but it is false for your brain.

The Read Operation Has a Side Effect

Every time you retrieve a piece of knowledge from memory, your brain marks it as useful and pushes it deeper into long-term storage. So the read operation on your brain literally strengthens the memory. The practical consequence: quiz yourself instead of rereading your notes. Rereading is re-consuming; it is far weaker than trying to recall. Always run the read operation on your brain before the read operation on your notes.

Retrieval practice is this act of pulling knowledge out from memory. Spaced practice is spreading study sessions out over time instead of cramming. Cramming the night before a midterm is the single least effective strategy for any kind of long-term memory. Counterintuitively, the more you have partly forgotten before a session, the more powerful the retrieval is when you force yourself to recall it. So split studying into small weekly chunks across all your courses.

Concrete Habits the Course Bakes In

The exit ticket at the end of every lecture (summarize what you learned, answer an application question, list what confused you) is retrieval practice — and you are told not to look at your notes while doing it, because that would skip the read-from-brain step. The study gym, quizzes, and flashcards exist so you can space and retrieve all quarter long. Ten minutes of summarizing from memory beats re-watching the whole lecture recording tomorrow.

Part IV

Requirements vs. Design

Where We Are Now

Part I established that you must consider requirements before implementation. This part makes that precise: it defines what a requirement actually is, how it differs from a design decision, and where both fit in the engineering process. This is the first hard technical distinction of the course — and the lecture flagged that some students find it instantly intuitive while others need time.

The What vs. The How

Key Concept — Requirement vs. Design

A requirement describes what the system must do to deliver value to a user — and deliberately leaves how open. A design decision chooses how to implement that requirement. Requirements are about the what; design is about the how.

Two worked examples from the lecture make the line concrete:

Requirement (the What)Design Decision (the How)
The system must calculate and display the shortest route between the user's location and the destination.Use Dijkstra's algorithm; represent the map as a weighted graph.
The system must ensure only registered, verified users can access the financial dashboard.Implement it with OAuth 2.0 or JSON Web Tokens.

Notice the requirement names the user value without committing to any mechanism. Why insist on keeping the "how" out?

1

Prevent premature constraints. If you bake "use HTTPS" or "use JWTs" into a requirement, you shrink the design space and may shut out a better solution before anyone evaluates it.

2

Preserve flexibility and agility. Design decisions are supposed to change frequently. Keeping requirements separate means you always know what the user truly needs versus what was just one implementation choice — so you can swap the how without losing the what.

3

Put decisions in the right hands. Stating requirements as pure needs lets the right people make the design choices, and lets you derive tests: a good requirement tells you directly how to check that you built the right system.

Phases and the Waterfall

Software engineering mirrors civil engineering's activities. There are four core phases:

The core engineering activities: requirements → design → development → testing → delivery.

The early-days approach was the waterfall model: gather all requirements, then do all design, then all development, then all testing, then deploy — strictly in sequence for the whole system. The flaw: five years later the customer sees the result and says it is not what they meant. Misunderstandings surface far too late.

We Keep the Phases, Drop the Strict Sequence

Modern software is built in increments, not one giant waterfall. But you still pass through every activity for each increment. The takeaway: never skip the requirements stage, never skip design, and never deploy without testing. When CrowdStrike skipped adequate testing, a faulty update took much of the world economy offline for a day. The phases are non-negotiable; only their rigid ordering is relaxed.

Part V

User Stories

So requirements should capture pure user value. But how do you write one down in practice? In real-world agile projects, the most common format is the user story — an informal, lightweight description of a need from a user's point of view.

The Three-Part Framework

Key Concept — The User Story Template

Every user story follows the same three-part shape:

As a <user group>, I want to <perform an action>, so that <I achieve a goal>.

Each part does a specific job:

1

"As a <user group>" identifies who receives the value. Most systems have several user groups — a learning platform like Bruin Learn serves students, professors, readers, and administrators, each with different needs. Never just write "as a user"; name the role.

2

"I want to <action>" is where the actual requirement lives. This is the core of the story.

3

"so that <goal>" is context, not the requirement itself. It tells engineers why this delivers value, so they can make choices that maximize that value for the customer.

User Story# Example: the airport gangway need
As an airline,
I want to connect gangways from the airport to my A380 planes,
so that I can offer long-distance flights with many passengers.
A User Story Is a Placeholder for a Conversation

The textbook definition: a user story is "a placeholder for a conversation." It is intentionally kept somewhat vague and abstract so that you then talk to the real customer, product manager, or product owner to fill in the details. The story starts the dialogue; it does not end it.

Acceptance Criteria

Vague is fine for starting a conversation, but eventually you need a precise way to decide whether a proposed solution actually satisfies the need. That precision comes from acceptance criteria, written in a given–when–then format.

Key Concept — Given / When / Then

Each acceptance criterion has three parts: Given a precondition, When an action occurs, Then a post-condition must hold. A user story's full criteria are a list of these triplets, logically combined with AND — a solution must satisfy all of them.

Take the story: "As a student, I want to update my homework submission before the deadline, so that I can change it if I find a mistake." Watch how one criterion alone is not enough.

1

Given the student submitted the assignment and the current time is before the deadline, when the student navigates to the submission page, then there should be an Update button.

2

Is that enough? No — it only says a button exists, not what it does. So add: Given the current time is before the deadline, when the student clicks Update and selects a new file, then the submission record should show the new file.

3

You can keep going — e.g. given the time is after the deadline, then the button should not appear (a need from the instructor, not the student). But not forever: you stay within the space of requirements, never drifting into design. Add enough criteria that the most important behaviors a developer might miss are pinned down and testable.

Existence Is Not Value

A criterion that only guarantees a button exists does not capture the real need — the button has to actually do the thing. The lecture returns to this repeatedly: criteria must define the valuable behavior, because tests are derived from them. If the criteria accept a useless solution, your tests will pass on a system that doesn't help anyone.

Part VI

The INVEST Principle

How do you know whether a user story is any good? Software engineering loves acronyms, and the one for quality user stories is INVEST. Each letter is a property a well-formed story should have.

Six Letters

LetterPropertyWhat it means
IIndependentStories can be defined, scheduled, and implemented in any order. Overlap makes estimation and scheduling hard — and dependence can only be judged relative to a specific other story in the backlog.
NNegotiableIt stays in the space of requirements (the what), not design decisions (the how). Details are open for conversation.
VValuableIt delivers value to a real user group — not to the developer. "As a developer, I want clean code" is not a user story.
EEstimableYou can estimate how long it takes, which requires a clear, well-defined scope. Ambiguous scope makes estimation impossible.
SSmallA small chunk of work, ideally fitting one iteration. Make it as small as possible while still valuable — note the built-in tension with V.
TTestableYou can objectively decide yes/no whether every acceptance criterion is met. "High accuracy" or "looks modern" is not objectively testable.
Small and Valuable Pull Against Each Other

Adding more to a story makes it more valuable but also larger. So the goal is to split stories into the smallest pieces that are still valuable. And "small" is context-dependent: a story counts as small not because it objectively takes little time, but because you cannot split it into a smaller story that is still valuable on its own.

Why User Stories Are Worth the Effort

Done well, user stories are user-centric (they force you to think about real needs), flexible and agile (edit, reorder, or delete them freely), and they give you traceability: a pull request can say "this implements user story X," so later you can trace exactly which code satisfies which need and why it was built that way.

Spotting Violations

The exam-style skill is recognizing which INVEST letter a bad story breaks. Here are the lecture's worked examples — these came from real 35L midterm questions.

1

"As a student, I want direct integration with Piazza, Gradescope, Google Drive, and Zoom…"  Violates Independent / Small. Four platforms are bundled into one story; integrating Piazza alone is already valuable, so it can and should be split into smaller stories that each still deliver value.

2

"As a student, I want the website to support HTTPS so my data is safe."  Violates Negotiable. HTTPS is a design decision, not a requirement. The real requirement is "I want my data not readable by anyone but authorized users" — leaving the protocol open.

3

"As a student, I want to take CS 130 in spring… then there should be an Enroll button."  Violates Valuable. An Enroll button on its own delivers no real value — it doesn't actually help the student enroll. (Note: the button criterion is testable, so testability is not the issue here.) The story needs more criteria capturing the genuine need.

4

"As a TA, I want scanned characters recognized automatically at high accuracy…"  Violates Testable (and therefore Estimable). "High accuracy" is hopelessly ambiguous — 50%? 90%? 100%? — and it does not even specify which character sets. Because success is undefined, you cannot test it, and the effort could range from importing a library to a decade of PhD research, so you cannot estimate it either.

Two Exam Traps the Professor Flagged

Independent is relative. You can only call a story non-independent by naming the specific other story it depends on, within a given backlog. If no such story exists in the set, you cannot claim a dependence. We judge by the acceptance criteria. The "I want" body sets context, but the actual scope is defined by the acceptance criteria — a story can name a great goal yet still fail Valuable if its criteria accept a solution that doesn't meet the real need.

Estimable and Testable Often Travel Together

When success criteria are vague, the story is usually not testable and not estimable — different interpretations ("high accuracy" = 50% vs. 100%) imply wildly different amounts of work. They don't always coincide, though: "I want a modern-looking page" is hard to test but often still estimable, because any reasonable interpretation of "modern" takes roughly the same effort to build.

Putting It All Together

This first lecture is really one argument told from several angles. It opens with a problem of scale: real systems are too large for anyone to fully understand, so we need engineering principles rather than heroics (Part I). It then asks whether AI makes those principles obsolete and answers no — AI is an amplifier of existing skill, which raises the stakes on building strong foundations now (Part II). That motivates a detour into how to learn, because if skill is the lever, then desirable difficulties, retrieval, and spacing are how you grow it (Part III).

The back half delivers the lecture's first concrete engineering technique. The airport disaster and the bridge both point to the same rule: get the requirements right before you build. Part IV sharpens that into the precise distinction between what (requirements) and how (design), and places both inside the four engineering phases. Part V gives you the practical tool for writing a requirement down — the three-part user story plus given–when–then acceptance criteria. Part VI gives you the quality bar for those stories, INVEST, and the diagnostic skill of naming exactly which letter a weak story breaks.

Notice how the threads connect. "Requirements before code" (Part I) is what makes user stories matter (Part V). The requirement-vs-design line (Part IV) is literally the N in INVEST (Part VI). And the learning strategies (Part III) are how you will master all of it: quiz yourself on these definitions from memory rather than rereading them, and space that practice across the quarter.

The One-Sentence Takeaway

Software engineering is the disciplined practice of figuring out what is truly needed before deciding how to build it — and a good user story, validated against INVEST, is the first tool you have for writing that "what" down precisely enough to test.
