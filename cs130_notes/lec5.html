Information Hiding & the Art of Modular Design

An interactive guide to Lecture 5 — why software became humanity's hardest engineering problem, and how hiding the right decisions behind the right interfaces is what keeps 150-million-line systems buildable.

Part I

The Software Crisis — Why Any of This Matters

Why Are We Learning This?

In the professor's words, today's topic is, in his personal opinion, the most important topic in all of software engineering. Some courses squeeze it into a ten-minute aside. This course gives it an entire lecture, because every other technique you will learn — design patterns, testing strategy, architecture — is ultimately in service of the one problem this lecture names.

That problem is complexity. Software is not hard because computers are hard; it is hard because programs grow larger than any single human brain can hold. The whole discipline of software engineering exists to answer one question: how do we keep building systems that are far too big for any one person to understand?

The story starts in the 1960s. During NASA's Apollo program, Margaret Hamilton led the flight software effort. She noticed something startling: the complexity of the software had come to exceed the complexity of the hardware of the entire mission. She coined the very term "software engineering" to argue that this was a new engineering discipline, unlike mechanical or electrical engineering before it.

Key Term — The Software Crisis

The software crisis is the observation, first formalized at a 1968 NATO conference, that software is routinely delivered late, fails to meet its specification, and exceeds its estimated cost. The crisis was declared resolved by no one — the professor notes it is "kind of still true today."

That 1968 NATO conference gathered, in one room, an astonishing concentration of future Turing Award winners — Edsger Dijkstra, Alan Perlis (the very first laureate), Tony Hoare, Niklaus Wirth, Peter Naur, Brian Randell. The greatest minds in computing agreed software complexity was an existential problem. And it has only grown since.

The Numbers Keep Climbing

Track the lines of code in safety-critical systems over the decades and the growth is staggering:

SystemApprox. Lines of Code
Apollo guidance computer~145,000
Space Shuttle~500,000
F-22 fighter jet~1.7 million
Boeing 787 Dreamliner~6.5 million
A modern car~100–150 million

A modern car carries roughly three orders of magnitude more code than the Apollo mission that first triggered the crisis. No single person can understand a 150-million-line system. So how do we build them at all?

The Big Idea

We build incomprehensibly large systems by designing them so that you can understand each module individually, without looking inside the other modules. Human IQ has been flat since the 1960s while system complexity has grown exponentially — the only way to close that gap is structure, not smarter people.

The Quadratic Problem of Connections

Why does complexity grow so much faster than size? Because the trouble is not the number of modules — it is the number of connections between them. The possible connections between nodes in a graph grow quadratically.

Double the modules (4 → 8) and the possible connections more than quadruple (6 → 28). Complexity rides on the edges, not the nodes.

The number of possible pairwise connections among n modules is n(n-1)/2. Four modules give six possible links; eight modules give twenty-eight. So every new module you add forces you to think about quadratically more potential connections. If you don't put abstractions in place to cut those links, each new feature becomes quadratically harder to add than the last.

The Cost of Change Rises Over Time

On any long-lived system, early features are easy to add and later ones are brutally hard — precisely because of this quadratic growth. The professor notes this is true for humans and for AI coding agents: vibe-coding the first few diagrams "just works," but adding constraints later "introduces new bugs that don't work."

The Big Ball of Mud

When complexity is left unmanaged, the result has a name: the big ball of mud — the modern term for the software crisis playing out in a single codebase.

Key Term — Big Ball of Mud

A big ball of mud is an architecture with no discernible structure: spaghetti code, hidden bugs everywhere, where a simple change request requires touching dozens of unrelated files. Its symptoms: an immense cost of change, very low understandability, painful onboarding, and extreme fragility.

The professor's example: a friend joined a large financial institution whose system had become a big ball of mud. Onboarding was nearly impossible — you could not understand any module in isolation, so you always had to read every other module too. The mental model required to hold the whole system in your head was simply too large for a human. And it was fragile: forget one place that needed to change, make the edit ad hoc, and the system filled up with bugs.

Part II

A Concrete Example — The Payment System

Where We're Headed

Part I told us what goes wrong (the big ball of mud) and why (quadratic connections). Now we work a single example end-to-end. The principle we are about to discover by hand — hide what is likely to change — is the spine of the entire rest of the lecture.

Code That Mentions PayPal Everywhere

Consider an online shop's payment system, written in Python. It has three services: an OrderService, a RefundService, and a WalletService. Each one takes an order and a PayPal account and does its job. Assume the functionality is perfectly correct — all error handling works, refunds work. The professor's question is deliberately not about functionality: where is the big ball of mud hiding in this code?

# Every service hard-codes the decision: "we use PayPal"
class OrderService:
    def checkout(self, order, paypal_account):
        paypal.authenticate(paypal_account)   # PayPal mentioned
        paypal.charge(order.total)            # PayPal mentioned

class RefundService:
    def refund(self, order, paypal_account):
        paypal.authenticate(paypal_account)   # PayPal mentioned
        paypal.refund(order.total)            # PayPal mentioned

class WalletService:
    def top_up(self, order, paypal_account):
        paypal.authenticate(paypal_account)   # PayPal mentioned
        paypal.create_payment_method(...)     # PayPal mentioned

The defect is structural, not behavioural. Every single module knows that PayPal exists. The word paypal is a literal scattered across all three services.

Now ask: what change is likely? Not some exotic quantum-crypto upgrade to PayPal's API. Something ordinary — a user wants to pay with Bitcoin, or Stripe, or WeChat Pay instead. The moment you support a second payment method, every red line that mentions PayPal in every service has to change. The decision "we use PayPal" has leaked into every module, so changing it means changing everything.

The Smell — A Decision That Leaked

When a single design decision (which payment provider) appears verbatim in many modules, that decision becomes impossible to change cheaply. The number of modules you must edit to make one change is a direct measure of how bad your modularization is.

The Fix — Hide PayPal Behind a Gateway

The cure is to create an abstraction for "a payment provider" and make PayPal just one instance of it. We define a PaymentGateway interface listing what every payment provider must be able to do — charge, refund, create_payment_method. Then PayPalGateway is one implementation behind that interface; StripeGateway or BitcoinGateway would be others.

# The stable contract — what any provider must do
class PaymentGateway(ABC):
    def charge(self, payment_details): ...
    def refund(self, payment_details): ...
    def create_payment_method(self, payment_details): ...

# The ONLY module that knows PayPal exists in this world
class PayPalGateway(PaymentGateway):
    def charge(self, payment_details):
        paypal.authenticate(payment_details.token)
        paypal.charge(payment_details.amount)
    # ...refund, create_payment_method...

# Services now speak only to the interface — no "paypal" anywhere
class OrderService:
    def checkout(self, order, gateway: PaymentGateway, details):
        gateway.charge(details)

Now the services say only gateway.charge(details). There is no paypal, no stripe, no bitcoin anywhere in them. The payment_details object carries the per-transaction specifics (which card, which account) that let the chosen gateway identify the user's selection.

The Aha Moment

The decision "we use PayPal" is now known by exactly one module — the PayPalGateway. As the professor put it: no other module even knows PayPal exists in this world. They don't even know about it — "and that's exactly how it should be." Adding Bitcoin means writing one new gateway and changing zero services.

After refactoring to a PaymentGateway interface, what makes the design better — even though functionality is unchanged?

Part III

The Information Hiding Principle

From Example to Principle

We discovered by hand that hiding the "which provider" decision in one module fixed everything. Now we generalize that intuition into a named, theory-backed principle — the one the professor calls the most important idea in software engineering.

First, What Is a Module?

Before we can talk about hiding things inside modules, we need to know what a module is.

Key Term — Module

A module is a self-contained piece of code that does one job, or a few closely related jobs. Crucially, "module" is hierarchical — it exists at many levels of abstraction at once.

From smallest to largest, every one of these is a module: a function → a class (many functions) → a directory (many classes) → a package (many directories) → a library (many packages). This nesting matters enormously later — when we talk about dependencies "between modules" vs "within a module," the answer always depends on which level of the hierarchy we're standing at.

Parnas's Criterion for Decomposing Systems

Twenty years after the software crisis was named, David Parnas answered the question we'd been circling: how do you decide what goes in which module? His landmark paper is titled, literally, "On the Criterion to Be Used in Decomposing Systems into Modules."

The naive criterion — split by processing steps, or by "things more than ten lines long," or by alphabetical order — is wrong. Parnas's criterion is:

The Information Hiding Principle

Begin with a list of difficult design decisions, or decisions which are likely to change. Then pick each module to hide one such decision from all the others. The only assumptions allowed to appear in the interfaces between modules are the ones not likely to change.

Read that twice — it is a deceptively simple sentence carrying very deep thought. The criterion for splitting a system into modules is not "what does this code do" but "what might change, and how do I quarantine that change to a single module?"

Study Note — A Paper to Actually Read

The professor singles out Parnas's paper as one of the very few software-engineering papers he recommends every undergraduate read. Students who think they already understand information hiding gain a much deeper grasp after reading it. (Aside: Parnas was, astonishingly, denied tenure at CMU — at the time, the idea that you must "care about the humans building the software" was not considered real science.)

A modularization is simply one way of carving a system into modules — and many different modularizations are possible. The "three services that each mention PayPal" version and the "PaymentGateway interface" version are two different modularizations of the same system. A good modularization is one built around the set of changes likely to happen, so that each such change stays localized to one module.

The Iceberg — Interface vs Implementation

Picture every module as an iceberg. The small tip above the water is the interface: what the module does, the stable contract other developers see. The vast bulk below the water is the implementation: how it does it — hidden, for nobody else to know.

A module as an iceberg: a tiny visible interface (the stable contract) above a huge hidden implementation (everything free to change).

The professor's favourite real-world analogy is the power outlet — in his view the single most complexity-hiding interface you will ever touch. Three holes in a wall. Behind them: the entire power-generation grid. You plug in your laptop without knowing or caring whether the electricity comes from solar, nuclear, or wind — and the grid can rebalance between those sources at runtime without you noticing. That is a perfect interface: trivial to use, hiding immense, changing complexity. Design every interface so that anyone looking at it knows exactly how to use it, with all the mutable complexity sealed behind.

What Counts as a "Secret" to Hide?

So which design decisions belong below the waterline? The lecture lists the classic secrets a module should hide:

Secret (hide it)Why it's likely to change
Data structures & formats — array vs binary tree vs hash tableEverything you learned about data structures must stay behind the interface; you may want to swap one for another
How data is stored on disk / table layoutDatabase schemas and storage formats evolve
Algorithms & implementation logicYou may switch from one algorithm to a faster one later
Library dependencies — Axios vs Fetch, OpenGL vs another rendererLibraries come and go; you want to switch without rewriting callers
Database technology — MongoDB vs MySQL vs Supabase"Database technologies come and go" — only one module should name the one you chose
Hide It in the Docs, Too — Not Just the Syntax

Algorithms are tricky: the syntactic interface usually hides them already, but documentation can leak them. If you write "this method uses merge sort," callers may overfit to that — relying on properties of merge sort — and break when you switch algorithms. Hide implementation details in the documentation as well as the signature.

The professor's rule of thumb from his other course: a student may use Supabase for a project only if exactly one module knows Supabase is being used. If every module knows, the answer is no — because then the database choice can never be swapped. You isolate it into a single data storage engine module that hides the specific technology and table format, and talks to the rest of the system through a small abstract interface that does not expose raw SQL.

It Is NOT Just "public vs private"

The professor's strongest warning: practitioners constantly reduce information hiding to "oh, that's just making fields private." It is not. Marking getters, setters, or methods private is data hiding, and it is not enough. Information hiding is about the structure of your modules — where you draw the boundaries — not merely the access modifiers on individual members. He says the name itself was Parnas's worst mistake; it should be called the "design-decision encapsulation principle."

Part IV

Contract or Secret? — Making the Call

The principle is only useful if you can apply it under pressure. The heart of the lecture was a series of judgment calls: for a given design decision shared between two modules, should it be a visible contract (part of the interface, both modules know it) or a hidden secret (one module hides it from the other)? There is a rule of thumb: if the modules genuinely cannot function without sharing the decision, it must be a contract; otherwise, hide it.

Worked Decisions From Class

1

Stateful vs stateless protocol, between client and server? → Contract. If the client doesn't know whether the protocol carries session state, it literally cannot know what messages to send. The server must tell the client. The two modules cannot communicate otherwise.

2

Network protocol, between the network component and the database component (both inside the server)? → Secret. Only the network component needs to know the network protocol. The database component has no need to know, so it should be hidden from it.

3

Relational vs NoSQL database? → Secret. Unanimous in class. The database technology is a textbook example of a decision likely to change; only the storage module should know it.

4

Server's language (Node.js / Java / Dart), between client and server? → Secret. Client and server talk over HTTP, a common language — they don't make direct function calls — so the client never needs to know the server's implementation language, and you should be free to rewrite the server later.

5

Implementation language, between the User class and the Payment class? → Contract. Here the modules are classes that call each other directly. The User class cannot invoke the Payment class without knowing its language. A design where it could would be terrible to write.

The Same Decision, Different Answers

Notice steps 4 and 5 ask the same question — "should the implementation language be hidden?" — but get opposite answers. Across the HTTP boundary (4) it's a secret; between two classes calling each other (5) it's a contract. The answer depends on how the two modules actually communicate, not on the decision itself.

The PayPal Dilemma — When Hiding Conflicts With Security

The richest case: should "we use PayPal" be a contract between client and server, or a secret one side hides? The class split nearly 50/50, and the professor loved it because both sides have real arguments.

You could hide it from the server entirely: handle all payment in the client, then have the client just send the server a message saying "payment handled." Now the server doesn't even know which payment methods exist — a beautifully hidden secret. But there's a fatal flaw:

Security Beats Hiding Here

Client-side code can always be manipulated. If the server has no idea what payments exist and just trusts a "payment handled" message, an attacker can reverse-engineer that message and send it without ever paying. The server has no way to verify. So here the payment decision must be a visible contract — the "cleaner" hidden design is defeated by security.

One student offered a brilliant resolution: introduce a third component — a trusted third-party payment gateway — known to both client and server. This is exactly Parnas's move of picking a new module to hide the decision when neither existing module can. It's also how real web services work: they delegate to an external payment provider. We arrive right back at the gateway architecture from Part II — now justified not just by changeability, but by security too.

Check Your Understanding

Correct — B. Because client and server speak HTTP rather than calling each other's functions, the client never needs the server's language — so it can be hidden. Two classes invoking each other's methods must share a language, making it an unavoidable contract. The lesson: whether a decision can be hidden depends on the communication mechanism between the modules. D is nonsense (HTTP is a protocol, not a programming language), and C contradicts the lecture.
Part V

Deep vs Shallow Modules

Why This Matters

Information hiding tells you what to hide. Module depth tells you how much you should be hiding relative to how much interface you expose — it's the quality metric for "is this a good module?"

Measuring a Module by Its Depth

Compare the size of a module's interface against the amount of code hidden behind it. This ratio names two kinds of module:

Key Terms — Deep vs Shallow Modules

A deep module hides a large amount of implementation behind a small interface. A shallow module hides only a little implementation behind a relatively large interface. The power outlet is the ideal deep module: three holes, an entire power grid behind them.

A deep module pays off: a small surface to learn, enormous complexity sealed away. A shallow module makes you learn a big interface for little benefit.

In general you should optimize for deep modules, because they best serve the whole purpose of information hiding: reducing the things a human must understand. A great example is an operating-system call like read or write — a tiny interface concealing a mountain of state exceptions and hardware-specific decisions.

When Is a Shallow Module Actually Right?

Deep is the default, but the professor pushed the class to find the exceptions — and there are legitimate ones:

1

Orchestration code meant to be read. A top-level module that wires together calls to many others, whose purpose is to let a human read the overall flow, should be simple and small on the surface even if it does little itself.

2

Adapters whose entire reason for existing is to hide one decision. The PaymentGateway from Part II is deliberately shallow — it has little code, just translating checkout / refund / payment requests into PayPal's specific API. But that shallowness is the point: the module exists only to hide the existence of PayPal. When the whole value of a module is the decision it conceals, shallow is correct.

Shallow Can Still Hide a Lot

A tempting "shallow" example — authentication, "just returns true/false" — turns out to be deep: behind that boolean hide two-factor flows, cryptographic details, and session state. Don't confuse a small interface with a small secret. The gateway adapter is genuinely shallow; authentication only looks shallow.

Part VI

Coupling, Cohesion & Hidden Dependencies

Connecting the Vocabulary

The next few concepts heavily overlap with information hiding — the professor warns they can feel like "the same idea over and over." That's fine: they give you precise vocabulary and new angles on the one core insight. Group together what changes together; separate what changes for different reasons.

Coupling vs Cohesion

Key Terms — Coupling & Cohesion

Coupling is the dependencies between modules. Cohesion is the dependencies within a module. The goal is low coupling and high cohesion: few links across module boundaries, tightly related contents inside each module.

Why is low coupling good but high cohesion also good, when both are "dependencies"? Because low coupling lets people work on each module without fear of breaking another's. And high cohesion means a module is a coherent set of related things — if the contents aren't related to each other, why are they one module? Split unrelated halves apart so each can be understood on its own. Group together what is semantically related.

The Change-Ripple Effect

High coupling almost always produces a ripple: you change one module, which forces a change in another, which forces another. Such architectures are hard to understand, hard to reuse, and hard to test in isolation. Low-coupling modules with small interfaces are easy to set up, test, and understand.

Remember that "between" vs "within" is relative to the hierarchy level. Functions within a class should call each other freely (high cohesion); functions across classes should have few dependencies. Classes within a package may depend on each other; classes across package boundaries should not. Keep dependencies local — don't let them cross the big branch boundaries of the hierarchy.

Syntactic vs Semantic Dependencies

Not all dependencies are visible. The lecture draws a sharp line between two kinds:

Syntactic dependencySemantic dependency
A can't compile/run without BA doesn't function correctly without B
Visible: a call, a class name, an interface referenceInvisible to the compiler: e.g. B assumes A pre-processed the data
Any static analyzer finds them automaticallyA static analyzer sees nothing — yet the bug is real
Errors caught by compiler/interpreterLogical/runtime errors, far harder to find

Two modules that both "use PayPal" may make no calls to each other — zero syntactic coupling — yet they share a semantic dependency: change the payment decision in one and forget the other, and you get a bug a static analyzer will never flag. One practical way to surface semantic dependencies: mine your commit history for files and methods that frequently change together.

Dependency Injection Doesn't Delete a Dependency

People often hide a syntactic dependency with dependency injection and declare it "gone." It is not gone — you've only made it less obvious. Aim to eliminate the dependency entirely; if you can't, make it explicit so others can plan for it, rather than burying it.

Related Design Principles

Information hiding sits among a family of overlapping principles, all pointing the same direction:

1

Separation of concerns. A slight generalization of information hiding: separate the implementation of different requirements (a "concern" is essentially a requirement). A 1972 result warns it's impossible to separate all concerns — you'll always have cross-cutting concerns that span module boundaries (the "tyranny of the dominant decomposition"). Information hiding is the special case where the concerns are design decisions likely to change.

2

The single choice principle. Whenever you have a set of alternatives — payment methods, customer types — only one module should know the exhaustive list. Replicate the list across many modules and every new alternative forces you to remember to edit all of them. In practice you implement this with polymorphism: an interface with many implementations, instantiated and injected in one place.

3

Change impact analysis. How do you grade a modularization? List the changes that could reasonably happen, prioritize them by likelihood, and evaluate the effort each would take against your current architecture. Pick the modularization that best absorbs the high-probability changes.

Hard to Change ≠ Hardly Changes

A subtle distinction the professor drilled: hide what is hard to change — not what hardly changes. The whole point is that something likely to change becomes hard to change if you don't isolate it, because the edit then sprawls across many modules. Isolate it now so the future change stays cheap.

Putting It All Together

This whole lecture is one argument, told in six movements. Part I named the disease: software outgrew the human brain in the 1960s and has only gotten worse, because complexity rides on the quadratically-growing connections between modules, producing the big ball of mud. Part II showed the disease in miniature — a payment system where every module mentioned PayPal — and discovered the cure by hand: hide the changeable decision behind one gateway.

Part III gave that cure its name and theory: Parnas's Information Hiding Principle — decompose a system by hiding each likely-to-change design decision inside its own module, exposing only stable assumptions in the interfaces. The iceberg and the power outlet make it concrete: a tiny visible contract over a vast hidden, mutable implementation. Part IV turned the principle into judgment — contract or secret? — and revealed the answer depends on how modules communicate and on cross-cutting forces like security, sometimes forcing you to invent a third module (the trusted payment gateway) to hold the secret.

Part V gave a quality metric — prefer deep modules that hide much behind little, accepting shallow ones only when hiding the decision is itself the whole point. And Part VI supplied the surrounding vocabulary — low coupling, high cohesion, the invisible semantic dependencies that bite you at runtime, and the sibling principles (separation of concerns, single choice, change impact analysis) that all restate the same truth in different words.

The One Sentence to Remember

Uncle Bob (Robert C. Martin) compresses the entire lecture: "Gather together the things that change for the same reasons. Separate those things that change for different reasons." Design for changeability, not just functionality — optimize code for readability over write-ability, because program comprehension eats 58% of a developer's time, and anything that lowers cognitive load lets you (and your AI coding agent) build more.
