Design Patterns: Strategy, Observer & Mediator

From "where do I put this code?" to a shared vocabulary of proven solutions — built on the design principle of hiding what is likely to change.

Part I

Information Hiding, Revisited

Today's lecture is about design patterns — reusable solutions to recurring design problems. But every one of those patterns exists to serve a single, deeper goal that we set up in the previous lecture: information hiding. Before we can appreciate why a pattern is good, we have to be crystal clear on what "good design" even means. So we begin by re-examining that principle on a concrete piece of code.

Key Concept — Information Hiding

The information hiding principle says: identify the design decisions that are likely to change, and hide each one inside a single module, behind an interface, so the rest of the system cannot see it. A module is an independent unit of work. The point of breaking a system into modules is parallel development — so a teammate can work against your interface without constantly messaging you about how it works inside.

Notice the emphasis: not every detail, but the details that are likely to change. A "secret" is just a design decision the rest of the system is shielded from. Good design asks two questions about every secret: Is it hidden? and — just as important — is it likely to change? A secret that will never change is fine to leave exposed. A secret that will change but is exposed everywhere is a future disaster.

Designing for Change: The Mortgage Calculator

The lecture opened with a mortgage-calculator program to critique. It has a calculatePayment method that takes a principal, an annual rate, and a number of years; a format method that rounds amounts; and a print method that writes the annual interest rate to System.out. The exercise: what secrets are hidden, and what secrets leak?

The way to answer is to imagine concrete change scenarios — realistic future requests — and ask, for each, how many modules you'd have to touch.

1

Change: payments become biweekly instead of monthly. Where does "monthly" live? It is baked into calculatePayment — but it also leaks into print, which writes the literal word "monthly" in a string. So this one change forces edits in two modules. That second leak is the bug in the design: the developer told the print function a domain fact (payment frequency) that it had no business knowing.

2

Change: interest compounds daily instead of monthly. Compounding is fully contained inside calculatePayment. Only one module changes. This secret is well hidden — good.

3

Change: round to a different precision. The "ignore the cents" rounding logic is buried inside the function. Hidden — good.

4

Smell: print reaches into domain logic. The print function depends on the domain calculation, when really printing should be the job of a separate class. A UI/output concern is entangled with business logic.

Lay this out as a change-impact matrix: one row per anticipated change, one column per module, marking every module that must change. The "biweekly payments" row lights up two cells (calculatePayment and print); the "daily compounding" row lights up only one. When a likely change forces edits across many modules, that is your signal to redesign so the change is localized — ideally to a single module, or at worst to the two sides of one client/server boundary.

The Big Idea

Before you write a single line, ask: what changes are likely, and which have a large impact? Good design is the art of arranging code so that the high-likelihood, high-impact changes touch as few modules as possible. This change-scenario mindset is the thread that runs through every design pattern in this course — and, the professor noted, it is a big part of why engineers earn a high salary.

The Getter Trap: Syntax Is Not Hiding

A tempting belief is that any use of private or any getter method "achieves" information hiding. Several quiz prompts from the lecture poke at this — and the lesson is that you must separate syntax from the design decision underneath it.

Common Mistake — "I made it private, so it's hidden"

Making a field private and adding a public getter that just returns it hides nothing. You have only prevented callers from setting the field. They still know the field exists, and they still know its data structure. You changed the syntax without hiding a single design decision.

Two more cases from the lecture sharpen the distinction:

  • Dynamic vs. static call (polymorphism). Using a polymorphic call instead of a hard-coded static call does not, by itself, help information hiding. The call still happens; a reader still knows it happens. Unless you've hidden the actual implementation behind an interface, swapping a static call for a dynamic one hides nothing.
  • Exposing the internal data structure through a getter. This one genuinely relates to information hiding — and it is a violation. If an interface returns the internal data structure (say, a raw int or the underlying collection), callers now depend on that structure. If you ever change it, they all break. A good interface hides what the data structure is.
The Real Test

To judge information hiding, don't ask "is anything private?" Ask: which design decisions are exposed, and are those decisions likely to change? An exposed decision that will never change is harmless. An exposed decision that will change is a liability — and the whole point of patterns is to wrap such decisions behind an interface before they bite you.

Part II

What Is a Design Pattern?

Now that we know good design means localizing likely changes, we need a vocabulary for the recurring shapes that good designs take. That vocabulary is design patterns. Why learn them? Because the problems they solve show up constantly — the professor estimated at least once a month in real development. Knowing the patterns is like carrying a toolbox: the more you have, the faster you recognize and solve the problem in front of you.

A Surprising Origin

Design patterns didn't start in computer science. The architect Christopher Alexander invented the idea for civil architecture — reusable templates for good buildings. His book was read mostly by computer scientists, who went crazy for it and invited him to software conferences. In his own field, almost no one noticed. He's a celebrity in software engineering and largely unknown in architecture.

Unpacking the Definition

Key Concept — Design Pattern

A design pattern is a common, acceptable solution to a recurring problem that arises in a specific context.

Every word in that sentence is load-bearing. The lecture untangled it:

1

Common — it appears in many places. A brilliant solution that exists only in your code isn't a pattern; that's just genius. A pattern earns the name by recurring across many systems.

2

Acceptable — generally good, but not always the right choice. Patterns have drawbacks. You decide case by case whether to apply one. They are better than the alternatives on average, not universally optimal.

3

Recurring problem — not a one-off, but something you'll meet over and over. This is exactly why studying them in college pays off: you're pre-loading solutions to problems you're guaranteed to hit.

4

Specific context — a pattern must be tailored to your situation. It is not copy-paste code from Wikipedia. After you adopt a pattern, you still make additional design decisions to fit it.

Two Mistakes Students Make

(1) Believing code quality is measured by how many patterns you used. It is not — naming more patterns in your final presentation does not earn a better grade, and over-applying patterns makes a mess. (2) Treating a pattern as a copy-paste code template. A pattern is an idea you adapt, not a snippet you transplant.

The Big Idea — A Shared Language

The deepest value of patterns is communication. When you tell a teammate "let's use the Observer here," anyone who has studied the pattern instantly knows the whole structure — no UML diagrams, no hundred-line examples needed. It is a compressed shared vocabulary for design. And the same compression now works on your AI coding agent: "use the Strategy pattern for this" conveys a precise design in four words, because the model has seen these patterns a million times in its training data.

How Patterns Are Described

Patterns are written up in a standard template. Knowing the template tells you what to look for — and what to memorize — for each pattern:

SectionWhat it tells you
Name & classificationThe handle (e.g., Observer), and its family: creational, structural, or behavioral. Some patterns have aliases — Observer is also called Listener.
IntentThe whole point: which changes the pattern is designed to absorb.
StructureClass diagrams showing the roles and relationships.
ParticipantsThe roles the pattern adds to your classes (e.g., Context, Strategy). Each role layers new responsibilities onto whatever class plays it.
CollaborationsSequence diagrams: how the roles interact at runtime.
ConsequencesAdvantages and disadvantages. This is the part to memorize, so you know when to apply the pattern — and when not to.
Sample codeIllustrative only. Don't fixate on it; the implementation depends heavily on the language.
Patterns Add Roles to Classes

A subtle but important idea: every object-oriented pattern defines roles, and mapping a role onto a class adds responsibilities to that class. The same class can even play roles from several patterns at once. This is why "patterns" is less about new code and more about which obligations each class takes on.

Patterns Are Not a Substitute for Thought

Patterns are trade-offs — there's no silver bullet. Their implementation depends strongly on the language: in Java you lean on interfaces; in Python there's no first-class interface (you reach for abstract base classes); in Ruby you can sometimes just write include Singleton; and in aspect-oriented programming a pattern can collapse to two lines. The patterns in this course are object-oriented patterns — they assume the OO paradigm.

The value of patterns is the abstraction of design experience: the "Gang of Four" (Gamma, Helm, Johnson, Vlissides) distilled decades of industry wisdom into their 1995 book, Design Patterns: Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software. Studying patterns is a shortcut to that collective experience — a cheat code that upgrades you toward a senior engineer's instincts without living through every lesson yourself.

Don't Name the Pattern in the Class Name

It's tempting to call a class WeatherObserver or FlyStrategyImpl. Often a bad idea — and here's the punchline the lecture drove at: putting the pattern's name in the class exposes a design decision that callers shouldn't depend on. That is itself a violation of information hiding. If you later refactor away from the pattern, the name lies.

Part III

Strategy — Delegation over Inheritance

We start with Strategy because it's the most straightforward and one of the most-used patterns. The professor introduced it through a now-classic example (from the Head First Design Patterns book): a duck simulator.

The Duck Problem

We want to simulate many kinds of ducks. The naive design: an abstract Duck class with quack(), swim(), and display(), and concrete subclasses — MallardDuck, RedheadDuck — that override display() so each looks right. So far this is just basic inheritance and polymorphism. Then the trouble starts.

The Trouble — Cross-Cutting Behavior

Add a fly() method to Duck. Now a RubberDuck appears: it can't fly, it squeaks instead of quacking. A DecoyDuck appears: it can't fly and can't quack. Flying and quacking cut across the hierarchy — some ducks have each behavior, some don't, in every combination. Inheritance can't express this cleanly.

The lecture walked through the failed fixes, which is the most instructive part:

1

Add a FlyableDuck subclass between Duck and the flyers. This works exactly once. Now add rocket-powered ducks, dancing ducks — each new cross-cutting feature wants its own intermediate level, and with single inheritance you only get to insert one. You'd have to reserve that one slot for your single most important feature.

2

Define fly() on Duck and override it to throw an exception or do nothing for non-flyers. This violates the Liskov Substitution Principle: if the superclass promises a behavior (doesn't throw), a subclass must not break that promise. Overriding a concrete method to do nothing also defeats everyone's expectations. Not clean design.

3

Make Flyable and Quackable interfaces and implement them per duck. Now every flying duck re-implements identical fly() code, every quacking duck re-implements identical quack() code. Massive duplication — three copies of "fly with wings," two copies of a quack. Also bad.

Key Concept — Liskov Substitution Principle

One of the SOLID design principles: if code works with a superclass, it must keep working when you hand it any subclass. A subclass may not violate the guarantees the superclass provides. (SOLID is taught more fully in CS 35L; the professor offered to share slides.)

The Fix: Delegation over Inheritance

The Big Idea — Favor Delegation over Inheritance

Inheritance is a one-time chip: with single inheritance you can only "spend" it once. So whenever a behavior varies independently across your objects, don't inherit it — delegate it. Pull the varying behavior into its own class hierarchy with an interface, and have your object hold a reference to one implementation and forward calls to it. This is a layer of indirection: another object decides how the behavior works, on your behalf.

Concretely: define a FlyBehavior interface with implementations FlyWithWings and FlyNoWay (a null object — it doesn't override flying, it simply is "can't fly"). Define a QuackBehavior interface with Quack, Squeak, and MuteQuack. The Duck aggregates one of each and delegates to it.

abstract class Duck {
  FlyBehavior flyBehavior;       // the varying part, delegated
  QuackBehavior quackBehavior;

  // These setters MUST be public — see callout below
  public void setFlyBehavior(FlyBehavior fb)   { flyBehavior = fb; }
  public void setQuackBehavior(QuackBehavior qb) { quackBehavior = qb; }

  // One-liners: just forward the call to the behavior object
  public void performFly()   { flyBehavior.fly(); }
  public void performQuack() { quackBehavior.quack(); }

  // swim() is shared by ALL ducks, so it stays here — no abstraction yet
  public void swim() { /* every duck floats the same way */ }
}

class MallardDuck extends Duck {
  public MallardDuck() {
    flyBehavior   = new FlyWithWings();   // pick behaviors in the constructor
    quackBehavior = new Quack();
  }
}

Two design choices in that code deserve attention:

Why the setters are public

setFlyBehavior and setQuackBehavior are public so behavior can be swapped at runtime, and so an outside object can configure the duck. A MallardDuck can be upgraded to rocket-powered flight mid-flight: mallard.setFlyBehavior(new FlyRocketPowered()). Runtime interchangeability is the heart of Strategy — if you don't actually need it, a simpler non-OO approach may be better.

Don't Make Premature Abstractions

Notice swim() was not pulled into a SwimBehavior interface. Every duck swims identically, so there's no variation to encapsulate — yet. A premature abstraction is one you don't need in any foreseeable change scenario; it just adds classes and confusion. The same change-scenario thinking from Part I tells you which abstractions to build now and which to wait on: abstract a behavior precisely when a likely change calls for the flexibility.

This delivers the Open–Closed Principle (another SOLID principle): a module should be open for extension, closed for modification. To add rocket flight, you write a new FlyRocketPowered class and plug it in — no existing code changes. New behavior arrives by adding code, never by editing it.

The Pattern Structure & Its Roles

Generalize away from ducks. Strategy adds these roles:

RoleIn the duck example
Context — has operations callable from outside; holds a strategyDuck
Strategy (interface) — the abstract varying behaviorFlyBehavior, QuackBehavior
Concrete Strategy — one realization of the interfaceFlyWithWings, FlyNoWay, Quack, Squeak, MuteQuack
Strategy. The 1 on the aggregation marks that a Context holds exactly one strategy of each kind — remember this number; it is what distinguishes Strategy from Observer.
Note: a UML correction the class spotted

During the live lecture a student noticed the slide had drawn the «interface» marker in the wrong place — it belongs above the class name inside the box. The professor confirmed the slide had a bug and praised the student for paying attention to UML detail. The point for you: UML notation is precise, and the midterm tests the direct mapping between Java code and UML. Read every line and multiplicity carefully.

Consequences — The Trade-off

Disadvantage: some duplication remains — a behavior shared by several concrete strategies still gets implemented once per class, and callers must invoke performFly()/performQuack() rather than methods directly on the duck. Also: by exposing the strategy, you expose a design decision — usually fine if that decision is unlikely to change. The intent (what we accept this cost for): easily add new behaviors, change behaviors, and remap which ducks use which behavior.

Part IV

Observer — One-to-Many Notification

Strategy handled "a class needs one swappable behavior." Observer handles a different recurring problem: "many objects need to be told whenever one object changes." You've used it without knowing — every UI framework, every event system, the View in most frameworks is built on it.

The Weather Station Problem

We're building a weather station. A WeatherData class produces getTemperature(), getHumidity(), getPressure(), and when readings update it calls measurementsChanged(). We have three displays — current conditions, statistics, forecast — and we want to add more later. The naive measurementsChanged():

void measurementsChanged() {
  float t = getTemperature();
  float h = getHumidity();
  float p = getPressure();

  currentConditionsDisplay.update(t, h, p);   // hard-coded
  statisticsDisplay.update(t, h, p);         // hard-coded
  forecastDisplay.update(t, h, p);           // hard-coded
}

You see code like this in the wild constantly. To add a display, you add a line here. Why is that bad? The class noted several reasons — duplication is the obvious one, but the lecture pushed toward the deeper, modularity reason:

The Real Problem — Mixed Concerns

WeatherData is a domain class: its job is to define what weather data is. The displays are a UI concern. By naming concrete displays inside it, we mix domain logic with UI. Now the weather-data expert must remember which displays exist, and the display author must understand the domain class. Ideally WeatherData should not even know the UI exists. The goal of design (from last lecture) is to minimize what anyone must hold in their head — and mixing concerns blows that up.

The Pattern: Subject and Observer

Key Concept — Observer Pattern

The Observer pattern defines a one-to-many dependency between objects: when one object (the Subject) changes state, all its dependents (the Observers) are notified and update automatically. It is the foundation of the publish–subscribe model.

The fix introduces two tiny interfaces:

  • Subject — declares registerObserver(), removeObserver(), and notifyObservers().
  • Observer — declares exactly one method: update(). That's the entire contract.

Now WeatherData implements Subject and holds a list of observers. It doesn't know what they do, how many there are — zero or a million — or what type they are. It only knows they honor the update() contract.

class WeatherData implements Subject {
  List<Observer> observers = new ArrayList<>();

  public void registerObserver(Observer o) { observers.add(o); }
  public void removeObserver(Observer o)   { observers.remove(o); }

  public void notifyObservers() {
    for (Observer o : observers)        // iterate — don't name names
      o.update(temp, humidity, pressure);
  }
}
Observer. The aggregation carries a * — a Subject holds many Observers. The Subject's arrow stops at the Observer interface; it never knows the concrete display classes below.
Design Patterns Aren't Magic

A student asked: under the hood, isn't this just storing everyone who's observing? Yes — and that's the key insight. A pattern doesn't add magic; it restructures code to match the mental model, so people find the right place to make a change. Sometimes the compiled code is literally identical to the naive version. (Here it isn't quite — you've added a list and a few CPU cycles.) We happily pay that small runtime cost because for most real systems, changeability matters more than performance.

Observer's real win is reduced coupling — exactly the principle from the previous lecture. The Subject depends only on the tiny Observer interface. Observers can be added at any time and are notified automatically. Subjects and observers are independently reusable, because each only honors a small contract.

Push vs. Pull

There are two flavors of Observer, differing in how data reaches the observer:

Push modelPull model
update(temp, humidity, pressure) — the Subject sends all potentially-relevant data as parameters.update() with no parameters — the Observer then pulls the data it needs from the Subject.
Observer needs no reference back to the Subject.Observer must hold a reference to its Subject to query it.
Cost: every observer gets a flood of data whether it wants it or not.Cost: introduces a dependency from Observer back to Subject.

Part V

Mediator — Centralizing Tangled Communication

Strategy and Observer each tamed a single dependency. Mediator tackles the worst case: many objects that all need to talk to each other. Its intent is to centralize complex communication and control between related objects.

The Smart Home Problem

Picture a Java-enabled smart home. Bob hits snooze; his alarm should tell the coffee maker to start brewing — but not on weekends (too much caffeine), so the alarm must check the calendar. The sprinkler must turn off before the shower schedule so Bob doesn't get soaked, so it checks the shower and the weather. Trace the dependencies:

The Big Ball of Mud

The alarm checks the calendar and the sprinkler, then starts the coffee. The coffee pot checks the calendar and the alarm. The sprinkler checks the calendar and the shower. Every appliance knows about several others. The dependency graph approaches a complete graph — everything coupled to everything. Adding one new appliance, or changing one rule, means touching code scattered everywhere.

Mediator turns a tangle of mutual dependencies (left) into a star: every appliance talks only to the mediator, which holds all the coordination logic (right).

The Mediator solution: strip every appliance down to its single responsibility. The alarm just goes on/off. The coffee pot just goes on/off. The sprinkler just goes on/off. The calendar just reports the day and time. All the coordination — "if snooze and it's a weekday, start the coffee; turn off the sprinkler before the shower" — moves into one Mediator object that talks to each appliance.

The Big Idea — Total Decoupling

With a Mediator, the individual objects are completely decoupled — they don't even know the others exist. The alarm has no idea there's a coffee pot in the house. This makes every appliance highly reusable (drop it into any home) and gives you a single place to change: add a tea maker, or change how snooze behaves, and you edit only the Mediator. You've also shrunk every appliance's interface to a trivial on/off.

The Big Trade-off

Mediator is the lecture's clearest illustration that every pattern is a trade-off. The benefits are real — increased reusability, simplified maintenance (one place to change), fewer message types flying around — but they come at a steep price:

Consequences — The Cost

All the messy logic now lives inside the Mediator, which becomes very hard to understand: you can't grasp it without understanding every object it coordinates. It is also a single point of vulnerability and complexity. The security angle is stark — once someone compromises the Mediator, they control everything: read your calendar, run your sprinkler, know when you're in the shower. You've concentrated all knowledge and control in one object.

When Mediator Actually Helps

Mediator pays off when the dependency graph is dense — near-complete, like the smart home, where everything genuinely must coordinate with everything. There it objectively reduces dependencies. But on a sparse graph it can backfire: the Mediator must now know about all objects (raising its cognitive load to "understand five things"), whereas without it each object only needed to understand its two or three real neighbors. Use the change-scenario lens: how connected is the graph, really?

A student's idea: a hierarchy of mediators

A student asked whether you could have multiple levels of mediators — a "mediator of mediators" — for the dense regions of a large graph. The professor agreed you can stack patterns this way; patterns are ideas, not fixed templates, so you can compose them. He hadn't seen that exact instance named in practice, but expected that any sufficiently complex control system naturally evolves into something like it, whether or not anyone calls it "Mediator."

Putting It All Together

Today's lecture is really one idea wearing three costumes. The foundation, from Part I, is information hiding driven by change scenarios: find the design decisions likely to change, and arrange the code so those changes stay localized. Patterns are just the recurring shapes that good, change-resilient designs take — and a shared vocabulary so you and your teammates (and your AI agent) can name those shapes instantly.

Each pattern absorbs a different kind of change, and they line up neatly by how an object relates to the behavior it depends on:

PatternRecurring problemMultiplicityThe change it absorbs
StrategyOne class needs a swappable behavior, varying across instances and at runtimeContext → 1 strategyAdd/change a behavior; remap which object uses which
ObserverMany objects must react when one object changesSubject → many observersAdd new observers freely without touching the subject
MediatorMany objects must coordinate with each otherMediator ↔ all objectsChange any coordination rule in one place

Notice the recurring move: each pattern replaces a rigid, hard-coded dependency with a layer of indirection behind an interface. Strategy replaces inheritance with delegation. Observer replaces hard-coded update calls with a list behind a one-method interface. Mediator replaces a web of mutual references with a single coordinator. Every one of them buys changeability — and every one charges a price (duplication, a little runtime overhead, or a complex central object). The professor's refrain holds: there is no silver bullet. Your job is to weigh the trade-off using the change scenarios from Part I.

And the thread connecting Strategy and Observer to information hiding is literal: Observer is fundamentally about reducing coupling, the principle from the previous lecture; exposing a strategy or naming a pattern in a class is a potential information-hiding violation — acceptable only when the exposed decision is unlikely to change. The same two questions govern everything: is the secret hidden, and is it likely to change?

Why This Still Matters in the Age of AI

The professor closed on this: a pattern name is a high-bandwidth instruction to an AI coding agent, which already knows every pattern from its training data. But knowing when to apply a pattern — reasoning about likely changes and trade-offs — is the judgment AI hasn't mastered, and it's exactly what these lectures train. His advice: use AI to do more, not the same in less time, and keep exercising your own continual-learning ability — your most valuable, least-automatable asset.
