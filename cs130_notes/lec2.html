Modeling & UML — Drawing the System Before You Build It

Week 1 — why software engineers reach for the whiteboard, and the UML diagrams (use case & state machine) that let us find bugs before we write a single line of code.

Part I

Warming Up: Stress-Testing User Stories with INVEST

The lecture opened with a "software-engineering workout": the professor put a couple of user stories on the board and asked the class to hunt for flaws. Before we can judge a user story, we need to remember what a user story is and what makes a good one — so let's catch ourselves up from scratch.

What Is a User Story, and What Is INVEST?

A user story is a short, plain-language description of a feature told from the point of view of the person who wants it. It follows the template "As a <role>, I want <goal> so that <benefit>." User stories are the lightweight way agile teams capture requirements — statements of what the system should do.

Key Concept — INVEST

INVEST is a checklist of six qualities a good user story should have: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable. You use it as a lens to spot weak stories. This lecture's warm-up only exercised four of the letters — Independent, Negotiable, Estimable, and Testable — so those are the ones to know cold.

Story #1 — When a Story Leans on Unfinished Work

The first story: "As a customer, I want to pay for my items using a credit card so that I can complete my purchase." The catch the professor planted: user registration and card management are not done yet.

1

Paying with a card assumes you have an account and a stored card. But those stories (registration, card management) aren't built. So the payment story depends on them — it violates Independent.

2

A natural follow-up: if it's not independent, is it also not Testable? The professor's nuance: not necessarily. Once the stories it depends on are implemented, this one becomes testable. Dependence does not automatically destroy testability.

3

And how do we size a dependent story? You estimate only the delta — the additional work this story adds on top of what it depends on — not the cost of everything underneath it.

Testable and Estimable Are Independent Axes

The professor pressed the class to find a story that is testable but not estimable, and vice versa. Testable but not estimable: "solve P vs NP" — we can check a proof, but we have no idea how long it takes. Estimable but not testable: "give the page a modern design" — we can guess the effort (it's roughly the same whatever "modern" means), but we can't write a test for "modern." The two properties are not 100% correlated, so always check each one separately.

Story #2 — "Blazingly Fast and Extremely Modern"

The second story: "As a website visitor, I want the homepage to load blazingly fast and look extremely modern so that I have a pleasant browsing experience."

  • Not Estimable — "blazingly fast" has no number. Ten milliseconds every time means duplicate servers, load balancing, caching, eventual consistency — a huge job. "A few seconds" is trivial. You can't estimate a target you can't measure.
  • Not Testable — both "modern" (subjective) and "blazingly fast" (unquantified) can't be turned into a pass/fail test.
  • Arguably not Small — you could split "blazingly fast" from "modern look" into two separate stories, since they're quite different concerns.
Exam Tip Straight From the Professor

For story #2 the "correct" exam answers are Estimable and Testable. Picking Small as well — or not picking it — would both be accepted. When two readings are defensible, say so and justify; you won't be marked down for the well-argued one.

Part II

Why We Model: The Whiteboard Is Our Favorite Tool

Having sharpened our requirements thinking, the lecture pivots to today's real topic: modeling. But before any notation, the professor answers the only question that matters — why bother drawing pictures of software at all?

Reason 1 — Words Build Different Pictures in Different Heads

Imagine a COMSCI 130 team talking about "the structure they want to build." One person pictures a skyscraper, another a mall, another a factory, another a government building. Speech is one-dimensional — a sequence of words — so it's terrible at conveying spatial structure. A model on the whiteboard is multi-dimensional: it synchronizes everyone's mental image and shares your idea with someone else.

The Big Idea

Software's complexity has grown exponentially, while human cognition has stayed flat. The main bottleneck in software engineering isn't writing code — anyone can write complex code — it's transferring the idea in your head into another developer's head. Modeling exists to overcome that bottleneck. Nobody reads a thousand lines of code to understand your design; they want a higher-level, visual abstraction.

Reason 2 — Find Bugs Early, When They're Cheap

Like a civil engineer analyzing a bridge blueprint before pouring concrete, we analyze software models before writing code. Many models can even be automatically checked for certain properties — and our human intelligence can spot still more. The payoff is timing: the earlier you find a bug, the cheaper it is to fix.

1

Requirements phase — nearly free. Nothing is built; you just realized a requirement was missing (e.g. the user never told us what counts as a "strong password").

2

Design phase — still cheap. Little code is written, so a fix is easy.

3

Implementation phase — more expensive. Code now exists and must be reworked.

4

Maintenance phase — most expensive. The original developer has left the company, and a stranger must reverse-engineer the code to fix it.

Why Modeling Pays Off

Modeling moves the bug-finding earlier in the life cycle. The sooner you can criticize your own ideas and spot an issue, the cheaper the fix. That single sentence is the economic justification for everything else in this lecture.

Reason 3 — A Model Is a Deliberate Mapping (and a Deliberate Abstraction)

In modeling theory, a model is a mapping from elements in the real world to elements in the model. Crucially, the mapping is partial in both directions: some real-world things appear in the model, some real-world things are intentionally left out, and the model may even contain elements that don't exist in the world. Leaving things out is not a flaw — it is the entire point.

A model maps selected real-world elements to model elements. Some real things are deliberately omitted; the model may add things of its own.
Deep dive: the "perfect model" debate (the Quine / Mount Everest tangent)

During the break, students debated whether a self-printing program — a quine — is the one case where the perfect model of a thing is the thing itself, because every component is mission-critical and nothing can be abstracted away. The professor's resolution: every model is designed with a specific goal. A map of the climbing path and a weather map are both models of Mount Everest, each useful for a different purpose. A "model" that contains all the information (the object itself) is perfect at completeness but useless for any goal that benefits from leaving information out. So the ideal model is not the object — it's the abstraction that best serves your purpose.

Part III

UML: One Language to Draw Them All

We now know why we model. But there are over 50 historical modeling languages — state machines, entity-relationship diagrams, data-flow diagrams, and more. Which one do you use? The industry converged on a single standard, and this section is its origin story and ground rules.

The Three Amigos and the Birth of UML

In the 1980s, object-oriented programming rose to prominence. OO is a great fit for modeling because a program is built from separate classes that interact and that mirror real-world things. This sparked an explosion of competing visual notations. Then three researchers — Grady Booch, Jim Rumbaugh, and Ivar Jacobson (the "Three Amigos") — merged their notations into the Unified Modeling Language (UML), which became the industry standard.

Key Concept — UML

The Unified Modeling Language is the most widely used modeling language in software. Learning it means you can read diagrams others made and write diagrams others will understand. Its full specification is 700+ dense pages — but that spec is written for tool builders, not for you. Don't read it.

Semi-Formal, and Almost Everything Is Optional

UML is deliberately semi-formal and graphical. Two ground rules follow from that:

The "Leave It Out" Rule

In UML, almost everything is optional. If you draw an element, you make a claim ("these two things are connected"). If you leave it out, that part is simply unspecified — you're making no claim. Models are never "complete"; they're intentionally abstractions. Remember this — it returns as the key difference between the two kinds of state machine in Part VI.

Being semi-formal means some elements have a defined meaning but many are not formalized. The upside: it's flexible enough for anything. The downside: it can be ambiguous. UML is also built to be extended — e.g. SysML extends UML for systems engineering (NASA uses it) to enable automatic analysis. Contrast this with purely textual, fully-formal modeling languages used in aerospace: powerful, but hard for humans to read. UML's graphical nature is precisely why it spreads ideas between people so well.

The Family of UML Diagrams

UML offers many diagram types, which split into structural views (what the system is made of) and behavioral views (how it acts over time). This course teaches several:

DiagramWhat it describesBest for
Use caseFunctional requirements, end-user viewWhat the system should do (like user stories)
ClassStructure: classes, methods, attributesThe developer's view of the system
SequenceMessages exchanged over timeProtocols
State machineStates an entity moves throughSystems whose behavior changes sharply by state
Activity / ComponentBehavior & high-level architectureOrder of interactions; module structure
Descriptive vs Prescriptive — Check Which One

A diagram can be prescriptive (describing a system you're about to build) or descriptive (describing the system as it currently is). If a diagram claims to be descriptive, treat it with suspicion — it's probably outdated, because most diagrams are maintained by hand. Inferring accurate diagrams automatically from source code is still extremely hard (the professor's own PhD work on auto-generating component diagrams for robots was a research prototype).

Part IV

Use Case Diagrams: Requirements You Can Show the User

The first concrete UML diagram. A use case diagram captures functional requirements from an end-user-facing perspective — think of it as user stories drawn as a picture. The running example is an automatic train: an unmanned people-mover (a "Waymo on tracks") that loops a circular track, stopping at stations A and B when a passenger presses a request button.

The Notational Elements

A use case diagram has exactly five kinds of element:

The five elements: actors (stick figures), the system boundary (box), the system name, use cases (ovals), and associations (lines).
  • System boundary & name — a box that's mostly symbolic; it labels the system (so readers know this is the train system, not the ticket machine) and shows that actors live outside it.
  • Actor — a stick figure that represents a role, not a specific human. A technician can also be a passenger in their free time; one person may play several actors, and one actor may be played by many people.
  • Use case — an oval, essentially a user story.
  • Association — a line meaning the actor is involved in that use case (as primary, secondary, or even tertiary actor). Multiple actors can connect to one use case — e.g. both a customer and an admin can initiate "cancel order."

Inside a Use Case: The Flow of Events

A use case can be detailed with a sequence of events: the actor name, an entry condition, an exit condition, and the step-by-step flow. For the train's "normal ride":

1

Passenger P arrives and presses the request button.

2

The train arrives and stops at the platform; the doors open.

3

The passenger steps into the train; the doors close.

4

The passenger presses the request button for the final stop.

5

At the final stop the doors open and the passenger exits.

You don't write this flow for every use case — modeling is context-dependent, so you detail only the use cases that need it.

Relationships Between Use Cases: Include, Extend, Generalize

Use cases can relate to each other. The professor admits the exact notation is "tedious" — and that you'll need it memorized or on your exam cheat sheet. All three arrows point in the reading direction.

RelationshipNotationMeaning (quantifier)
«include»Dashed line, open arrow, keyword «include» (base verb, not "includes")For all — every run of the base use case always performs the included one. "Ride «include» Push Button": riding always involves pushing the button.
«extend»Dashed line, open arrow, keyword «extend»There exists — in some exceptional runs, the extending use case is part of the flow. "Pay Fine «extend» Ride": only some rides include paying a fine.
GeneralizationSolid line, hollow (open) triangle arrowheadis-a — a specialized use case inherits the parent's behavior and can override parts. "Push Train Button" is a specialization of "Push Button."
Notation Footguns to Memorize

The arrows are dashed, not dotted, not solid, with an open (not filled) arrowhead, and the keyword goes in double angular brackets in its base form — «include», never "includes." A filled arrowhead or a solid/dashed line means something completely different in class diagrams. Conform to the notation exactly or the diagram says the wrong thing.

Reading the direction of association — a clever rule

Notice "Pay Fine" has no association line to the Passenger, but "Push Button" does. The rule: an actor's association means that actor can initiate the use case. The passenger initiates pushing the button; the passenger does not initiate paying a fine — the system initiates that. The missing line is itself a meaningful claim.

Include vs Generalization — When to Use Which

Both decompose a use case, but they say different things. «include» means "every flow through the parent contains this smaller sub-use-case" (a for-all hierarchy). Generalization means "here are different variants of the use case," like class inheritance — and it lets you name the special case so you can refer to it elsewhere. The same generalization triangle, with the same is-a meaning, reappears in class diagrams later in the course.

Extension Points and UML Notes

To say where in a use case an extension kicks in, you mark an extension point — a named location in the flow of events (e.g. "status validation"). You then attach a UML note (a box with a folded corner on a dotted leader line) holding the condition. Example from a library: "Refuse Loan «extend» Borrow Book", at the status validation extension point, under the condition too many books borrowed.

"Refuse Loan" extends "Borrow Book" at the status validation extension point; a UML note (folded-corner box on a dotted leader) carries the condition.

One use case can have multiple extension points, and multiple extensions can attach to the same point (each as its own note with its own condition). These notes have no formal meaning — they're explanatory annotations layered onto the diagram.

What Use Case Diagrams Are For

Use case diagrams describe requirements, not design — they say what the system should do, never how it's built. That makes them boundary objects: artifacts you can show actual users and ask "is this the functionality you want?" You can supplement them in side documents with preconditions, postconditions, main/alternative flows, and non-functional requirements.

Part V

The Limits of User Stories: A Crash, and a Better Tool

The professor circled back to finish the requirements thread with a sobering case study — because it reveals exactly what user stories cannot do.

The Lufthansa Runway Crash

A plane is supposed to engage reverse thrust if and only if it is on the ground. The engineers implemented the "on the ground" check as "are the wheels spinning?" One day a plane landed on a wet runway: there wasn't enough friction, the wheels didn't spin fast enough, the system concluded the plane wasn't on the ground, reverse thrust never engaged, and the plane couldn't stop and crashed.

1

Intended requirement: reverse thrust ⇔ on the ground. Reasonable.

2

Design decision: approximate "on the ground" by "wheels spinning." This is where the assumption was baked in.

3

Reality: a wet runway breaks the assumption — wheels can be on the ground yet not spinning. The proxy was wrong.

A Design Bug, Not a Requirements Bug

The class proposed user stories to prevent it (e.g. a manual override button, echoing the 737 MAX). But the professor's key point: the requirement ("reverse thrust iff on ground") was fine. The failure was the design choice of how to detect "on the ground." User stories only capture requirements — they couldn't have caught this. A story trying to encode the design fix would even violate Negotiable, because it bakes a design decision into the requirement.

The Big Idea

User stories are great for end-user-facing functional requirements, but they are poorly suited for formal requirements (safety-critical logic) and for quality requirements (reliability, safety, performance, availability). For safety-critical formal requirements, reach instead for state machines or predicate logic — which is precisely where this lecture is headed next.

Quality Attribute Scenarios

So how do we specify quality requirements? With quality attribute scenarios. Every such scenario pairs a stimulus (an event arriving at the system) with a response measure (a measurable outcome).

Quality attributeStimulusResponse measure
PerformanceA web request arrivesAverage response time ≤ 10 ms (or a max/min bound per request)
MaintainabilityA change request: swap the backend databaseTakes less than one developer-week, without touching certain modules

The professor noted that a fuller treatment uses a six-part framework (as taught in CMU's Software Architecture in Practice), but for this course it's enough to think in terms of a stimulus and a measurable response.

Engineer's Takeaway

Match the requirement format to the requirement type. End-user functionality → user stories / use cases. Quality attributes (performance, maintainability, availability) → quality attribute scenarios. Safety-critical formal logic → state machines or predicate logic. Picking the wrong format is itself a modeling mistake.

Part VI

State Machine Diagrams: Behavior You Can Verify

This is the tool the crash story pointed us toward — and the professor's favorite. A state machine diagram describes behavior: the various stages an entity passes through in its lifetime, the transitions between them, and what it means to be in each state. UML state machines build on the general idea of an automaton (a finite-state machine) but add UML's own notation.

States, Transitions, and the Trigger / Guard / Effect Anatomy

A state is a condition the entity is in; it's drawn as a rounded rectangle. The initial state is a solid filled circle; the final state is a circle-within-a-circle. A transition is a straight arrow between states, labeled with up to three optional parts:

trigger [guard] / effect
Key Concept — trigger [guard] / effect

Trigger = the event/action that fires the transition (a button press, a received message). Guard = a condition in square brackets, checked after the trigger; the transition only fires if it's true. Effect = a side effect after the slash (increment a variable, show a message). All three are optional — show only the parts that are relevant.

Action vs Activity — Time or No Time

An action is atomic and timeless — it completes instantly without interruption (it's what happens on a transition). An activity is something that takes time — it's what happens while in a state, where time can pass. Keeping this straight matters: triggers/effects are actions; staying in a state can run an activity.

Solid circle = initial state. Transition drop [students > 1] / remove student: trigger drop, guard [students > 1], effect after the slash.

State machines can include decision points with guarded branches. In the lecture's class-enrollment example, a drop transition hits a decision point that checks "is at least one student left?" — modeling a class that survives until the very last student drops, and where grades are only submitted after exam week.

Hierarchical State Machines — States Inside States

The professor's favorite feature: a state can contain its own state machine. Consider an online shop. We start in Browsing; selecting an item moves us to Items Chosen (where we can pick more); clicking checkout drops us into a Checkout super-state that has its own internal machine: Incomplete → Credit Card Validation → (Complete | Transaction Denied).

Finding Design Bugs by Reading the Diagram

This is modeling's whole promise in action — the class spotted real flaws just by looking, no code required:

  • No way to un-select items. You can only add to the cart, never remove (except by entering bad card info and bailing out).
  • You can't leave checkout without entering credit-card data. Change your mind about paying? You're stuck.
  • Ambiguous cart state after purchase. The diagram never says whether returning to Browsing empties the cart — so the claim is unspecified and must be clarified.

Catching these in a diagram is far cheaper than digging through millions of lines of code — and it's exactly the conversation to have with your product owner.

The Superpower of Hierarchical States

How do you fix "can't leave checkout"? You could draw a cancel transition from every internal state — a spider-web. Instead, draw one transition from the super-state itself to Browsing, labeled cancel. It automatically applies to every state nested inside. One arrow replaces many. A history state (an H in a circle) adds more power: re-entering a super-state resumes where you left off instead of restarting at the initial substate.

Protocol State Machines — Pre/Post Conditions You Can Formally Verify

Recall the "leave it out" rule from Part III. It's exactly what separates the two kinds of state machine:

Behavioral State MachineProtocol State Machine
Shows behavior the system can go throughShows only what is possible
A missing transition = unspecified ("don't know")A missing transition = definitely impossible
Uses guards […] on transitionsUses preconditions (before trigger), postconditions (after trigger), and state invariants
For shared understanding & conversationFor strong guarantees & formal verification

In a protocol state machine you write [precondition] trigger / [postcondition]. A condition before the trigger is a precondition ("this action is only available if this holds"); a condition after is a postcondition ("this is guaranteed afterward"). A condition attached to a state that always holds while in it is an invariant. In a bank-account example: you can only activate a new account if is verified (precondition); afterward is unique ID is guaranteed (postcondition); and the closed state carries the invariant has no balance.

The Big Idea

Protocol state machines can reference real methods/predicates from your code (e.g. isUniqueID()), so a formal verifier can prove the diagram's invariants and conditions always hold. This is how Amazon hardened the AWS kernel: they specified state machines, paid modeling experts for ~two years to formally verify it with a model checker like TLA+, found a handful of deep bugs, and could then tell customers they didn't just test — they formally verified. Use this heavyweight detail (pre/postconditions, invariants) when you need strong guarantees — avionics, medical devices, infrastructure — and use lightweight behavioral machines when you just need everyone on the same page.

Check Your Understanding

Correct — it is definitely impossible. This is the crucial difference from a behavioral machine, where an omitted transition just means "unspecified." Protocol machines show only what is possible, so absence is a hard claim of impossibility. That stronger semantics is what makes them suitable for formal verification with tools like TLA+. Note also that protocol machines use preconditions/postconditions/invariants, not the "guards" of option D.

Putting It All Together

This lecture moved from requirements to models, and the through-line is a single question every engineer must keep asking: what is the cheapest place to find a problem, and which notation lets me find it there?

We started by stress-testing user stories with INVEST (Part I) — and immediately bumped into their ceiling. The Lufthansa crash (Part V) showed user stories can't catch design bugs or formalize safety, and they can't express quality requirements — those need quality attribute scenarios (stimulus + response measure). That gap is the motivation for modeling (Part II): the whiteboard synchronizes mental images, and it pulls bug-finding to the cheap, early end of the life cycle.

UML (Part III) gives us one shared, semi-formal, graphical vocabulary — with its defining "leave-it-out = unspecified" rule. We then saw that rule animate two diagrams. Use case diagrams (Part IV) draw the functional requirements as a picture you can hand to a real user — a boundary object — with «include» (for-all), «extend» (there-exists), and generalization (is-a). State machine diagrams (Part VI) capture behavior: behavioral machines for shared understanding (where omission means "unspecified"), and protocol machines for ironclad guarantees (where omission means "impossible," and pre/postconditions/invariants enable formal verification with TLA+). Notice the same idea threading through: an absent element is itself a claim, and which claim it is depends on the diagram type.

The meta-lesson the professor kept returning to: every model serves a goal, and the engineer's hard task is matching the notation to the property you want to analyze. Functional, end-user requirements → user stories / use cases. Quality attributes → quality attribute scenarios. Safety-critical guarantees → protocol state machines + predicate logic + a verifier. There is no single perfect model — the perfect model of Mount Everest is Mount Everest, and that's useless. Leaving information out is the entire value of a model.

What's Next

The professor flagged where these ideas reappear. The generalization triangle from use case diagrams returns with identical "is-a" meaning in class diagrams — the developer's structural view of the system (classes, attributes, methods) coming up next. Sequence diagrams will model protocols (message order over time), and you'll keep meeting SysML and formal verifiers as the course pushes toward systems where "it compiles" isn't a strong enough guarantee. Keep the UML notation conventions (dashed vs solid lines, open vs filled arrowheads, the keyword brackets) on your exam cheat sheet — they're tedious but tested.
