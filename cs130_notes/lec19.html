AI Coding Agents & the Engineer's New Job

An interactive guide to Lecture 19 — how LLMs and coding agents actually work, the risks they create, and the software engineering discipline that turns them from a liability into leverage.

Part I

Will AI Take Your Job?

This lecture opens with the question on everyone's mind: will an AI write all the code so that human software engineers are no longer needed? Before we can reason about that, we need a way to measure how transformative a new technology actually is — otherwise we are just trading hype for fear. The professor frames the whole lecture around one historical comparison, so we will start there.

The Compiler Analogy and the "No Silver Bullet" Argument

In the 1960s, programmers wrote machine code and assembly — instructions tied directly to the hardware, one tedious line at a time. When compilers arrived, people panicked that the job of the "assembly programmer" would vanish. It largely did. So a natural worry today is that LLMs are the compiler of our era and the "programmer" job will vanish next.

But the professor draws a sharp distinction. To do this we need a term that recurs all lecture:

Key Concept — Productivity Gain

A productivity gain here means development-time speedup: does it now take one year instead of ten to build a given piece of software? A 10× (one order of magnitude) gain means cutting the work to a tenth.

Compilers achieved a genuine 10× productivity gain — the only invention in the history of software engineering ever to do so. Each high-level statement (say in Fortran) translated to roughly ten assembly instructions, so writing one line did the work of ten.

This is the argument of Fred Brooks — author of The Mythical Man-Month and his essay "No Silver Bullet." Brooks argued that no future tool, process, or management technique would ever deliver another 10× gain. His reasoning rests on a second key distinction:

Essential ComplexityAccidental Complexity
Inherent to the problem domain. Part of the problem, therefore part of the solution. Cannot be removed by tooling.Added by us when we don't implement things in the right way. Can be reduced.
Requirements, design, the actual logic the software must capture.Boilerplate, ceremony, fighting the language or tools.

Brooks's punchline: we do not spend 90% of our time on accidental complexity, so logically no technique that only attacks accidental complexity can give us another 10×. The studies the professor could find put AI's measured productivity gain at roughly 21%–50% — that is about 1.5×, not 5× and certainly not 10×.

The Big Idea

Until we reach AGI, AI will not be a "silver bullet." It speeds up development — the accidental-complexity part — but the essential challenges of requirements, design, and quality assurance still demand human intelligence. So those are the most durable skills you can build.

Why the Compiler Won and the LLM Hasn't

Here is the deepest technical reason, and it connects directly to ideas from a programming-languages mindset. A compiler is a sound abstraction: if you wanted to, you could predict the exact assembly it produces. It always gives you precisely what you asked for, so you never have to read the output. That predictability is what made it safe to stop looking at the lower level.

Sound vs Unsound Abstraction

An LLM is non-deterministic, black-box, and unpredictable. You cannot predict from a prompt exactly what code you will get. Because you can't predict it, you might get something you didn't want — so you are forced to review it. That review work is effort a compiler never demanded. An unsound abstraction can't be trusted blindly, which caps the productivity gain.

More With Less, Not Less Effort

The transformation is still real. The former CEO of GitHub (where Copilot was built) put it bluntly: "either you embrace AI or you get out of this career." But his key observation is subtle:

Ambition, Not Just Cost

In practice AI is not turning a 40-hour week into a 20-hour week. Developers ship more features and raise their ambition — "doing more with less." The real shift is the ceiling going up, not the cost going down. And the durable skills — requirements, design, quality assurance — remain as important as ever.

Part II

How LLMs Actually Work

To use AI responsibly as an engineer, you have to understand the machine you are steering. If you treat an LLM as a mind, you will misjudge it. The professor walks through the three phases of building one, then gives a mental model — the statistical parrot — that explains almost every failure mode you'll see.

Pre-training, Post-training, Inference

1

Pre-training. A neural network is trained on essentially all accessible code — every open-source repository — by learning to predict the next token given the previous tokens. That next-token objective is the engine, and (as researchers like Yann LeCun argue) also a fundamental limitation of the current architecture.

2

Post-training. The model is fine-tuned on labeled input–output pairs. Coding is a task LLMs are incredibly good at precisely because so much labeled data exists: LeetCode problems paired with optimal solutions, tickets paired with the pull requests that implement them. Human feedback is also folded in via reinforcement learning — when ChatGPT shows you two answers and asks which is better, you are an unpaid reinforcement trainer.

3

Inference. You give a natural-language prompt; the model predicts the most likely sequence of next tokens. State-of-the-art tools do this non-deterministically — the source of the unpredictability from Part I.

The Statistical Parrot

Because the model is biased toward outputs that appear often in its training data, two consequences follow. Ask for "the shortest Python Fibonacci" — a thing solved a million times — and it answers with near-perfect accuracy. Ask for something rare and quality collapses.

The Clock at 5:25

Until recently, asking an image model for a clock showing 5:25 always failed — it drew the hands near 10:10. Why? Photographers compose clocks at that angle because it looks good, so Google Images is saturated with 10:10. The model reproduces the distribution of its training data, not the real world. The same bias governs code.

The professor's central metaphor: imagine an LLM as a parrot witness in a criminal trial. A parrot is likely to repeat what was said many times before; it may repeat phrases out of context; it may confidently quote things that are factually false; and it may invent plausible-sounding statements. Useful sometimes — but you would never bet your life on it.

What the Parrot Does to Your Code

A coding agent will reliably reproduce code seen often before — but it is biased toward older code, so it may hallucinate API calls that don't exist, emit outdated or insecure patterns, and confidently invent calls to non-existent functions. The professor reports these properties persist even on the highest "thinking/max" settings of frontier models. Trust accordingly.

From Chat Box to Coding Agent

Two developments soften (but never erase) the parrot problem. Chain-of-thought reasoning lets a model work through a problem rather than purely parroting — using tools to look up real API documentation and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to query the facts it needs. The professor's analogy: like a student, an AI does better on an open-book exam than a closed-book one.

A coding agent installed in your IDE (Codex, Claude Code, vs-code plugins, standalone apps) can read files, edit files, run commands, run tests, inspect errors, and iterate. That access to the same context a human has is exactly what makes them powerful — they are no longer generating blind.

Why This Matters Later

Keep the parrot model in mind for Part III. Every risk — insecure code, hallucinated APIs, the illusion of competence — is downstream of "this thing predicts likely tokens, it does not understand the world." And every mitigation in Part IV exists to compensate for exactly that.

Part III

The Risks of AI-Generated Code

Now that we know what an LLM is, we can predict how it goes wrong in practice. The professor groups the dangers into three: insecure code that feels secure, unmaintainable code that compounds into debt, and — most insidious — the erosion of your own skills.

The Illusion of Security

Studies cited: AI-generated code caused serious security vulnerabilities in about 20% of companies; a GitHub-Copilot-style study found ~40% of generated code contained a vulnerability. But the professor flags one result as the most important of the whole topic:

The Most Important Insight

In a controlled study, developers using AI produced more security vulnerabilities than a control group — yet rated their own code as more secure than the AI-free group did. AI can make insecure code feel deceptively secure. The danger isn't only the bugs; it's your false confidence about them.

There is a countercurrent too. A (possibly unreleased) frontier security model — referred to as "Mythos" in lecture — reportedly found a 15-year-old vulnerability in OpenBSD, a system built for high security. If true, that's a "narrow superintelligence" for security: super-human on one specific task even if not on others. The professor's honest stance: nobody outside a handful of companies knows if the marketing is exaggerated, and a model strong at security may not generalize that skill elsewhere.

A New Attack: Prompt Injection

Attackers plant malicious instructions in web pages or Stack-Overflow-style answers. A coding agent browsing the web reads them and may run a shell command that opens a backdoor. This is why you should not run an agent in full auto mode — recent harnesses add an LLM "judge" to screen commands, but that screen is itself only as good as the LLM doing the judging.

The Maintainability Collapse

The headline study (the "cursor" study) used a clean natural experiment: you can date when a repo adopted the Cursor agent by when its .cursorrules file appears, then compare against similar repos that didn't adopt it.

1

Right after adoption, lines added per month jumped 3–5× — a massive productivity surge.

2

The surge lasted only about 60 days (1–2 months), then almost entirely disappeared.

3

Eventually development slowed below the pre-AI baseline. The early speed was borrowed against the future.

4

The cause: a 30% rise in static-analysis warnings and a 41.6% rise in code complexity. The AI wrote so much unmaintainable code that it became a burden — e.g. inlining CSS straight into HTML with no separation.

Key Concept — Code as a Liability

Every line of code you write is a burden on your future maintainability. An agent that blindly emits code without regard for architecture adds liability faster than it adds value. A March study found >15% of commits from each AI assistant introduced at least one issue (code smell or security), and 24.2% of those issues survived into the latest production version.

A fair counter (raised by a student, Nathan): what is the comparable human defect rate? The professor agrees this is the right question — the real bar is "as good as a human," not "perfect," the way Waymo is judged safe because it crashes less often than human drivers, not never. He also names the AI productivity paradox: the old loop was 2 hours coding / 6 hours debugging; the new one can be 5 minutes generating / 24 hours debugging. Prototypes race to 75%, then each re-prompt buys diminishing returns (80%, 85%, 90%) while consuming the time a careful engineer would have spent reaching 100%.

Cognitive Offloading and Skill Formation

The result the professor — as an educator — finds most important. In a controlled experiment, developers solved tasks with and without AI, then were quizzed on the libraries they'd used.

Cognitive Offloading

AI users were more productive but learned less — they let AI do the work (cognitive offloading) and built no skill. Crucially, a different interaction style reversed this: the contextual-inquiry approach — treating AI as a tutor ("explain how I can do this effectively") rather than as a junior dev who hands you the answer — gave the productivity gain and grew skills more than working without AI at all. How you interact matters more than whether you do.

Accelerator, Not Leveler

Google's DORA report found AI is not a leveler of skills — it is an accelerator. High-performing teams with strong foundational practices get a much bigger boost; struggling teams have their dysfunction magnified. In an expert's hands AI is powerful; in a novice's hands it creates the illusion of competence and causes more problems than it solves. Build skills now so you sit on the side of the curve that benefits.

Part IV

Software Engineering Techniques to Tame AI

Everything so far diagnoses the problem; now we cure it. The professor's framing question is sharp: imagine a teammate who is fast but unreliable and ships buggy code. The techniques that tame that human are exactly the techniques that tame AI — and you already learned them in this course.

AI as Your Pair Programmer: Driver & Navigator

Treat AI the way you'd treat a pair-programming partner — building the system with you, not for you. There are two roles:

You as Driver (AI navigates)You as Navigator (AI drives)
You write the code. Recommended for homework. Then ask AI to critique it: "scan this code for problems," "look for performance issues." You get TA-quality feedback instantly and grow your skills.AI writes the code; you stay in charge, review critically, and ask it to explain: "explain the algorithm you used here." Common in industry.
You Can't Navigate What You Can't Drive

You cannot meaningfully advise AI on something you can't do yourself. Supervise from a position of strength, not weakness. This is why the professor recommends students drive their own homework and use AI as a tutor — protecting skill formation from Part III.

Models as a Shared, Sound-ish Abstraction

Recall the sound-abstraction problem: you can't predict an LLM's raw output. The fix is to interact at a higher level of abstraction where you can verify the intent — and the lecture's prime example is the UML class diagram.

Instead of prompting "implement a Monopoly board game," prompt for the design first, then the code:

# Step 1 — get the design, not the code
Implement a Monopoly board game with the player as a State pattern.
Before writing any code, first show me a class diagram.

# Step 2 — only after you approve the diagram
Now implement this. Use React and TypeScript;
use Playwright for end-to-end tests.
Why UML Works So Well

UML is a huge portion of the training data, so the model produces and implements class diagrams extremely accurately. Crucially, a class diagram is a shared high-level representation you both understand: instead of reviewing a million lines, you review one diagram and say "this is exactly the design I wanted." Whatever the problem, ask: what shared abstraction lets me talk to AI above the code?

Tests First: TDD and Test-Driven Generation

The fast-but-unreliable teammate — human or AI — is tamed the same way: lots of tests. Two distinct techniques:

Key Concept — Test-Driven Development (TDD) with AI

You write the tests first, then prompt the AI to make them pass. The prompt that works in the professor's experience: "I am using TDD. Implement a general solution that passes test X. Do not change the test."

The Two Cheats You Must Forbid

Both halves of that prompt are load-bearing. (1) "Do not change the test" — otherwise the easiest way to pass a failing test is to delete it. (2) "general solution" — Kent Beck (TDD's inventor) found AI would otherwise build a dictionary mapping each test input to its expected output and pass every test without solving anything. Review the code to confirm it's neither a giant lookup table nor a quietly-deleted test.

Key Concept — Test-Driven Generation (TDG)

Distinct from TDD: you force test-driven thinking onto the AI. (1) Ask it to generate black-box tests from the specification first — this is how you get around the black box. (2) Spend most of your review on whether those tests adequately capture the spec. (3) Then ask it to generate the implementation; then (4) explicitly activate a refactoring step (it won't refactor on its own). (5) Review the implementation for overfitting to the tests. Research shows merely giving the model tests during generation improves correctness.

Architecture: Information Hiding Works on AI Too

Why does software architecture affect AI's effectiveness? Recall information hiding — designing modules so a developer can change one part without understanding the whole, reducing the cognitive load of a change.

Same Trick, Same Win

If a human doesn't have to read a million lines to make a change, neither does the AI. Information hiding cuts the context the agent must load. Google's study of ~1,000 developers found teams with loosely coupled architectures got 20–30% productivity gains from AI, while tightly coupled teams got little or none. AI is bad at evaluating information hiding (there's no training data for that) — that judgment is your skill, and it's what will define your success.

Part V

Prompting, Skills, and the New Bottleneck

The final part is about operating the agent day to day: how to package your expertise so the agent reuses it (skill files), the prompt patterns that consistently help, and which human skills now matter most once development stops being the bottleneck.

Agent Skills: Packaging Your Expertise

An agent skill is a lightweight, open format (pioneered by Anthropic) for extending an agent's capabilities with specialized knowledge and workflows. Concretely, a skill is roughly a Markdown file giving the agent extra instructions — for example, "whenever you write tests, follow the principles of good test design from my CS 130 lecture." A skill can also bundle tools (e.g. a Python file that calls an API) and reference files.

Key Concept — Progressive Disclosure of Skills

Each skill carries a short description (a few sentences) that the agent loads up front. That description tells the agent when the skill applies. Only when it decides the skill is relevant does it load the full skill body — which in turn tells it when to load additional reference files. This minimizes context pollution: you can define hundreds of skills, and the agent autonomously loads only the small portion each task needs.

An Agent Is Not One Agent

The goal isn't one agent that does everything. Partition agents and skills so that any given task loads only a small relevant slice. Done well, this genuinely reduces the context the agent processes; done poorly, it just saves you some typing without helping the agent. Note: an agent can learn faster than a junior dev only via skill files you write — its own per-task learning rate is lower than a human junior's.

Prompting Patterns That Work

The professor frames prompt engineering as applied requirements engineering — describing requirements specifically, with clear success criteria. The patterns:

1

Role + Task + Context. "Act as an expert software engineer with expertise in Python" correlates the output with high-quality examples. Then give a concrete task. Then give useful context — more is better, up to the point where too much pollutes the window.

2

User stories (INVEST). Feeding well-formed user stories with clear acceptance criteria works because the model has read enormous numbers of user stories.

3

Design-decision prompts. "Describe the pros, cons, and common consequences of these design alternatives." The professor's favorite: let AI process vast documentation quickly, then you make the decision.

4

Question prompts. "Before you start, ask me any questions about important decisions and requirements." Surfaces things you'd have missed, reducing rework. (Variant: "ask me ordered yes/no questions.")

5

Replace-TODO-with-prompt. Put the task in the code, not the chat: stub interfaces, scatter TODOs, then "resolve all remaining TODOs in this file; ask me before changing other code." This keeps the architecture in your hands.

Plan Before A Thousand Lines

Use plan mode (or do it manually): ask the agent to explain its proposed approach and take your feedback before it writes a thousand lines. Injecting feedback early is far cheaper than reviewing — and re-prompting — a huge generated diff.

The New Bottleneck Is the Old Skills

For decades, development was the bottleneck, so that's where we built skills. Now development is fast, so the bottleneck has moved — to delegation and agent orchestration, verification and quality control, product understanding, requirements and specification, and architecture and system design.

Not a Coincidence

Almost every one of those new bottleneck skills is the focus of this class. The future, per the GitHub CEO, "belongs to developers who can model systems, anticipate edge cases, and translate ambiguity into structure" — exactly the durable skills AI lacks. You are always the senior engineer responsible for the AI's output; your job is to read, verify, and understand the code.

Putting It All Together

The five parts form one continuous argument. Part I established the ceiling: because an LLM is an unsound abstraction, it can't deliver the compiler's 10× — it speeds development (accidental complexity) but leaves the essential challenges of requirements, design, and quality assurance to you. Part II explained why it's unsound: a next-token "statistical parrot" reproduces the distribution of its training data, which is exactly why it hallucinates APIs, ships outdated code, and excels only on well-trodden problems.

Part III cashed those mechanics out as concrete risk — insecure code that feels secure, a maintainability collapse that turns a 3–5× surge into below-baseline output within 60 days, and cognitive offloading that quietly erodes the very skills that make you valuable. Part IV answered with discipline you already own: treat AI as an unreliable pair partner, raise the conversation to a verifiable shared abstraction (UML), pin down behavior with TDD and TDG, and design with information hiding so the agent — like a human — needs less context. Part V operationalized all of it: package your expertise as skills, prompt as applied requirements engineering, and recognize that the new bottleneck skills are precisely the ones this course teaches.

The One Sentence to Remember

AI amplifies whatever you bring to it: in an expert's hands it is leverage, in a novice's it is an illusion of competence. So your job — and your safest career bet — is to keep building the durable engineering skills (architecture, design, testing, requirements) that let you supervise AI from a position of strength.
