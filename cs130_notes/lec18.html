Designing Testable Software & Testing Quality Attributes

An interactive guide to Lecture 18 — testability, test doubles, chaos engineering at Netflix, architectural tactics, and how to test reliability, performance, security & availability across real-world domains.

Earlier lectures asked: does my code do the right thing? We tested functionality — given an input, did we get the expected output? This lecture asks two harder questions. First, how do we design software so that it is even possible to test it well? And second, how do we test the non-functional goodness of a system — is it reliable, fast, secure, and available — when the answer to "is it correct?" is no longer a simple yes-or-no?

Why this lecture exists

This is a brand-new lecture, added because of where industry is heading. As AI takes over more of the coding and design work, the professor argues the human developer's job shifts toward requirements engineering and quality assurance. If you want a job titled "software engineer," this is the part of the work that stays human — and that this lecture trains you for.

Part I

What Is Testability?

Why Testability Beats Testing

Think of building a car. To unit-test the engine, you don't try to test it while it's bolted into the chassis surrounded by everything else. You remove the engine and put it on a test bed where you can drive it with exactly the inputs you want and watch exactly the outputs you care about. That removability — that designed-in ease of isolating and exercising a component — is the heart of what we mean by testability.

Here is the key reframing the professor wants you to internalize: testing is nice, but testability is better. A test, on its own, does nothing to your software's quality. If a test never finds a bug, it has had zero impact on the code. What testing buys you is confidence. But when you design for testability, you almost always end up with a better design overall — fewer dependencies, smaller interfaces, more understandable components — even setting testing aside entirely.

Key Concept — Testability

A quality attribute that defines the degree to which the functionality and quality attributes of a system or component can be assessed via automated runtime observability. Informally: how easily can we make the system reveal its faults? and how hard is it to write effective tests?

The Two Pillars: Controllability & Observability

Testability is not one thing — it decomposes into two distinct challenges, each with its own separate solutions. A test always has two jobs: first drive the system into the situation you want to examine, then look at what it does. Those map onto the two pillars.

Key Concept — Controllability

How easy it is to provide a program or component with the needed inputs, values, operations, or behavior, and bring it into the desired state. This is the setup side of a test — getting the system into the situation you want to assess.

Key Concept — Observability

How easy it is to observe the behavior of a program in terms of outputs, quality attributes, and effects on the environment or other components. This is the assertion side of a test — being able to see what the system did.

A neat way to remember it: controllability is about writing a good setup; observability is about writing good assertions. They are genuinely separate. Sometimes the hard part is getting the system into the right state (controllability); sometimes the hard part is seeing what it did (observability). A pure mathematical function is trivial on both — you pass an argument (control) and read the return value (observe). Real-world systems — web apps returning HTML, robots interacting with the physical world — are hard on both.

A test drives the system in (controllability) and reads its behavior out (observability).

Part II

Test Doubles — Faking Your Dependencies

Builds On Part I

The two pillars from Part I aren't abstract. Test doubles are the concrete tools that give you controllability and observability when your system talks to a component you don't control.

The Problem: A Dependent-On Component

Recall the Global Distribution System (GDS) — the system that lets almost all airlines and booking systems talk to each other. Suppose you're writing your own booking system. Your booking system is the System Under Test (SUT), but it relies on GDS as a separate component you don't control. A more modern example: you're building an app on top of OpenAI's or Anthropic's API. You depend on a separate service you can't fully control.

Why does that break testing? Because real calls cost real money and have real effects. Do you want to pay ten dollars every time you run your test suite? Do you want your trading-system tests to place actual buy and sell orders on the stock exchange? Obviously not. So we must replace the real dependency with a fake.

Key Vocabulary — Indirect Inputs / Outputs

Direct inputs are what the test hands the SUT directly (like the arguments to a function). When the SUT sends a request to the dependent-on component, those are indirect outputs — the test can't see them directly. What the SUT receives back from that component are indirect inputs. They're "indirect" because they flow between the SUT and another component, outside the test's direct view (e.g. HTTP requests).

This gives us a clean mapping. There are three test doubles, each solving a different combination of "which direction?" and "which pillar?"

Test DoublePillarWhat it does
Mock objectObservabilityVerifies indirect outputs are well-formed via assertions inside it.
Test spyObservabilityRecords indirect outputs; the test later asks for the history and asserts on it (e.g. ordering).
Test stubControllabilityInjects indirect inputs by returning hard-coded values.

Mock Object — Asserting Inside the Fake

Suppose you want to check that your booking system sends well-formed GDS requests, or that your trading system sends well-formed buy/sell orders — without firing real ones. The mock object replaces the dependent-on component with an object that has the same interface but no real implementation. Instead, it contains assertion statements. When the SUT calls it, the mock checks that what it received is well-formed.

1

Your test injects a mock object in place of the real GDS / API client.

2

The SUT runs and sends its indirect outputs (requests) to the mock.

3

Assertions inside the mock fire, checking each request is well-formed.

Practical Note

You rarely write mocks by hand. Many testing frameworks support this — you just decide to use a mock object and write the assertions. The framework does the plumbing.

Test Spy — Recording, Then Asserting

Now change the goal. You no longer just want well-formed requests — you want them sent in the right order. Buy then sell, not sell then buy. Can you just put ordering assertions inside the mock? Awkwardly, no — ordering is a property across calls, not within one call.

The fix is the test spy. Instead of asserting on the spot, the spy records every request it receives and exposes a way to retrieve the full history. The assertions then live in your test, which asks the spy "give me the list of everything you received" and checks the sequence.

Mock vs. Spy — Where the Assertions Live

The whole distinction is location of the assertions. In a mock, assertions live inside the double and fire immediately. In a spy, the double just collects data and the assertions live in the test, after the fact. And yes — a spy can also carry well-formedness assertions on top of its recording, so you can stack both patterns on one object.

Test Stub — Injecting Indirect Inputs

The first two are observability tools. The test stub is the controllability tool. Here the question flips: how does your SUT behave when the dependent-on component sends it certain inputs? What if GDS is offline, or returns an error code instead of a real response? How does the LLM client handle a weird API response?

A stub replaces the dependent-on component with something that simply returns hard-coded values — exactly the indirect inputs you want to inject. In a normal test you'd supply inputs straight from the test; but you can't reach indirect inputs that way, so the stub stands in and feeds them to the SUT. A stub might proactively send a request, or it might hard-code a return value in response to a request — depending on which kind of indirect input you're simulating.

Don't Conflate the Three

In industry, people lump all three under "mock," and mocking libraries usually do all three. The academic umbrella term is test doubles (with fakes and others beyond these three). For the exam, keep them distinct: stub = controllability (inject indirect inputs); mock & spy = observability (verify indirect outputs).

Expand: The fuller controllability & observability checklists

For controllability, the professor's checklist includes: manipulate configuration settings in the test environment (config is part of a component's interface, so test it); easily inject test data and insert stubs; avoid cyclic dependencies — if the component you depend on also depends on you, it becomes nearly impossible to mock or stub them individually; and for systems touching the physical world, use a simulator/emulator to control temperature, wind, etc.

For observability, the checklist asks: can the test access all component states it needs? Note the tension — if any component can read that state, you've violated information hiding. The fix is to expose such state only to tests via the interface, not to other components. Also: are test doubles easy to insert, and are logs generated for all critical events (so you can infer behavior to assert on)?

Part III

Testing Quality Attributes

How to Specify a Quality Requirement: Scenario + Measure

A quality attribute is a degree of "goodness" about a system that is not purely functional — reliability, performance, security, availability, and so on. The trap is writing vague requirements like "the system should be fast" or "the system should be secure." Those are untestable. Every quality requirement needs two precise parts:

Key Concept — Scenario + Measure

A quality requirement is specified as a scenario (what event arrives / what situation holds) plus a measure (the quantified response we demand). Crucially: the scenario maps to controllability — get the system into that situation — and the measure maps to observability — quantify what it does.

This is the unifying idea of the whole lecture: every quality attribute, from reliability to security, reduces to "control the system into a scenario, then observe a measure."

Reliability & Chaos Engineering at Netflix

Reliability is about deviations from the normal operating scenario for which the system should still work correctly. For Netflix, the requirement "watching movies should be reliable" becomes: scenario — the user requests to watch Squid Game even though the network is slow (defined precisely); measure — the percentage of such deviations under which the user still sees the movie instead of an error. Netflix faces constant deviations: high latency, surging load, DDoS, or everyone wanting the one new hit show and nothing else.

So how does Netflix test reliability when a microservice (say, video validation, video encoding, or content analysis) becomes unavailable? They kill it on purpose.

The Big Idea — Chaos Engineering

Chaos engineering randomly (or pseudo-randomly) kills running services to test that the system stays reliable. Netflix is famous for doing this not only in a test environment but in production — their "Chaos Monkey" shuts down live services so developers are forced to build microservices robust enough to survive being killed at any moment.

Why run chaos in production rather than just a test environment? Because a test environment may not generalize: different servers, far smaller scale. The downside of production chaos is real — if a service fails, a customer can't watch their show and might cancel. But the trade-off is worth it because the test environment can't reproduce production's true conditions. In chaos terms: killing a random service is the controllability challenge; checking whether it restarts and how other services are impacted is the observability challenge.

Generalizing Beyond Netflix

For any system, reliability testing = inject deviations with a test stub (kill services, or simulate a component returning error codes), then assert (controllability via the stub; observability via functional assertions plus a heartbeat to confirm recovery, perhaps measuring time-to-recovery). For web apps, the tool Artillery does Chaos-Monkey-style killing and floods your app with requests.

Performance — Latency vs. Throughput

Performance requirements also need scenario + measure: describe what event arrives and what the system response should be, measured as average / min / max latency or throughput (or deadlines).

Key Distinction — Latency vs. Throughput

Latency is the time to execute one single operation/request. Throughput is how many operations complete in a given amount of time. They are different performance properties and you sometimes optimize them separately: a batch system may want high throughput while tolerating high latency per request; an interactive system may demand low latency even if total throughput suffers.

Netflix tests performance with the Latency Monkey (part of their "Simian Army" of tools): they inject latency into components, or slow down the connector between components, then check timeouts and retries. Generalized: inject load or latency via a stub (a component that waits, then forwards the request), stress-test by bombarding the system with requests (Artillery again), and measure execution times with test spies that log timings, then look for bottlenecks.

Threat to Validity — The Heisenberg Effect

Performance testing has a built-in flaw: the act of measuring perturbs what you measure. Injecting latency or logging adds its own overhead on top of the latency you wanted to study, so results may not generalize. The professor calls this a Heisenbug situation — observing the behavior can change it (a bug that vanishes when you look). Sometimes the added delay even helps, e.g. crossing a threshold that triggers auto-scaling. These methods are imperfect but the best we have.

Security & Availability

Security requirements specify the functionality to preserve plus a type of attack, then measure how the system responds — is the attacker prevented, how long to detect, how long to repel? You can't write "the system is secure"; you specify which attack and the response. To test it, you simulate the attack: a red team of security engineers, an automated attack, or — increasingly — an AI agent trying to break the system by injecting malicious input or unauthorized requests. Then observe whether functionality is preserved and whether the attack was detected / prevented from propagating.

Why Security Is the Hardest to Automate

The moment you can name a specific attack, it's probably already outdated. You must keep imagining new kinds of attacks — which is exactly why AI agents that generate novel attacks are a promising tool here.

Availability looks similar to reliability but the measure differs: percentage of uptime, time-to-repair, or time to detect and recover from partial unavailability. The painful problem: how do you test "99.999% availability" (roughly ten hours of downtime per year) without waiting a whole year? You can't. Instead you create the rare events deliberately (high load, injected faults), check responsiveness (ping-echo again), and extrapolate from how often those rare events occur in the real world. This is why availability is so hard to prove that breaking into the "big leagues" of Azure / Google Cloud is largely about reputation for honoring service-level agreements (SLAs).

Availability as a Business Conversation

A business customer (say UCLA buying BruinLearn) tolerates some deviation: maybe one day down per quarter is fine, so they demand a mean-time-to-repair of 24 hours. You either serve only customers happy with the guarantee you can give, or offer tiers — charge a premium customer 1000× for higher availability, and during partial outages preferentially keep them running while lower tiers absorb the downtime.

Part IV

Architectural Tactics

Builds On the Architecture Lecture

Earlier we saw architectural styles — the one or two predominant shapes of your architecture, each trading off quality attributes. You pick a style once or twice and you're largely done. But when your boss says "now make it more reliable" or "more available," you reach for tactics: small, design-pattern-like additions you drop into an existing system to promote one specific quality attribute. They're all catalogued in the architecture textbooks.

Detecting Faults: From Naive Idea to Ping-Echo

Problem: to ensure dependability, detect faults before they escalate to failures by determining whether a component or node has stopped working — exactly the Netflix "is this service down?" question. A first idea (Nathan's in class): every component that makes a request notices when it gets no response. That works, but it shifts the burden onto every single component, bloating each one with detection code.

Key Concept — Ping-Echo Tactic

A dedicated watchdog component regularly sends "ping" requests to all other components and waits for an "echo." If a component fails to respond within a timeout, it's considered offline and a recovery mechanism spins up a replacement. Detection is centralized in one place instead of scattered everywhere.

1

Watchdog sends a ping; healthy component echoes back. All good.

2

Watchdog pings again; this component has stopped working — no echo.

3

Timeout expires → mark offline → recovery component starts a replacement.

Downsides of Ping-Echo

Two problems. First, who watches the watchdog? If the watchdog dies, detection dies. Second, it floods the network: the watchdog sends pings to everyone and every component must spend compute responding — roughly twice the message traffic, hurting performance.

Heartbeat — Same Goal, Fewer Messages

Can we detect failures without the request/response traffic? Yes — invert it.

Key Concept — Heartbeat Tactic

Every component regularly broadcasts an "I'm alive" message on its own. No one sends a request — a listener just watches the heartbeats. If a component's heartbeat doesn't arrive within a timeout, it's considered offline and recovery begins.

AspectPing-EchoHeartbeat
Both…improve availability, inhibit performanceimprove availability, inhibit performance
Message countHigher — request + echo (≈2× traffic)Lower — one-way "I'm alive"
Where logic livesOne component; reuses each component's existing ability to reply to messagesDistributed — every component needs an internal timer + heartbeat subroutine
Turn it on/offEasy — just stop sending pings from the one watchdogHarder — behavior is baked into every component

The trade-off is responsibility vs. performance: ping-echo concentrates the logic in one place (easy to toggle) but sends more messages; heartbeat distributes the logic into every component (more complexity per component) but sends fewer messages.

Real-World Footgun — Heartbleed

OpenSSL's heartbeat implementation had a buffer over-read bug, producing Heartbleed — one of the most famous security bugs of the early 2010s. A reliability tactic, implemented carelessly, became a catastrophic security hole.

Caching — Avoiding Expensive Re-Acquisition

Problem: a resource is very expensive to compute but requested many times. The tactic is caching — temporarily store a local copy. A design choice: a transparent cache sits invisibly in front of the provider (the resource user always talks to the cache, which fetches from the provider only on a miss), versus a cache the user queries explicitly first, falling back to the provider itself on a miss. Each has slightly different trade-offs.

Caching avoids re-paying the cost of an expensive resource on repeated requests.
Where to Read More

The professor's go-to is Software Architecture in Practice (Bass, Clements & Kazman). Also recommended: A System of Patterns (Siemens) and the more practitioner-minded Design It! From Programmer to Software Architect. All three should be free with a UCLA account. The full catalog of tactics lives in these books — this lecture only samples a few.

Part V

Increasing Test Coverage

The Balance: Effort vs. Confidence

Test coverage is always a balance between testing effort and confidence. More tests cost more to write and run. You want to test critical functionality first and have system-integration tests, but you also want enough confidence — 50% coverage is "basically nothing," nobody writes home about it. The smart move is to ask which complex parts deserve more tests and whether more confidence comes from more assertions rather than merely covering more lines (the "mutation gap" idea from an earlier lecture).

Key Vocabulary — Fault, Error, Failure

A fault is the location/line in the code that is buggy. An error is what happens when that line executes and the program enters an incorrect state. A failure is an error that propagates all the way to the system boundary (the user sees it, or wrong data is stored). Fault-tolerant mechanisms work by detecting an error and responding before it becomes a failure.

Why even write unit tests, if system/integration tests give a better effort-to-confidence ratio? Because when an integration test fails, you see the failure but it's hard to pinpoint the fault. Unit tests localize the bug. Now — how do we get high coverage without hand-writing a gazillion input/output cases? Two big ideas: random generation, and asserting relationships instead of exact outputs.

Monkey Testing & Fuzzing

Imagine a web app with decent tests. Users can click anything in any order. Writing one Playwright script per scenario is hopelessly tedious — you'll run out of patience after ten. The fix: stop relying on your mental model of "what the user should do" and just generate random inputs.

Key Concept — Monkey Testing

Put a "monkey" in front of your UI: randomly trigger any possible system events, sampling from the whole input space, avoiding repetition, trying to break the system. Then check for crashes or for your in-code assertions being violated. This is exactly why dense assertions pay off — with many assertions, random inputs alone give you very high effective coverage.

Monkey Testing vs. Fuzzing

Same idea, different level. Monkey testing generates random UI events. Fuzzing generates random inputs of any kind (not just UI). Both lean on the principle that "the quality of your code is measured by the density of assertions in it" — a rough but cheap proxy for how much you can trust the code.

But monkey testing alone isn't enough: it only catches crashes and assertion violations. It won't verify the system computes the right number. For that you still need real input/output oracles — or the next technique.

Metamorphic Testing — Asserting Relationships

Sometimes a test oracle (the mapping from input to expected output) is very expensive or impossible to write. What's the expected output of an image recognizer for a given photo? What are all the digits of pi? You can't enumerate those. But you may know relationships that must always hold, even without knowing any single exact answer.

Key Concept — Metamorphic Testing

Generate random inputs sampling the whole input space (like fuzzing), but instead of checking crashes, assert metamorphic relationships — system-level properties that hold for all inputs. Example: even if you don't know sin(3.51), you know sin(π − x) == sin(x) for every x. Feed in huge, tiny, and zero values and check the relation holds throughout.

Metamorphic relationships are everywhere once you look. A few from the lecture:

1

Round-trip: USD→EUR then EUR→USD (at one instant) should return the original amount, even though you don't know the exact rate.

2

Robustness: add minor noise to an image; object recognition should still recognize it.

3

Subset: filtering an online shop by price or store ID should return a subset of the unfiltered list.

4

Netflix examples: the same search query should return the same results regardless of user profile (though order may vary, and age restrictions might break it); or a watched movie shouldn't reappear in recommendations (a property you might choose to enforce).

Why Metamorphic Testing Alone Isn't Enough

It can be fooled by systematically wrong implementations. If your sin is always off by one, or your tax is always off by $100, or object recognition never recognizes anything — the metamorphic relation may still hold (you took a "shortcut through the oracle") and the bug slips through. You still need some real input/output tests to anchor correctness.

Watch Your Ranges

Relationships often hold only on sub-ranges. "More income → more tax" may fail below a tax-free threshold (say $20k) or for negative income. Specify the ranges and conditions under which your metamorphic relation actually applies.

Test-Driven Development

If you follow TDD — never writing a line of production code without first writing a test for it — you naturally end up near 100% coverage: guaranteed 100% statement coverage, usually 100% branch coverage, often 100% path coverage. It's a discipline that bakes high coverage in by construction.

Part VI

Testing Across Domains

The Big Idea — "It Depends"

In software engineering, the correct answer to 90% of questions is "it depends." Your testing approach must be tailored to the domain — web apps, mobile, robotics, or a helicopter destined for Mars each pose different controllability and observability challenges.

Web Apps & Playwright

Web apps are hard on both pillars. Controllability: you can never fully predict how a user will behave — user interaction is the wild card, and there are endless ways to interact. Observability: your output is an HTML page, which is hard to assert on. You must simulate clicks, text entry, and page loads, then verify output that could be anywhere on the page — and if you over-fit assertions to exact pixels, any CSS or UI change breaks your tests.

Playwright is the state-of-the-art framework that addresses exactly these challenges. For controllability you navigate to a URL and query inputs by role or ID, fill text, and click links — simulating real user interaction. For observability you assert on visibility (text should be not just present but actually visible, since JavaScript can hide it) or on a locator having certain text, substring, or message count.

// Playwright — controllability: drive the page
await page.goto('https://example.com/login');
await page.getByRole('textbox', { name: 'Email' })
          .fill('user@example.com');
await page.getByRole('link',    { name: 'Submit' }).click();

// observability: assert on visibility / text
await expect(page.getByText('Welcome')).toBeVisible();
Practical Advice For Your Project

If you're building a web app, use Playwright — and combine it with Artillery for reliability/load testing. There's a Playwright tutorial in the course e-book. Concepts transfer even to companies' internal in-house frameworks, so learning it pays off well beyond this class.

Robotics, Self-Driving Cars & Mobile

For space/robotic systems, controllability is brutal. To test the Mars helicopter (Ingenuity), engineers used a pressure chamber with the atmosphere removed and a spring to offset gravity — physically recreating Mars. It translated to Mars, though not 100%. The recurring problem is the simulation gap: simulated sensor/video input doesn't fully match the real world, and the input space (any image, any video, any sensor reading) is enormous.

One mitigation: record real-world events and replay them — in the Robot Operating System, "bag files" record events to play back later. That lowers testing effort but only covers the events you actually recorded. Cautionary tales abound: a Volvo self-driving car assumed animals always touch the ground (true for Nordic moose and deer) and then misjudged kangaroos in Australia. Holding self-driving cars to the same safety standards as comparable systems would require driving every road on Earth eight times before each release — infeasible — which is why robotic systems are so hard to test.

Key Concept — Mobile: Monkey Testing & Device Fragmentation

Monkey testing is hugely popular on mobile; Android and iOS each ship their own monkey frameworks. iOS is easier because devices are uniform. Android adds a controllability challenge: countless hardware configs (different GPUs, sensors, system setups), so testing means uploading your APK to clouds of real devices across thousands of configurations (e.g. Google's cloud testing) — effective but expensive.

Putting It All Together

Every idea in this lecture hangs from a single hook introduced in Part I: testability = controllability + observability. Once you see that, the rest of the lecture is the same two-pillar pattern applied at ever-larger scope.

In Part II, the pillars became concrete tools. A test stub gives you controllability by injecting indirect inputs; a mock object and a test spy give you observability by verifying indirect outputs (well-formedness vs. ordering). In Part III, the very definition of a quality requirement — scenario + measure — turned out to be the two pillars wearing different clothes: the scenario is something you control the system into, the measure is something you observe. Reliability, performance, security, and availability are all just different scenario/measure pairs, and Netflix's Chaos Monkey, Latency Monkey, and Simian Army are industrial-strength ways of controlling rare scenarios so the system's response can be observed.

Part IV stepped back from testing to design: tactics like ping-echo and heartbeat are how you build the availability that Part III demands, and they themselves are validated using Part II's stubs (kill a service, watch it recover). Part V attacked the cost of testing head-on: monkey testing and fuzzing crank up controllability by generating inputs randomly; metamorphic testing rescues observability when you can't write an oracle, by asserting relationships instead of exact answers. And Part VI drove home the field's mantra — "it depends" — showing that the same two pillars demand wildly different machinery for a web app (Playwright), a Mars helicopter (a pressure chamber), and an Android fleet (cloud device farms).

The Through-Line

Testing gives confidence; testability gives a better design. The closing observation seals it: systems that are easy to test are also easy to change — both come from the same discipline of fewer dependencies and smaller interfaces. High testability ⇄ high changeability. Designing for one gives you the other for free.
