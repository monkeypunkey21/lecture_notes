Convolutional Networks & the Two Faces of AI

The course finale — how CNNs see images through convolution and pooling, and the deep divide between model-free learning and model-based reasoning.

Part I

Convolution — A Neuron That Roams

Why Are We Learning This?

Of all the places neural networks have changed the world, the loudest is computer vision — getting a computer to look at the raw pixels of an image and say "that's a cat," "that's a stop sign," "that's a tumor." The architecture that made this possible is the convolutional neural network, or CNN. A second family, the recurrent neural network (RNN), powers sequence-to-sequence tasks like speech recognition and machine translation, but our focus today is the CNN, because it is conceptually clean and it lets us see exactly how a network learns to perceive.

Here is the striking thing the professor wants you to notice up front: by the end of this, you will see how little algorithmic machinery a CNN actually contains compared to the search, logic, and probabilistic reasoning we spent the term building. Almost the whole structure reduces to two simple ideas, repeated:

Key Concept — The Two Pillars of a CNN

(1) Local detection of features. A small detector looks at one little patch of the image at a time and reports whether some pattern — an edge, a corner, later a whole eye or wheel — is present there. (2) Aggregation / abstraction. A second kind of layer summarizes those local findings, throwing away precise position to keep "is the feature roughly here?" A CNN simply alternates these two operations, and finishes with a fully connected layer that makes the final decision.

The Filter: A Neuron Described as a Matrix

Start with the simplest input: a small grayscale image, say 6×6. Each cell is a pixel, and the number in it is the brightness (the grayscale level). So the image is just a 6×6 matrix of numbers.

Now recall what a single neuron does: it has a list of inputs, multiplies each input by a corresponding weight, sums them, and passes the result through an activation function. A CNN's central trick is to point one neuron not at the whole image but at a small 3×3 patch of it. That neuron then has exactly 3×3 = 9 inputs and 9 weights. Instead of drawing nine wires, we lay those nine weights out as a 3×3 matrix and call it a filter.

Key Concept — Filter (Kernel)

A filter is a small matrix of weights — here 3×3 — that represents one feature-detecting neuron. You overlay it on a patch of the image, multiply each weight by the pixel underneath it, sum everything up, and that single number is the neuron's output for that patch.

The word convolution names the act of taking this filter and sliding it across every patch of the image, computing one output number per position. The filter "roams" over the image. That is the whole operation.

Convolution: the highlighted 3×3 patch, weighted by the filter, produces the single highlighted output cell. Slide the patch over all positions to fill the 4×4 feature map.

Computing One Output Number

Let us trace exactly what happens when the filter sits on the top-left 3×3 patch. We multiply each pixel by the filter weight sitting on top of it, then add up all nine products.

1

Line up the filter on the patch. Each of the nine pixels gets paired with the weight directly above it. Pixel 3 pairs with weight +1, pixel 0 pairs with weight +1, and so on.

2

Multiply each pair. First row contributes 3·(+1) + 0·(+1) + .... A whole row of zero pixels contributes zero. The bottom row contributes the negative weights, e.g. 2·(−1) + 7·(−1).

3

Sum the nine products into a single number. (Normally you would now apply the activation function — ReLU, which clamps negatives to zero — but here we just record the raw sum to keep the arithmetic visible.)

4

Write that number into the output's top-left cell. Then slide the filter one step right and repeat. Sliding over all valid positions of a 6×6 image with a 3×3 filter yields a 4×4 grid of numbers.

That 4×4 grid is itself a matrix — the feature map. Crucially, every one of its 16 cells was produced by the same filter, i.e. the same nine weights, applied at a different location. So instead of 16 independent neurons, you have one neuron used 16 times. This weight-sharing is exactly why a CNN can detect "an edge" no matter where in the image the edge appears.

Where Do the Weights Come From?

Originally, engineers hand-picked filter values known to highlight, say, vertical edges or horizontal edges. The breakthrough was realizing the filter is just a neuron's weights — so we don't need to choose them. We let gradient descent learn them automatically from the loss function. The network discovers which patterns are worth detecting.

Part II

The Three Knobs — Filter Size, Padding, Stride

The basic convolution has three dials you, the designer, must set. They are called hyperparameters: values not learned by gradient descent but chosen ahead of time, usually by following community "common wisdom" about what has worked before rather than deriving them from scratch.

Key Concept — Hyperparameter

A hyperparameter is a setting of the model's structure or training that you fix before learning, rather than something the optimizer tunes. For convolution the three core ones are F = filter size, P = padding, and S = stride.

Filter Size (F)

Our example used F = 3 (a 3×3 filter). Common choices are 3×3 or 4×4. The bigger the filter, the more the output shrinks: a larger window has fewer valid positions to sit in. Each filter also carries an intuition — one set of nine numbers might fire on vertical edges, another on horizontal edges, another on some slanted or more abstract pattern. The output feature map, if you visualized it as an image, would have those very patterns highlighted.

Padding (P) and "Same" Convolution

Plain convolution shrinks the image (6×6 → 4×4) and quietly under-weights the edges: a corner pixel only ever sits in one filter position, while a central pixel sits in nine. Padding fixes both problems by ringing the image with extra border pixels (usually zeros) before convolving.

Adding a one-pixel border (P = 1) turns the 6×6 image into 8×8; a 3×3 convolution then yields a 6×6 output — the input size is preserved.

When the output matches the input size, we call it a same convolution. There is a formula relating F and P that tells you exactly how much padding preserves the dimension under a given filter; for F = 3, a padding of P = 1 does it. Larger filters need more padding to stay "same."

Stride (S)

So far the filter moved one step at a time — a stride of 1. With a stride of 2, the filter jumps two cells right each move, and two cells down at the end of a row. It skips positions, so it produces far fewer outputs.

Key Concept — Stride

The stride S is how many pixels the filter shifts between applications. A larger stride samples the image more coarsely, producing a smaller output. Stride 1 (overlapping windows) and stride 2 are both common.

The professor's quick quiz to the class: if you increase the stride, what happens to the output's height and width? The answer is that the output shrinks. Bigger stride means fewer landing spots, hence a smaller feature map. So both larger filters and larger strides reduce dimensions; padding pushes the other way, growing them back.

Part III

Volumes, Many Filters, and Pooling

Real images are in color, and real networks detect more than one pattern. Generalizing convolution along both of those directions is what turns the toy example into an actual CNN. The unifying idea: every layer takes a volume in and produces another volume out.

Color Images Are Volumes

A grayscale image is a single matrix. A color image needs three matrices stacked — one each for red, green, and blue. Each pixel now holds three numbers, so we say the image has 3 channels. A 6×6 color image is a 6×6×3 volume: height × width × channels.

To convolve a volume, the filter must also gain a third dimension equal to the number of channels. A 3×3 filter over a 3-channel image is really 3×3×3 = 27 weights. It roams in 3D over little cubes of the input. At each position it sums all 27 products into one number — so it collapses the three channels into one. The output of a single such filter is again a flat 4×4 feature map.

A 3×3×3 filter convolves a 3-channel volume and collapses the channels, producing a flat 4×4 map. The filter's depth must always match the input's channel count.

Multiple Filters Build a New Volume

One filter detects one kind of pattern. But to recognize a cat, you need to spot edges and textures and eyes and ears — many features. So you use many filters, each its own 3×3×3 cube of weights, each producing its own 4×4 map. Stack those maps and you have a new volume.

Convolve a 6×6×3 input with 2 filters and you get a 4×4×2 output. The "2" is no longer channels of color — it is the number of filters. Use 16 filters and you get 4×4×16. Add padding P = 1 with those 16 filters and you preserve the spatial size: 6×6×16. Increase the stride and the spatial size shrinks again.

The Big Idea

Every CNN layer is the same shape of operation: take a volume, convolve it with a bank of filters, get another volume. The depth of the output volume equals the number of filters you used. Stacking such layers — volume to volume to volume — is essentially the entire architecture.

Weight-Sharing, Again

Within that 4×4×2 output, the 16 orange cells are all the same neuron (the first filter) applied at 16 positions; the 16 yellow cells are all the second neuron. So 32 output values are really just two distinct sets of weights. This is why CNNs have so few parameters relative to their power — and it is the structural reason they generalize across position.

Max Pooling — The Aggregation Layer

Convolution handled "local detection." The second pillar — aggregation/abstraction — is delivered by max pooling. It looks similar to convolution (a window roaming over the input) but with a crucial difference: it has no weights. Over each window it simply outputs the maximum value.

Max pooling over a 2×2 block keeps only the largest value (30). It has no learnable weights — it just summarizes "the strongest response in this region."

Why keep only the max? Because once a filter has detected "there's a strong vertical edge somewhere in this corner," we mostly care that it's there, not the exact pixel. Max pooling throws away precise position, shrinks the volume, and makes the representation a little more abstract and a little more robust to small shifts. Pooling acts on each channel independently, so it changes height and width but not depth.

The Whole Architecture in One Breath

Now you can read a CNN end to end. The image enters as a volume. It passes through a convolution layer (local feature detection) producing a new volume, then a pooling layer (aggregation) producing a smaller volume, then convolution, then pooling — alternating the two pillars, getting deeper (more filters) and spatially smaller as it goes. Finally the volume feeds a fully connected layer that outputs the answer (cat / not-cat). And remember — all of those filter weights are learned by gradient descent. That conceptual simplicity, against everything CNNs can do, is exactly what "gives people pause."

Part IV

Model-Free vs Model-Based AI

This is where the lecture zooms out from CNNs to a question that frames the whole field — and the whole course. CNNs are powerful, but they have a specific character, and naming that character lets us see both their gifts and their failures clearly.

What "Model-Free" Means

A neural network is model-free. You hand it labeled data — images tagged "cat" or "no cat" — set up an architecture, run gradient descent, and out comes a classifier. At no point did you tell it what a cat is. You never wrote down "a cat is an animal with a head shaped like this and a tail like that." The network just fits a function from pixels to labels. People also call this function fitting or curve fitting.

Key Concept — Model-Free vs Model-Based

Model-free systems (neural nets) carry no knowledge of the phenomenon they predict; they extract patterns straight from data. Model-based systems (logic, Bayesian networks, the search and reasoning we studied all term) encode explicit knowledge — variables, relationships, structure — and then reason over it.

This Connects the Whole Course

Nearly everything before today — propositional and first-order logic, search, constraint satisfaction, Bayesian networks — was model-based: we represented knowledge and reasoned with it. CNNs are the course's clearest example of the opposite pole. Holding both in view is the point of this final lecture.

Three Weaknesses of the Model-Free Approach

Because they lean entirely on data and carry no model of the world, model-free systems inherit three much-discussed weaknesses.

1

Hungry for data. The only thing the network feeds on is data — and usually labeled data. Not hundreds of examples, but millions and beyond. For some domains that data exists; for many it simply does not.

2

Brittle / not robust. Even though they beat everything before them, they are surprisingly easy to break. Tiny changes to an input that you and I would never even notice can make the network's answer collapse.

3

Not transparent / hard to explain. We have intuitions about what they do, but they are not interpretable. When a network declines a loan, asking "why?" is genuinely hard to answer — and in sensitive domains, accountability and liability demand an answer. This is the field of explainable AI.

All three trace back to the same root: these systems are model-free, so they have no concept to point to when asked to justify, generalize, or learn from few examples.

Brittleness in Practice

The famous example: a network trained to recognize stop signs works beautifully — until someone sticks a small yellow sticker on the sign, and the network can no longer recognize it. To a human the sign is obviously still a stop sign. To the network, which never learned that "a stop sign is red, octagonal, and reads STOP," the perturbation is catastrophic. Changing an image's colors in particular ways can flip the label entirely.

Adversarial Attacks

Deliberately crafting tiny input changes to fool a network is an adversarial attack. There is an arms race: attackers find perturbations that flip the output, and defenders try to train and structure networks to resist them. The vulnerability exists precisely because the model is fitting patterns, not understanding objects.

"It Fits the Data, It Doesn't Learn the Concept"

When gradient descent reports "I minimized the loss," it means the network fit the training data well — not that it grasped the underlying concept. If the data isn't representative, or the test distribution differs, the network can fall apart, because it was never reasoning about what the object truly is.

Part V

Injecting Knowledge — Model-Based Learning

If model-free systems suffer from being knowledge-blind, the natural fix is to give them knowledge. This is the professor's own research area, and the closing technical idea of the course: model-based supervised learning, where you do the same task as a CNN but bake in what you know about the world.

From a Model to a Circuit

Take a concrete task: an image contains a main rectangle plus noise, and you want to predict properties of that rectangle — is it tall or wide, its height, width, and the position of its upper-left corner. A CNN would just learn this from labels. The model-based alternative instead writes down a model: a rectangle has a height, a width, a corner row and column, a shape that is a function of height and width, and these together generate the pixels.

Once you have such a model — often a Bayesian network where each node is a known function of its parents — you can compile it into a circuit (an arithmetic circuit, AC) whose inputs are the pixels and whose output is the prediction. That circuit can then be trained on labeled data just like a neural network, but it carries the structure of the model inside it.

Key Concept — BN + BK

BN + BK = a Bayesian Network plus Background Knowledge. You specify not just the network's variables but the precise functional relationships between each node and its parents — injecting every piece of knowledge you have about the model directly into the compiled circuit, rather than leaving those relationships to be guessed from data.

A seven-segment digit recognizer is built the same way, just bigger: a digit is a composition of seven segments, each segment is on or off as a function of which digit (0–9) it is, and each segment generates its own pixels. Compile that model into an AC and you get a digit classifier with real structure.

The two costs of going model-based

Model-based learning isn't free. Two hard barriers keep it from scaling the way CNNs have:

1. Compilation is expensive. Turning a model into a circuit is computationally involved and genuinely hard — even being able to compile these particular models is the fruit of very recent algorithmic results. This is the "heavy algorithmic CSE" front of the research.

2. Sometimes you can't build the model. For some phenomena we simply don't understand the domain well enough to write down a good model — whereas a CNN needs almost no understanding of what it's recognizing. When no faithful model exists, the model-based route is closed.

What the Experiments Showed

Two experiments compared a plain CNN against the knowledge-rich BN + BK circuit. They directly demonstrate the cures for two of the three weaknesses from Part IV.

1

Data hunger. As training data grew, both methods eventually converged to similar accuracy. But at only 250 examples, the knowledge-based model was dramatically more accurate. It already "knows" it's looking at a rectangle, so a little data goes a long way; the CNN, starting from nothing, needs far more data to catch up.

2

Brittleness. A clever test: train on one kind of noise (two pixels appearing horizontally) but test on a different kind (two pixels appearing vertically). The knowledge-based circuit was essentially unaffected. The CNN — which could hit nearly 100% when train and test noise matched — fell apart once the noise was flipped.

The Big Idea

Background knowledge buys you data efficiency and robustness. Because the model-based system carries a concept of the object, it generalizes from few examples and survives distribution shifts that demolish a pattern-fitting network. The flip side — compilation cost and the need for a buildable model — is why this approach hasn't yet scaled to CNN-level ubiquity.

Putting It All Together

This final lecture told one story in two halves. The first half built a CNN from scratch and revealed how little machinery it really contains: a filter is just a small neuron whose weights are shared as it roams an image (convolution); three hyperparameters — filter size, padding, stride — control how the output volume is shaped; many filters turn a volume into a deeper volume of detected features; and max pooling aggregates those features into a coarser, more abstract summary. Stack convolution and pooling, alternating local detection with aggregation, end with a fully connected layer, and let gradient descent learn every weight. That is the entire architecture that revolutionized computer vision.

The second half stepped back to ask what kind of thing we had just built. A CNN is model-free: it fits a function from pixels to labels without ever encoding what the object is. That gift — needing no understanding of the domain — is also its curse, producing the three weaknesses of data hunger, brittleness, and opacity. The antidote, model-based learning, compiles explicit knowledge (a Bayesian network plus background knowledge) into a trainable circuit, and the experiments showed it winning decisively on small data and on shifted distributions — at the price of expensive compilation and the need for a model you can actually build.

So the two halves are really the course's two pillars facing each other. Almost everything we studied — logic, search, constraint satisfaction, Bayesian networks — was represent-and-reason, i.e. model-based. CNNs are the purest curve-fitting, i.e. function-based, system in the syllabus. The closing perspective from the ACM article is that the real lesson of recent AI is about the structure of our world — that many tasks we assumed required deep understanding can be approximated astonishingly well by pattern-fitting alone. But pattern-fitting performance is not the same as intelligence: animals still out-reason our best systems on many tasks, and the honest path forward is to fuse the two approaches so that learning and reasoning reinforce each other.
