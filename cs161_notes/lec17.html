Entropy, Decision Trees & Random Forests

An interactive guide to Lecture 17 — measuring uncertainty with entropy, then using it to grow interpretable decision trees and combine them into powerful random-forest classifiers.

Part I

Entropy — Putting a Number on Uncertainty

Why Are We Learning This?

Today's goal is to build classifiers — machines that look at the characteristics of something and output a decision. A lending company feeds in an applicant's income and payment history and gets back approve or decline. A clinic feeds in test results and gets back cold or COVID-19. The most intuitive way to build such a machine is a decision tree, and combining many trees gives a random forest.

But before we can grow a single tree, we need one mathematical tool: a way to measure how uncertain we are about something. Eyeballing it works for simple cases — but the moment a variable has three, four, or more possible values, your eyes fail you. Entropy is the number that captures uncertainty precisely, and it will sit at the heart of everything that follows.

Key Concept — Entropy

The entropy of a variable X is a single non-negative number that quantifies how uncertain we are about its value, given its probability distribution. Higher entropy means more uncertainty. Entropy is zero exactly when we are completely certain of the outcome.

A motivating observation

The lecture revisits the old Bayesian-network example with three binary variables — earthquake, burglary, and alarm — and their marginal distributions (the probability of each being true or false on its own):

A distribution closer to 50/50 carries more uncertainty — and a higher entropy — than a lopsided one.

For earthquake, the split 0.1 / 0.9 is very lopsided: we are quite sure it is false, so uncertainty is low. For alarm, the split is closer to even, so we are much less sure. If you ordered them from most certain to least certain, you would say earthquake, then burglary, then alarm. Entropy puts numbers on exactly that ranking: 0.469 < 0.722 < 0.80.

The Entropy Formula

Entropy sums over every possible value of the variable. For each value, take its probability, multiply by the base-2 logarithm of that probability, add them up, and negate the total (the negation makes the answer non-negative, since logs of probabilities are negative):

// entropy of a variable X
H(X) = - sum over values x of [ P(x) * log2( P(x) ) ]

For the binary variable burglary with distribution 0.2 / 0.8:

1

Term for the "true" outcome: 0.2 × log2(0.2) = 0.2 × (-2.322) = -0.464.

2

Term for the "false" outcome: 0.8 × log2(0.8) = 0.8 × (-0.322) = -0.258.

3

Add and negate: -(-0.464 - 0.258) = 0.722. That matches the value in the figure above.

The Binary Entropy Curve

To build intuition, consider a binary variable Z whose "true" probability is p (so "false" has probability 1 - p). Plotting entropy as a function of p gives a smooth hump:

Binary entropy peaks at p = 0.5 (maximum confusion) and drops to 0 at both ends, where the outcome is certain.

The two endpoints — point A where p = 0 (we know Z is false) and point B where p = 1 (we know Z is true) — both have entropy zero: perfect certainty. The peak sits at p = 0.5, where a binary variable is maximally confusing and entropy equals 1. For a variable with more than two values, the maximum is larger — a fun exercise is to work out how it grows with the number of values.

What Entropy Really Measures

A student asked whether entropy's value means anything in absolute terms. It does. The field of information theory interprets entropy as the average number of bits you would need to communicate the value of a variable. The more uncertain the variable, the more information each outcome carries, and the more bits you need. That is why the maximally-uncertain binary variable has entropy of exactly one bit.

Part II

Conditional Entropy — The Value of an Observation

Why Are We Learning This?

Entropy tells us how uncertain we are right now. But the whole point of building a classifier is to reduce that uncertainty by gathering evidence. Suppose you are unsure whether a patient has the flu. You can run a blood test — but you do not yet know how it will come out. The natural question is: before running the test, how much will my uncertainty about the flu drop, on average, once I see the result? Conditional entropy answers exactly that, and it is the score a decision tree uses to pick which question to ask next.

Builds On Part I

Plain entropy H(X) uses the marginal distribution of X. Everything here is the same machine, but fed a conditional distribution instead. If you are solid on the entropy formula, conditional entropy is just two small additions on top of it.

Step 1: entropy after a known observation

Imagine you actually observed the variable Y and learned it equals a specific value y. The entropy of X in this new world, written H(X | y), uses the very same formula — you just swap the prior probability P(x) for the conditional probability P(x | y).

Step 2: averaging over outcomes we haven't seen yet

The interesting case is when we have not observed Y yet. We do not know which value it will take, so we cannot pick a single H(X | y). The solution is to consider every possible outcome of Y, compute the resulting entropy for each, and weight each by how likely that outcome is:

// conditional entropy of X given Y (Y not yet observed)
H(X | Y) = sum over values y of [ P(y) * H(X | y) ]

Concretely, the lecture asks: what happens to our uncertainty about burglary once we observe the alarm? Burglary alone has entropy 0.722. Observing the alarm splits into two worlds:

1

Alarm is true. The conditional distribution of burglary shifts, and its entropy actually rises to 0.825 — knowing the alarm fired makes burglary genuinely harder to call.

2

Alarm is false. Now burglary is much clearer; its entropy drops to 0.169.

3

Weight and add. Combine the two using the probabilities of the alarm firing or not. The weighted average comes out to H(burglary | alarm) = 0.329.

The Surprising Guarantee: Observation Never Hurts

Notice something: 0.329 < 0.722. Observing the alarm lowered our average uncertainty about burglary. This is not a coincidence — it is a theorem.

The Big Idea — You Can Never Lose by Observing

Conditional entropy H(X | Y) is always less than or equal to the prior entropy H(X). On average, observing another variable can only make you more certain about X — never less. This is the principle that makes "ask a good question, then ask another" a sound strategy for building classifiers.

Watch the Word "Average"

The guarantee holds for the average H(X | Y), not for any individual outcome. We just saw that learning alarm = true raised burglary's entropy from 0.722 to 0.825. A single observation can leave you more confused; it is only when you average over all of Y's possible values that uncertainty is guaranteed not to increase.

When is the inequality an exact equality — when does observing Y leave our uncertainty about X completely unchanged? When X and Y are independent. If Y tells you nothing about X, observing it cannot move the needle.

Part III

Decision Trees — Classifiers You Can Read

Classifiers and Labeled Data

A classifier takes an instance (a set of feature values) and returns a class (a decision). Decision trees and random forests learn classifiers through supervised learning: we train from labeled data, where each example already comes tagged with the correct class.

Key Concept — Labeled Data

A labeled data set is a table. Each row is an example; the columns are features (also called attributes) plus one special column, the class variable (or target), holding the correct answer. Supervised learning means the algorithm gets to see the answers during training.

The running example is the classic "restaurant" problem: should I wait for a table, or leave? The target WillWait is true or false (a binary classifier, though classes need not be binary), and there are about ten attributes per restaurant — price range, whether it is raining, the type of cuisine, the estimated wait, and so on — across 12 labeled examples.

The Anatomy of a Decision Tree

A decision tree turns those attributes into a flowchart. Each internal node tests one attribute; each branch corresponds to one value of that attribute; each leaf is a final decision. To classify an instance, you start at the root and walk down, following the branch that matches your instance's values, until you hit a leaf.

A fragment of the restaurant decision tree. Internal nodes test attributes; branches are values; leaves are decisions. To classify, walk root-to-leaf.

Reading the tree: if there are no patrons, don't wait. If there are some, wait. If it is full, we are still uncertain, so the tree asks a follow-up question — the wait estimate — and so on. Each test peels away uncertainty until a confident decision remains.

Why Trees Are Prized: Interpretability

You can read a decision tree and see exactly the logic it applies. That property — interpretability — is a major theme in modern AI, largely because the most powerful method, neural networks, lacks it. A neural network may perform brilliantly while remaining an opaque box; a decision tree is transparent by construction.

Interpretability Is Being Redefined

The professor notes that "interpretable because a human can eyeball it" is becoming an old notion. Recent work in explainable AI can take a numeric model — even a neural network — and compile its behavior into logic, then use automated reasoning to explain what it does. So we may not need to visually inspect a model to understand it; we can reason about a symbolic version of it instead.

Part IV

Building the Tree — Which Question to Ask First

Why Are We Learning This?

We can read a tree, but how do we grow one from data? The whole art is choosing, at each node, which attribute to split on. A good split sorts the examples into groups that are as pure (as certain) as possible. This is precisely where Parts I and II pay off: we score each attribute by the conditional entropy it leaves behind, and pick the attribute that drives uncertainty about the class lowest.

This Is Conditional Entropy in Disguise

Picking a split asks: "What happens to the entropy of the class variable WillWait after I test attribute A?" That is exactly H(WillWait | A) from Part II. A great attribute produces low conditional entropy — pure, confident child groups.

The class distribution at each branch

Take the "Patrons?" split. The full data set has 12 examples, evenly split 6 wait / 6 don't, so the prior entropy of WillWait is H = 1. After testing patrons we land in one of three groups, each with its own class distribution and its own entropy:

Each branch yields a class distribution and an entropy. Weight each by how many examples flow down it, then sum.

"None" and "Some" lead to perfectly pure groups (entropy 0 — total certainty). Only "Full" is muddy. Weighting by the fraction of examples down each branch — 2/12, 4/12, 6/12 — gives a conditional entropy near 0.46, a big drop from the prior of 1. Patrons is an excellent first question.

A Fully Worked Score

Let's score a generic attribute A step by step. We start with 100 examples (50 positive, 50 negative). Attribute A is binary:

1

Split the data. Of the 100 examples, 70 have A = yes (30 positive, 40 negative) and 30 have A = no (20 positive, 10 negative).

2

Entropy of the "yes" group. Distribution 30/70, 40/70 gives H = 0.985.

3

Entropy of the "no" group. Distribution 20/30, 10/30 gives H = 0.918.

4

Branch probabilities from the data. 70 of 100 examples went "yes," so P(yes) = 0.7 and P(no) = 0.3.

5

Weighted sum. 0.7 × 0.985 + 0.3 × 0.918 = 0.965. That is the score (conditional entropy) for attribute A.

We compute this score for every candidate attribute and choose the one with the lowest conditional entropy — equivalently, the largest drop from the prior, which is the attribute's information gain. The professor frames the choice as picking the attribute that maximizes a score; minimizing leftover entropy and maximizing gain are two sides of the same coin.

The Learning Algorithm

With scoring in hand, the full recursive tree-builder is short. It takes a set of examples, a set of available attributes, and the parent examples (carried over for one of the base cases):

function DecisionTreeLearn(examples, attributes, parent_examples):
  // ── base cases ──
  if examples is empty:
      return plurality(parent_examples)      // no data: fall back to parent
  if all examples share one class c:
      return c                              // entropy 0: pure leaf
  if attributes is empty:
      return plurality(examples)             // out of questions: majority vote

  // ── main body ──
  A = argmax over attributes of score(a, examples)   // best split
  tree = new node testing A
  for each value v of A:
      subset = examples where A == v
      branch = DecisionTreeLearn(subset, attributes - A, examples)
      add branch to tree under value v
  return tree

The three base cases stop the recursion gracefully:

Base CaseSituationReturn
Pure nodeAll examples share one class (entropy 0)That class
Out of attributesExamples still mixed, but no attributes left to testPlurality (majority) of the examples
Empty subsetA branch caught no examples at allPlurality of the parent examples

The key engine is argmax — "give me the attribute with the best score." Each recursive call filters to the examples matching one branch value and drops the chosen attribute from the candidate set, so as we descend the tree we work with smaller and smaller example sets and fewer attributes. Notably, the tree often uses only a subset of attributes — on the restaurant data it picked roughly four of the ten.

Cross-Validation — Proving the Tree Is Actually Good

Suppose you build a tree and a colleague builds a Naive Bayes classifier on the same data. Whose is better? You cannot judge a model by accuracy on data it was trained on — that rewards memorization. The fix is to hold data back.

Key Concept — Cross-Validation

Randomly split the data, e.g. 80% training / 20% testing. Train only on the 80%; the model never sees the 20%. Then measure accuracy on the untouched 20%. Repeat the random split several times (say five) and average the scores. This k-fold cross-validation estimates how the model performs on data it has never seen.

Part V

Random Forests — Wisdom of the Crowd

Why Are We Learning This?

A single decision tree can latch onto quirks of the training data — overfitting. The cure is delightfully simple: don't build one tree, build many, and let them vote. A collection of trees that vote is a random forest, an instance of the more general idea of ensemble learning.

Each tree classifies the instance independently; the forest returns the majority vote (Yes, No, No → No).

Give the forest an instance, ask every tree, and take the majority vote. With hundreds or thousands of trees, individual mistakes wash out and the ensemble resists overfitting. But for voting to help, the trees must differ from each other — so the forest injects randomness in two places.

Randomness 1: Bootstrap Data Sets

Rather than train every tree on the identical data, each tree gets its own bootstrap sample: draw the same number of examples from the original set, but with replacement — the same example can be picked more than once, and some never get picked at all.

1

Original set: examples {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}. We draw 5, with replacement.

2

One draw might yield {3, 5, 3, 1, 4} — example 3 appears twice and example 2 never appears.

3

The left-out example 2 is an out-of-bag example: unseen by this tree during training, so it is free to use for evaluation later.

Randomness 2: Random Attribute Subsets

The second twist is at each split. A normal tree scores all available attributes and picks the best. A random forest, at every node, first picks a small random subset of m attributes (out of N total) and only scores those, choosing the best among them.

Key Concept — The Three Forest Ingredients

(1) An ensemble of many trees with majority voting; (2) a bootstrap data set per tree (sampling with replacement); (3) a random subset of m attributes considered at each split. Together these decorrelate the trees so their votes genuinely combine.

Here m is a tunable parameter; a common starting point is m = √N. To choose it, try several values and evaluate each forest on its out-of-bag examples — the held-out points from bootstrapping serve as a free, built-in test set.

The Big Idea — Diversity Beats Brilliance

A random forest deliberately weakens each individual tree (limited data, limited attributes) so that the trees disagree in their errors. When you average many diverse, imperfect predictors, their independent mistakes cancel and the ensemble is far more effective than any single tree — the practical payoff of random forests.

Part VI

Bayesian Network Classifiers

Why Are We Learning This?

Decision trees and forests are not the only way to build a classifier. We can also turn a Bayesian network into one. This closes the loop with earlier lectures and shows that "classifier" is a goal, not a single technique.

The most common structure is Naive Bayes: a single class variable C at the top, with all the attributes hanging beneath it as children. The "naive" assumption is that the attributes are conditionally independent given the class.

Naive Bayes: the class C is the parent of every attribute. Fixing the attributes to an instance, we compute P(C | instance).

To classify an instance, you fix the attribute values and compute the probability of the class. For a binary class you might simply pick whichever class is more probable — but more generally you use a threshold.

Key Concept — Classification Threshold

Choose a threshold T (say 90%). Given an instance, compute P(C | instance). If it is ≥ T, output the positive class; otherwise output the negative class. The threshold lets you trade off false positives against false negatives — crucial in medical or financial decisions.

The lecture's example is the pregnancy network: an instance fixes the scan S, blood test B, and urine test U. With T = 90%, you declare "pregnant" only when P(pregnant | S, B, U) ≥ 0.90. A Naive Bayes classifier is attractive partly because inference on it is cheap — its tree-width is small, so the standard inference-complexity guarantee from the Bayesian-network lectures makes the probability easy to compute.

Connecting Back to Probability

This is the same probabilistic machinery from the Bayesian-network lectures, now repurposed as a decision rule. A classifier, in this view, is "a Bayesian network plus a threshold." It is a reminder that the entropy you learned today and the probability calculus you learned earlier are two faces of the same theory.

Putting It All Together

This lecture built a complete pipeline from a single primitive. We started with entropy — one number that measures how uncertain we are about a variable. We extended it to conditional entropy, which predicts how much an observation will reduce that uncertainty on average, and proved the comforting fact that observing never hurts.

Those two ideas are not abstractions: they are literally the scoring function that grows a decision tree. At each node we ask "which attribute leaves the class variable least uncertain?" — that is conditional entropy — and split on the winner, recursing until each region is pure. The result is an interpretable classifier you can read like a flowchart.

To make trees more powerful we surrendered some interpretability for accuracy with random forests: many trees, each trained on a bootstrap sample and restricted to random attribute subsets, voting by majority. The deliberate diversity cancels individual errors. Finally, Bayesian network classifiers showed that the same goal can be reached through pure probability plus a threshold, tying today's material back to the probability calculus of earlier lectures.

And the emerging answer, explainable AI, is to compile a numeric model into logic and reason about it symbolically, so we no longer have to choose between power and understanding.
