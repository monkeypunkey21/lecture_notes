Inference in First-Order Logic

From propositionalization and semi-decidability to chaining, unification, and lifted resolution — how a machine reasons over quantified knowledge.

Part I

Reducing First-Order Logic to Propositional Logic

Why Reduce At All?

Before this lecture, we already had a powerful, well-understood machine for reasoning: propositional inference. In propositional logic, every fact is an atomic true-or-false symbol like Rains or P, and we have fast, mature algorithms (SAT solvers) that decide whether one set of propositions entails another. "Entails" means: in every world where the knowledge base is true, the query is also true.

First-order logic (FOL) is more expressive. Instead of bare symbols it gives us objects (John, Richard), predicates that say things about objects (King(John)), functions that build new objects from old ones (Father(John)), and above all quantifiers — "for all x…" (∀) and "there exists an x…" (∃). That extra expressiveness is exactly why early AI was built on FOL. But it also breaks our propositional toolbox: a SAT solver has no idea what a quantifier is.

The strategy of this first part is therefore a translation: take a first-order knowledge base, mechanically strip out the quantifiers, and produce an ordinary propositional knowledge base that a SAT solver can chew on. This trick — called propositionalization — turns out to be not just a teaching device but the way modern systems actually do FOL inference in practice.

Why This Matters Later

Everything in Part I rests on two replacement rules: Universal Instantiation (kills ∀) and Existential Instantiation (kills ∃). The very same two rules reappear in Part VI when we convert FOL sentences to CNF for resolution — there ∃ is handled by a fancier version called Skolemization. Learn them once here; reuse them everywhere.

Universal Instantiation — Killing "For All"

A universally quantified sentence ∀x α says α holds for every object. Universal Instantiation (UI) says: if it holds for everything, then it certainly holds for any specific object I care to name. So I am allowed to drop the ∀ and substitute a concrete ground term (a term with no variables) for the variable.

Take the classic example:

∀x  King(x) ∧ Greedy(x) ⇒ Evil(x)

Suppose our vocabulary has two constants, John and Richard, and one function, Father. UI lets us generate concrete instances by plugging in any ground term:

1

Substitute x ↦ John:  King(John) ∧ Greedy(John) ⇒ Evil(John).

2

Substitute x ↦ Richard:  King(Richard) ∧ Greedy(Richard) ⇒ Evil(Richard).

3

Apply the function to make a new ground term Father(John), then substitute x ↦ Father(John):  King(Father(John)) ∧ Greedy(Father(John)) ⇒ Evil(Father(John)).

4

And nothing stops us nesting: Father(Father(John)), Father(Father(Father(John))), … In principle there are infinitely many instances. Hold that thought — it is the seed of Part II.

The general rule, written compactly, is:

   ∀x α
─────────────      where g is any ground term
 Subst({x/g}, α)

What Exactly Is a "Ground Term"?

Earlier we defined a term as one of three things: a variable (x), a constant (John), or a function applied to terms (Father(x)). A ground term is a term with no variables anywhere inside it.

Key Concept — Ground Term

A ground term contains no variables. A constant like Richard is ground. A function applied to ground arguments, like Father(Richard), is also ground (a function symbol on a ground term). But Father(x) is not ground, because x is a variable. Ground terms are exactly the things UI is allowed to substitute in.

Why This Is Really Propositional

Look at an instantiated sentence like King(John) ∧ Greedy(John) ⇒ Evil(John). It looks first-order, but there are no variables left. Each ground literal — King(John), Greedy(John), Evil(John) — is just a complicated name for something that is simply true or false. Rename them X₁, X₂, X₃ and the sentence becomes the purely propositional X₁ ∧ X₂ ⇒ X₃. Once the fancy syntax is gone, an ordinary propositional solver does the work.

Existential Instantiation — Killing "There Exists"

An existentially quantified sentence ∃x α says α holds for at least one object — but it does not tell us which one. Existential Instantiation (EI) handles this by giving that unknown object a brand-new name and then dropping the quantifier. Consider:

∃x  Crown(x) ∧ OnHead(x, John)

We invent a fresh constant — call it C₁ — and replace x with it:

Crown(C₁) ∧ OnHead(C₁, John)

Intuitively the two say the same kind of thing: the first says "some crown sits on John's head"; the second says "this particular object, which I'll call C₁, is a crown and sits on John's head." The crucial subtlety is that C₁ must be completely new — it cannot appear anywhere else in the knowledge base.

The Fresh-Constant Rule — Don't Reuse Names

Suppose the knowledge base already contained Red(C₁). If we reused C₁ for the unknown crown, we'd accidentally be asserting that the crown is red — but the original ∃ never said that! The fresh name guarantees that nothing else is secretly being claimed about the object. This new name is called a Skolem constant (after the logician Skolem).

The general rule:

    ∃x α
─────────────      where k is a constant symbol that
 Subst({x/k}, α)    appears NOWHERE else in the KB
The Big Idea — UI Preserves Truth, EI Preserves Satisfiability

These two rules are not equally strong. With no function symbols, Universal Instantiation produces an equivalent propositional KB — same models, exactly. Existential Instantiation only preserves satisfiability: the new KB is satisfiable if and only if the old one is, but they are not logically equivalent. Since entailment can be reduced to a satisfiability check, preserving satisfiability is enough to answer the queries we care about.

Part II

Semi-Decidability — When the Search Never Stops

Part I left us with a worry. As soon as we have a function symbol like Father, Universal Instantiation can generate infinitely many ground terms: John, Father(John), Father(Father(John)), … The propositionalized knowledge base could be infinite. Can we still answer "does α follow from the KB?" in finite time? The answer is a fascinating "yes-and-no," and it is one of the deepest results in logic.

Herbrand's Theorem — A Finite Subset Always Suffices

The escape hatch is a 1930 theorem by Jacques Herbrand. It says: even if the full propositionalized knowledge base is infinite, you never need all of it.

Key Concept — Herbrand's Theorem (1930)

If a sentence α is entailed by a first-order knowledge base, then α is entailed by a finite subset of the propositionalized knowledge base. In symbols: if Δ ⊨ α, there exists a finite Δ′ ⊆ propositionalize(Δ) with Δ′ ⊨ α.

Why does this matter? Because functions let us build arbitrarily deep nested terms — not just Father(John) but Age(Father(John)) and on and on — so the full instantiation is unbounded. Herbrand promises that a true entailment is always "caught" by some finite slice of that infinite set. We just have to find the right slice.

The Increasing-Depth Algorithm

Herbrand's theorem suggests an almost embarrassingly simple algorithm: keep instantiating to deeper and deeper nesting levels, and after each level check whether the (now finite, propositional) knowledge base already entails α.

// Prove that α follows from first-order KB Δ
for n = 0 to ∞:
    Δₙ = propositionalize(Δ, depth = n)   // UI up to nesting depth n
    if Δₙ ⊨ α:                              // a finite propositional check (SAT)
        return true
1

Depth 0 (Δ₀): instantiate variables using only the constants. This already gives a finite propositional KB. Check whether Δ₀ ⊨ α using a SAT-based entailment test. If yes — done.

2

Depth 1 (Δ₁): if not, allow one level of function nesting, e.g. add terms like Father(John). Re-check Δ₁ ⊨ α.

3

Depth 2 (Δ₂): still no? Allow two levels of nesting, e.g. Father(Father(John)). Re-check. And so on, incrementing n.

4

Guarantee: Herbrand says if α genuinely follows, then for some finite n the check succeeds. So this loop is guaranteed to halt and say "yes" whenever the answer is yes.

The Catch — Entailment in FOL Is Semi-Decidable

Now look at the failure case. Suppose α does not follow from Δ. The loop checks depth 0, fails, checks depth 1, fails, checks depth 2, fails… and never finds a contradiction-free reason to stop. It runs forever. We can never be sure whether we just haven't gone deep enough yet, or whether α truly doesn't follow.

"Semi-Decidable" Defined

A problem is semi-decidable if there is a procedure that is guaranteed to terminate with "yes" whenever the answer is yes — but may run forever when the answer is no. FOL entailment is semi-decidable: we can confirm that something does follow, but we have no general procedure that always terminates to confirm that something does not follow.

This is not a weakness of our particular algorithm — it is a fundamental limit. It was proved independently in 1936 by Alan Turing and Alonzo Church (the Church–Turing result on the undecidability of first-order validity). Contrast this with propositional logic, which is fully decidable: a finite truth table always settles the question.

How It's Actually Done in Practice

Because the infinite case is so painful, real systems usually restrict themselves to FOL without function symbols. Then the set of ground terms is finite, propositionalization yields a finite KB, and a powerful SAT solver or knowledge compiler finishes the job. Historically AI invested heavily in first-order computation for its first decades, but attention later shifted toward propositional solvers precisely because they're so effective — so today a common pattern is: translate the (function-free) FOL problem down to propositional logic and let a SAT solver do the heavy lifting.

Part III

Forward & Backward Chaining

Propositionalization is general but blunt. The next two methods work directly on first-order sentences, without translating everything down. They're not fully general — they apply only to a restricted, well-behaved class of knowledge bases — but on that class they're efficient and intuitive. The restriction is definite clauses.

Definite Clauses — Rules in Disguise

Recall from propositional logic the idea of a Horn clause: a disjunction of literals with at most one positive literal (so zero or one). A definite clause is the stricter cousin: a clause with exactly one positive literal.

Clause typePositive literalsExample
Horn clause0 or 1¬A ∨ ¬B ∨ C  or  ¬A ∨ ¬B
Definite clauseexactly 1¬A ∨ ¬B ∨ C

The reason definite clauses are so useful is that they are secretly just if–then rules. A clause ¬A ∨ ¬B ∨ C is logically the same as the implication A ∧ B ⇒ C. So every definite clause reads as: "a conjunction of positive premises implies a single positive conclusion."

Key Concept — Definite Clause as a Rule

A definite clause rewrites as P₁ ∧ P₂ ∧ … ∧ Pₙ ⇒ Q where every Pᵢ and Q is a positive literal (or atomic fact). A single positive fact like American(West) is also a definite clause — it's the degenerate rule with an empty premise. Chaining methods only work on knowledge bases made entirely of definite clauses.

The Story — Is Colonel West a Criminal?

We'll use one running story to illustrate both directions of chaining. In English: "The law says it is a crime for an American to sell weapons to hostile nations. The country Nono, an enemy of America, has some missiles, and all of its missiles were sold to it by Colonel West, who is American. Prove West is a criminal." Here are the seven first-order sentences:

// (1) It is a crime for an American to sell weapons to a hostile nation
American(x) ∧ Weapon(y) ∧ Sells(x,y,z) ∧ Hostile(z) ⇒ Criminal(x)

// (2) Nono has some missiles — ∃ removed by EI with fresh constant M1
Owns(Nono, M1)      Missile(M1)

// (3) All of Nono's missiles were sold to it by West
Missile(x) ∧ Owns(Nono,x) ⇒ Sells(West, x, Nono)

Missile(x) ⇒ Weapon(x)              // (4) missiles are weapons
Enemy(x, America) ⇒ Hostile(x)      // (5) enemies of America are hostile
American(West)                      // (6)
Enemy(Nono, America)                // (7)

Two things to notice. First, sentence (2) was originally existential ("Nono has some missiles"), so we used Existential Instantiation to introduce the fresh constant M1. Second, all the other variables are universally quantified, and by convention we just drop the explicit ∀. Every sentence here is a definite clause — perfect for chaining.

Forward Chaining — Start From Facts, Push Forward

Forward chaining is data-driven. You begin with what you know for sure (the ground facts), then repeatedly fire any rule whose premises you've already established, adding its conclusion to your pile. You keep going until the goal pops out.

1

Known facts: Owns(Nono,M1), Missile(M1), American(West), Enemy(Nono,America).

2

Fire rule (5) with x ↦ Nono: premise Enemy(Nono,America) matches, so conclude Hostile(Nono).

3

Fire rule (4) with x ↦ M1: premise Missile(M1) matches, so conclude Weapon(M1).

4

Fire rule (3) with x ↦ M1: premises Missile(M1) and Owns(Nono,M1) both match, so conclude Sells(West, M1, Nono).

5

Fire rule (1) with x ↦ West, y ↦ M1, z ↦ Nono: all four premises — American(West), Weapon(M1), Sells(West,M1,Nono), Hostile(Nono) — are now established, so conclude Criminal(West). Goal reached.

Forward chaining is "opportunistic": you fire whatever you can and keep growing the set of known facts until the target appears.

Backward Chaining — Start From the Goal, Work Backward

Backward chaining is goal-driven. You start with what you want to prove and ask: "which rule could conclude this?" Then you take that rule's premises as new sub-goals to prove, recursively, until every sub-goal bottoms out at a known fact.

1

Goal: Criminal(West). Only rule (1) concludes Criminal. Matching its head forces x ↦ West, leaving four sub-goals: American(West), Weapon(y), Sells(West,y,z), Hostile(z).

2

Sub-goal American(West): already a fact (6). Done — no further work.

3

Sub-goal Weapon(y): rule (4) says Missile(y) ⇒ Weapon(y), so the new sub-goal becomes Missile(y). Rule (2)'s fact Missile(M1) matches by binding y ↦ M1 — and that binding propagates everywhere y appears.

4

Sub-goal Sells(West, M1, z): rule (3) concludes it, forcing z ↦ Nono and leaving sub-goals Missile(M1) (a fact) and Owns(Nono,M1) (a fact). Both already known.

5

Sub-goal Hostile(Nono): rule (5) concludes it, leaving Enemy(Nono,America) — fact (7). Every sub-goal is discharged, so Criminal(West) is proved.

Backward Chaining Can Loop Forever

Backward chaining is typically implemented with depth-first search over the rule tree. Applied naïvely, it can fall into infinite loops (e.g. a rule whose sub-goal keeps regenerating the same goal), so real implementations add loop-checking and other safeguards. This method, refined, is the engine behind Prolog and logic programming — answering an existential query returns a binding list, so logical inference doubles as computation.

Side note: Datalog — the function-free, definite-clause subset

When you combine definite clauses with no function symbols, the only ground terms are constants, so everything stays finite and several desirable properties hold (e.g. inference terminates). This subset is called Datalog, and it comes from the database world — it's a common, practical fragment of first-order logic. Note also that even rule matching — deciding whether a rule's premises match what's already known — is not trivial in general; the textbook covers the indexing tricks used to do it efficiently.

Correct answer: B. Part IV

Unification — The Matching Engine

Notice that every chaining step above quietly relied on the same operation: deciding that Missile(y) could be made identical to the fact Missile(M1) by setting y ↦ M1. That "make-two-things-identical-by-substitution" step is called unification, and it is the heart of all first-order inference. Before we can lift resolution to FOL in Part V, we need to understand it on its own.

The Matching Problem

Two atomic sentences usually aren't literally the same, but they might be made the same by choosing the right values for their variables. A unifier is a substitution θ that, applied to both, yields identical results.

Key Concept — Unification

A substitution θ unifies α and β if Subst(θ, α) = Subst(θ, β) — applying θ to both makes them syntactically identical. For example, Knows(John, x) and Knows(John, Jane) are unified by θ = {x / Jane}: both become Knows(John, Jane).

Worked Examples — and One Failure

The professor solves these by visual inspection rather than running the full algorithm; that's enough for our purposes. Look for what must be true for the two atoms to coincide.

1

Knows(John, x) and Knows(y, OJ). To line up the first arguments, set y ↦ John; to line up the second, set x ↦ OJ. Unifier θ = {y/John, x/OJ}. Both become Knows(John, OJ).

2

Knows(John, x) and Knows(y, Mother(y)). First force y ↦ John, which turns the second into Knows(John, Mother(John)). Now match the second arguments by setting x ↦ Mother(John). A variable can be bound to a whole function term — that's allowed.

3

Knows(John, x) and Knows(x, OJ). This fails. Matching the first arguments demands x ↦ John; matching the second demands x ↦ OJ. The same variable x cannot be both John and OJ — a clash. No unifier exists.

Unifying Knows(John,x) with Knows(y,Mother(y)): bindings flow until both atoms collapse to one.
Why Unification Is the Linchpin

Unification is the precise, mechanical version of the "matching" we did informally in chaining. In Part V it becomes the new ingredient that makes resolution work in first-order logic: when we try to cancel a literal against its negation, the two won't be literally identical, so we first unify them, then apply the resulting substitution to whatever's left over.

Check Your Understanding
Why can Knows(John, x) and Knows(x, OJ) not be unified?
A
Because John and OJ are different constants and constants can never be unified.
B
Because the single variable x would have to equal both John (from the first argument) and OJ (from the second), which is contradictory.
C
Because the two predicates have different names.
D

Correct answer: B. The same variable appears in both atoms but in positions that demand conflicting bindings: aligning the first arguments forces x ↦ John while aligning the second forces x ↦ OJ. A substitution can only assign one value to a variable, so no unifier exists. Option A is false — a variable can perfectly well unify two different constants when they sit in different variable slots, as example 1 showed.
Part V

First-Order Resolution

Chaining was powerful but limited to definite clauses. Resolution is the fully general method — it handles any first-order knowledge base. At a high level the recipe is identical to the propositional case we saw earlier: to test whether α follows from Δ, convert Δ and the negated query ¬α to conjunctive normal form (CNF), then repeatedly resolve clauses, trying to derive the empty clause (a contradiction). If we reach a contradiction, then Δ ∧ ¬α is unsatisfiable, which means Δ ⊨ α.

Only two things are new at the first-order level: the resolution rule now needs unification (Part IV) to cancel literals, and CNF conversion gains a couple of extra steps (Part VI). Let's see the lifted rule, then a full worked proof.

The Lifted Resolution Rule

In propositional resolution, two clauses cancel when one contains a literal and the other contains its exact negation: from ¬A ∨ B and A ∨ D we cancel A/¬A and conclude B ∨ D. In first-order logic the complementary literals usually aren't identical, so we unify them first — and then we must apply that unifier to the surviving literals too.

// Given these two clauses:
∀x  King(x) ⇒ Unhappy(x)      ≡   ¬King(x) ∨ Unhappy(x)
    King(Ken)

// ¬King(x) and King(Ken) aren't identical — unify them:
θ = {x / Ken}

// Apply θ to the surviving literal Unhappy(x):
⇒  Unhappy(Ken)
The Big Idea — Resolution = Unify, Cancel, Substitute

First-order resolution is propositional resolution plus one step: find a unifier θ for the complementary literals, cancel them, then apply θ to everything that remains. Forgetting to apply θ to the leftover literals is the classic mistake — in the example above, the conclusion isn't Unhappy(x), it's Unhappy(Ken), because the unifier that bound x ↦ Ken must propagate.

A Full Proof — Did Curiosity Kill the Cat?

The story: Jack owns a dog. Every dog owner is an animal lover. No animal lover kills an animal. Either Jack or Curiosity killed the cat named Tuna. Cats are animals. Query: did Curiosity kill the cat? Here is the knowledge base in CNF (the conversion itself is Part VI), plus the negated query as clause 0:

// Δ in CNF, clauses 1–7
(1)  Dog(D)              // Jack owns a dog named D
     Owns(Jack, D)
(2)  Owns(Jack, D)       // (grouped with 1 above)
(3)  ¬Dog(y) ∨ ¬Owns(x,y) ∨ AnimalLover(x)     // dog owners are animal lovers
(4)  ¬AnimalLover(x) ∨ ¬Animal(y) ∨ ¬Kills(x,y) // lovers kill no animals
(5)  Kills(Jack,Tuna) ∨ Kills(Curiosity,Tuna)   // Jack or Curiosity
(6)  Cat(Tuna)
(7)  ¬Cat(x) ∨ Animal(x)                         // cats are animals

// Negated query, clause 0
(0)  ¬Kills(Curiosity, Tuna)

We resolve pairs of clauses, unifying as needed, hunting for the empty clause:

1

Resolve (1) & (3): unify Dog(D) with ¬Dog(y) via y ↦ D. New clause (8): ¬Owns(x,D) ∨ AnimalLover(x).

2

Resolve (2) & (8): unify Owns(Jack,D) with ¬Owns(x,D) via x ↦ Jack. New clause (9): AnimalLover(Jack).

3

Resolve (6) & (7): unify Cat(Tuna) with ¬Cat(x) via x ↦ Tuna. New clause (10): Animal(Tuna).

4

Resolve (4) & (10): unify Animal(Tuna) with ¬Animal(y) via y ↦ Tuna. New clause (11): ¬AnimalLover(x) ∨ ¬Kills(x, Tuna).

5

Resolve (9) & (11): unify AnimalLover(Jack) with ¬AnimalLover(x) via x ↦ Jack. New clause (12): ¬Kills(Jack, Tuna).

6

Resolve (0) & (5): cancel ¬Kills(Curiosity,Tuna) against Kills(Curiosity,Tuna) — no unification needed, they're identical. New clause (13): Kills(Jack, Tuna).

7

Resolve (12) & (13): ¬Kills(Jack,Tuna) and Kills(Jack,Tuna) cancel, leaving nothing — the empty clause (14), our contradiction.

Reading the Result

Deriving the empty clause means Δ ∧ ¬α is unsatisfiable, so Δ ⊨ α. The query was "did Curiosity kill Tuna?" and we proved it: working backward, the proof shows Jack is an animal lover, animal lovers don't kill animals, Tuna is an animal — so Jack is innocent, and since one of the two did it, Curiosity is guilty. Notice unification did the heavy lifting at every step except the last two.

Resolution proof tree: clauses combine pairwise (with unification) until the contradictory empty clause ⊥ falls out.

Part VI

Converting First-Order Sentences to CNF

Resolution assumed its inputs were already in CNF — a conjunction of clauses, each clause a disjunction of literals, with no quantifiers in sight. Part VI fills that gap. The procedure is the same skeleton as propositional CNF conversion, with a few extra middle steps to deal with the things only FOL has: quantifiers and variables. We'll run one sentence through all six steps:

∀x [ ∀y Animal(y) ⇒ Loves(x,y) ] ⇒ [ ∃y Loves(y,x) ]
// "everyone who loves all animals is loved by someone"

The Six Steps

1

Eliminate connectives other than ∧, ∨, ¬. Rewrite each P ⇒ Q as ¬P ∨ Q. Both implications disappear, each leaving a fresh negation in front of its antecedent.

2

Move negations inward. Use De Morgan's laws (¬(A∧B) ≡ ¬A∨¬B, ¬(A∨B) ≡ ¬A∧¬B) plus two quantifier rules unique to FOL: ¬∀x α ≡ ∃x ¬α and ¬∃x α ≡ ∀x ¬α. A negation crossing a quantifier flips it. Double negations cancel.

2½

After step 2 our sentence reads (schematically) ∀x [ ∃y (Animal(y) ∧ ¬Loves(x,y)) ] ∨ [ ∃y Loves(y,x) ] — note we now have two different ∃y in the same sentence.

3

Standardize variables apart. Two quantifiers must not share a variable name, or later steps get confused. Rename the second ∃y to ∃z. Now every quantifier owns a distinct variable.

4

Skolemize — remove every existential quantifier (detailed below).

5

Drop universal quantifiers. After Skolemization only ∀'s remain; since everything left is implicitly universally quantified, we simply erase the ∀ symbols.

6

Distribute ∨ over ∧. Using A ∨ (B ∧ C) ≡ (A∨B) ∧ (A∨C), push the sentence into a conjunction of disjunctions — pure CNF. Split into separately numbered clauses, ready for resolution.

Step 4 in Depth — Skolemization

Back in Part I, Existential Instantiation replaced ∃x with a fresh constant. That works when the ∃ stands alone. But here the existential is inside the scope of a ∀: ∀x … ∃y …. The "someone" who loves x may be a different person for each x — so y depends on x. A single constant can't capture that dependence.

Key Concept — Skolemization

To remove an existential quantifier inside the scope of universals over x₁,…,xₙ, replace its variable with a Skolem function f(x₁,…,xₙ) — a brand-new function symbol applied to the surrounding universal variables. This records that the existing object depends on those variables. If the ∃ is not inside any ∀ (as in plain EI), the function has no arguments and degenerates to a Skolem constant — exactly the fresh constant from Part I.

So in our example, ∃y (inside ∀x) becomes a function F(x), and the renamed ∃z (also inside ∀x) becomes G(x). Every occurrence of the existential variable is replaced by its Skolem term.

Because y sits inside ∀x, it must become a function F(x) — not a constant — to preserve the dependence on x.

After Skolemizing, dropping the ∀'s, and distributing, the example yields its two final clauses:

(C1)  Animal(F(x)) ∨ Loves(G(x), x)
(C2)  ¬Loves(x, F(x)) ∨ Loves(G(x), x)
Skolem Symbols Must Be New

Just like the fresh constant in Existential Instantiation, each Skolem function or constant must use a symbol that appears nowhere else in the knowledge base. Reusing a name would smuggle in false equalities between objects. And remember: Skolemization preserves satisfiability, not logical equivalence — which is exactly what resolution needs, since resolution works by refuting Δ ∧ ¬α.

Correct answer: B. Under ∀x ∃y, the witnessing object y may depend on which x we picked, so a single constant would wrongly assert one fixed object loves everybody. A Skolem function F(x) captures "the object that depends on x." Only when the existential is not inside any universal does it collapse to a Skolem constant — which is precisely the Existential Instantiation rule from Part I.

Putting It All Together

This lecture answered one question — how does a machine actually reason over quantified knowledge? — three different ways, each trading generality against efficiency.

The brute-force route (Parts I–II). Strip quantifiers with Universal and Existential Instantiation, turning ground first-order sentences into ordinary propositions, then unleash a SAT solver. Herbrand's theorem guarantees a finite slice of the (possibly infinite) propositionalization always suffices for a true entailment — but the symmetry breaks for non-entailment, which is why FOL inference is only semi-decidable, a limit Turing and Church nailed down in 1936.

The efficient special case (Part III). Restrict to definite clauses — if-then rules with positive literals — and you get forward and backward chaining: fire rules from the facts toward the goal, or unwind the goal back to the facts. This is the machinery behind Datalog and Prolog.

The general engine (Parts IV–VI). Resolution handles any knowledge base. It needed two new pieces we built in order: unification, the substitution that makes two literals match (Part IV), which powers the lifted resolution rule (Part V); and a richer CNF conversion whose star step is Skolemization (Part VI) — itself just the grown-up version of the Existential Instantiation we met all the way back in Part I. The thread connecting the whole lecture is that handful of substitution ideas: instantiate, unify, Skolemize.

The Through-Line

Every method here reduces first-order reasoning to something more concrete: chaining reduces it to rule-firing on ground facts, resolution reduces it to refutation in CNF, and propositionalization reduces it all the way down to SAT. First-order logic gives us the expressiveness; these algorithms give us the computation.
