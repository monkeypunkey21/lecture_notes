First-Order Logic — Reasoning About Objects, Relations & Quantifiers

An interactive guide to Lecture 11 — moving beyond true/false variables to a logic rich enough to talk about things, the relations between them, and statements about all or some of them.

Part I

Why First-Order Logic?

Last time the course studied propositional logic — a system where the only building blocks are simple true-or-false variables glued together with connectives like "and," "or," and "not." Propositional logic is clean and decidable, but it has a fatal weakness for AI: it cannot talk about things. It can only say "this fact is true" or "that fact is false." If you want to say something about every cell in a grid, or some person at UCLA, propositional logic forces you to spell out one variable per case, by hand, forever.

Today's lecture introduces the fix: first-order logic (often abbreviated FOL, and historically called predicate calculus). It is one of the most influential logics ever devised — central not only to AI and computer science but to philosophy and mathematics long before computers existed. When John McCarthy first proposed using logic to model reasoning in AI (a knowledge base of statements fed to a "thinking engine" that performs deduction), the logic he reached for was first-order logic, precisely because it is so much more expressive than propositional logic.

Key Concept — First-Order Logic (FOL)

A logic whose vocabulary includes objects (the things in the world), relations / predicates among those objects, and functions mapping objects to objects — plus quantifiers ("for all," "there exists") that let a single sentence speak about whole collections of objects at once. Also known as predicate calculus; the two names are interchangeable.

The Limits of Propositional Logic

To feel the pain that FOL relieves, recall the Wumpus World example used to motivate propositional logic. The Wumpus World is a grid of cells. A "wumpus" (a monster) lurks somewhere; pits are scattered around; and the rules of the world connect them — for instance, any cell adjacent to a pit feels breezy. The agent's job is to reason its way safely through the grid.

In propositional logic, you have to invent a separate variable for every fact about every cell. Breezy_2_3 means "cell (2,3) is breezy." Pit_1_1 means "there is a pit in cell (1,1)." A modest grid already needs a swarm of these variables. Worse, the rule "if a cell has a pit, every adjacent cell is breezy" cannot be stated once. You must re-state it, fully written out, for cell (1,1), then again for (1,2), then again for (2,1) — once per cell. The knowledge base balloons, and nothing in it captures the general idea.

The Core Problem

Propositional logic has no notion of "for every cell." A rule that should be one sentence becomes hundreds of nearly identical sentences. There is no way to factor out the pattern — the logic simply cannot see that all those cells are instances of the same idea.

The Wumpus World, Compactly

First-order logic lets you write the breeze rule once. The lecture sketched it as a single sentence (don't worry about parsing every symbol yet — this is a preview of what FOL buys you):

∀ s   Pit(s)  →  ∀ r  ( Adjacent(r, s)  →  Breezy(r) )

Read aloud: "for all squares s, if there is a pit in s, then for all squares r adjacent to s, that square r is breezy." Three brand-new ideas appear here, each of which the rest of this lecture unpacks:

1

Quantification — the ∀ ("for all") symbol. Instead of enumerating every square, one sentence ranges over all of them automatically.

2

Relations (predicates) — Adjacent(r, s) is a relationship between two objects, and Breezy(r) is a property of one object. These replace the one-variable-per-fact explosion.

3

Functions — you could even define a function Left(s) that, given a cell, returns the cell to its left. Functions let you refer to objects indirectly instead of naming each one.

That single sentence replaces the whole pile of propositional rules. That is what the lecture meant by FOL being "more succinct": you can express knowledge compactly, capturing the general pattern instead of grinding out every special case.

The Big Idea

First-order logic adds structure to the world. Propositional logic sees only a flat list of true/false flags; FOL sees objects, the relationships among them, and quantifiers that let one sentence speak for an entire collection. Expressiveness is the whole point — and, as we'll learn, also the source of its main difficulty.

Where This Is Heading

That expressiveness comes at a cost: the main challenge with full first-order logic is computational. Reasoning can be far harder than in propositional logic. That is why people invented restricted forms of FOL that sit between the two — restricted enough to be converted back into propositional logic and solved with propositional machinery. The next lecture takes up that story; today is about learning to read and write FOL.

Part II

Worlds, Objects & Relations

Before writing a single FOL sentence, we need to know what a sentence is about. In any logic, a "world" (or model) is a possible state of affairs — the thing a sentence is true or false in. The jump from propositional to first-order logic is, at its heart, a jump in what a world can contain.

What a World Looks Like

In propositional logic, a world is dead simple. You have variables A, B, C, …, and a world is just an assignment of true/false to each one. A world is a row of a truth table:

A propositional world is nothing but a list of variables, each flagged true or false.

In first-order logic, a world is far richer. It contains a collection of objects, and those objects can have properties, be tied together by relations, and be transformed into other objects by functions. Instead of a flat list of flags, an FOL world is a little universe of things and the structure among them.

Objects (Jack, Tom, George), a relation (Brother holds between Jack and Tom), and a function (FatherOf maps Jack to George).

The Four Ingredients

Every FOL world is built from four kinds of thing. Learn these four words; the entire syntax of the logic is organized around them.

Key Concept — The Four Ingredients of an FOL World

Objects are the things themselves: people, houses, the wumpus, numbers, colors. Properties are things true of a single object: breezy, large, red. Relations hold among several objects: inside, adjacent, larger-than. Functions map objects to objects: father-of, best-friend-of, age-of.

The cleanest way to keep relations and functions apart is to ask what each one returns. A function takes objects and gives you back another object. A relation takes objects and gives you back a truth value — it either holds or it doesn't. The lecture summarized it crisply: a relation (predicate) maps objects to a Boolean (true/false), while a function maps objects to objects.

FunctionRelation (Predicate)
Maps objects → an objectMaps objects → true / false
Age(Sally) is a number (an object)Greater(x, y) is true or false
"the father of Jack" — points at a person"Jack is the brother of Tom" — holds or not
Can be nested as part of a bigger termForms a complete sentence on its own
A Property Is Just a One-Argument Relation

Why are "properties" and "relations" sometimes lumped together? Because a property is simply a relation that takes a single argument. Smelly(x) — "x is smelly" — is a relation about one object. So in the formal syntax we'll meet shortly, both fall under the single umbrella of predicates. The distinction is conceptual, not structural, and we can often ignore it.

Dissecting Sentences

The best way to internalize the four ingredients is to take an English sentence and label every piece. The lecture did this live with the class; let's reproduce two examples.

Example 1: "one plus one equals two"

1

Objects: 1 and 2 — the numbers themselves are the things we're talking about.

2

Relation: equals — it holds (or not) between two objects.

3

Function: plus — it takes two number-objects and returns a number-object. "One plus one" is a function applied to objects, yielding the object two.

4

Properties: none here — everything was accounted for by objects, the relation, and the function.

Example 2: "square 1 is adjacent to square 2 and it is smelly"

Here the objects are the two squares; the relation is adjacent (it holds between two squares); there are no functions; and there is a property, smelly, true of a single square.

The Categories Aren't Forced — You Choose

Are these four categories mutually exclusive? Yes — a single thing belongs to exactly one. But how you carve up the world is your choice. The same English statement can often be modeled by treating something as a property in one design and a relation in another. There is no single "right" answer, only better and worse modeling decisions. This freedom — and the skill of using it well — is one of the genuinely interesting (and tricky) parts of representing natural language in FOL.

Part III

The Vocabulary & Syntax

Now we leave the intuitive picture and pin down the actual syntax — the rules for what counts as a legal FOL sentence. The lecture flagged early that, unlike propositional logic, FOL is rich enough that we'll spend most of our time on syntax and on the art of representing knowledge, only hinting at the more involved formal semantics.

Step Zero: Picking a Vocabulary

In propositional logic, the only problem-specific thing you invent is the set of variables; everything else (the connectives) is fixed. In FOL there is more to choose, and the choices split cleanly into two groups.

Domain-specific (you invent these)Always present (built in)
Constants — names of objects: 2, Jack, UCLAVariables — lowercase: x, y, …
Predicates — relations/properties: Adjacent, SmellyConnectives — ∧ ∨ ¬ → ↔
Functions — object→object maps: Left, AgeEquality = (a special predicate)
Quantifiers — ∀ (for all), ∃ (there exists)
Key Concept — Vocabulary (Ontology)

Before representing anything in FOL you must pick a vocabulary: which constants, predicates, and functions exist in your domain. This domain-specific choice is sometimes called the ontology. For trivial examples the vocabulary is obvious; for substantial problems it is a genuine design task with multiple valid options.

Convention used in this course: variables are written in lowercase (x, y, z) and constants in uppercase (Jack, UCLA). Keeping these visually distinct prevents a great deal of confusion later.

Building Blocks: Terms

FOL sentences are assembled in two layers. The lower layer is the term — anything that refers to an object. You cannot ask whether a term is true or false; a term is not a claim, it is a name. There are exactly three ways to build one:

Key Concept — Term (a way to refer to an object)

A term is one of: (1) a constant, e.g. 2, UCLA, Jack; (2) a variable, e.g. x (used with quantifiers); or (3) a function applied to terms, e.g. FatherOf(Jack). The third case is recursive: a function's arguments are themselves terms, so you can nest — FatherOf(FatherOf(Jack)) names Jack's grandfather.

A term as a tree: each function wraps another term, bottoming out at the constant Jack. The whole tree still names a single object — Jack's grandfather.

Atomic Sentences

The upper layer is the sentence — something that can be true or false. The simplest sentence is an atomic sentence: a predicate applied to terms. This is the analogue of a single propositional variable, but with internal structure.

// predicate applied to terms → an atomic sentence
Brother(Jack, Tom)              // terms are simple constants
Brother(FatherOf(Jack), Tom)   // a term can be a nested function
Smelly(x)                      // a one-argument predicate (a "property")

Notice how the two layers fit together: FatherOf(Jack) is a term (it names an object), and feeding it into the predicate Brother produces an atomic sentence (it makes a claim). Terms refer; predicates assert.

Complex Sentences

Once you have atomic sentences, you combine them exactly as in propositional logic — using the connectives. If S, S₁, S₂ are sentences, then so are their negation, conjunction, disjunction, implication, and equivalence:

¬ Greater(1, 2)
Greater(1, 2)  ∧  ¬Greater(3, 2)
Greater(Age(Sally), Age(Tom))   // compares two people's ages

That last line is worth a second look. Age(Sally) and Age(Tom) are function terms returning numbers, and Greater is a relation between those numbers. We've stacked functions inside a predicate to say something genuinely useful — "Sally is older than Tom" — without ever naming their actual ages. This connective layer behaves just like propositional logic; the genuinely new power arrives with quantifiers in Part IV.

This term/sentence distinction is the backbone of FOL syntax: terms name, predicates assert.
Part IV

Quantifiers — The Heart of FOL

Everything so far could (clumsily) be mimicked in propositional logic. Quantifiers are what make first-order logic genuinely more powerful. A quantifier lets one sentence say something about all objects, or about at least one object, without listing them. There are two, and the trick to mastering them is a single mental technique: unfolding.

Universal Quantification: For All (∀)

The universal quantifier ∀ reads "for all." Its general shape is ∀ <variable> <sentence>. Here is the canonical example:

∀ x   At(x, UCLA)  →  Smart(x)
// "everyone at UCLA is smart"

Labeling the pieces: At is a two-argument predicate (a relation), Smart is a one-argument predicate (a property), UCLA is a constant, and x is a quantified variable.

The Unfolding Technique — Universal = Big Conjunction

To truly understand ∀ x P(x), imagine replacing x by every possible object and joining the results with AND. That is, ∀ x is a giant conjunction. This unfolding is your secret weapon: when a quantified sentence confuses you, mentally expand it and the meaning becomes obvious.

Unfolding the UCLA sentence, assuming the objects are John, Mary, … :

( At(John, UCLA) → Smart(John) )  ∧
( At(Mary, UCLA) → Smart(Mary) )  ∧  …
// and also for function terms:
( At(FatherOf(John), UCLA) → Smart(FatherOf(John)) )  ∧  …

Two things to notice. First, x ranges over function terms too — FatherOf(John), FatherOf(FatherOf(John)), and so on. Second, because functions can be nested without limit, this conjunction can be infinite. That is exactly why quantifiers are indispensable: ∀ packs a potentially infinite conjunction into one finite sentence.

The Most Common Beginner Mistake

With ∀, the natural connective is implication (At(x,UCLA) → Smart(x)), not conjunction. Writing ∀ x At(x,UCLA) ∧ Smart(x) would claim that everything in the universe is both at UCLA and smart — including rocks and the number 7. The implication form correctly restricts the claim to the things actually at UCLA.

Existential Quantification: There Exists (∃)

The existential quantifier ∃ reads "there exists." Its shape is ∃ <variable> <sentence>. Example:

∃ x   At(x, UCLA)  ∧  Tall(x)
// "someone at UCLA is tall"

Now unfold it. With existentials you don't AND the cases together — you OR them. If the only objects are Ed, Sandy, and Tom:

( At(Ed, UCLA) ∧ Tall(Ed) )  ∨
( At(Sandy, UCLA) ∧ Tall(Sandy) )  ∨  …
Key Concept — The ∀/∃ Connective Pairing

Universal ∀ unfolds to a conjunction (AND) and naturally pairs with implication (→). Existential ∃ unfolds to a disjunction (OR) and naturally pairs with conjunction (∧). Memorize this pairing — using the wrong connective is the single most frequent FOL error.

Don't Pair ∃ With Implication

Swapping the connectives breaks the meaning. ∃ x At(x,UCLA) → Tall(x) is satisfied by any object not at UCLA (a false antecedent makes the implication vacuously true), so it says almost nothing. The lecture left this swap as a home exercise precisely because unfolding reveals how badly it misfires.

Properties of Quantifiers — Order Matters

Some rearrangements of quantifiers are harmless; one is dangerous. You can convince yourself of each by unfolding.

1

Same-type quantifiers commute. ∀x ∀y means the same as ∀y ∀x (both unfold to one big AND over all pairs). Likewise ∃x ∃y equals ∃y ∃x (one big OR over all pairs).

2

Mixed quantifiers do NOT commute. ∃x ∀y is not the same as ∀y ∃x. Flipping a ∀ past an ∃ changes the meaning.

The lecture's vivid example: let Loves(x, y) mean "x loves y."

SentenceEnglish meaning
∀y ∃x Loves(x, y)Everyone in the world is loved by at least one person (each person may have a different admirer).
∃x ∀y Loves(x, y)There is one person who loves everyone in the world (a single universal lover).
Why the Flip Matters So Much

In ∀y ∃x, the x is chosen after y, so the lover can depend on who is being loved — different admirers for different people. In ∃x ∀y, the x is chosen first and must work for all y — one person loving the entire world. The order of quantifiers encodes what depends on what. This is exactly the kind of subtlety that makes FOL powerful and treacherous.

Duality & De Morgan for Quantifiers

The two quantifiers are duals: given one, you can always simulate the other. You never strictly need both. The bridge is a quantifier version of De Morgan's laws.

Recall ordinary De Morgan: pushing a negation through an AND flips it to OR, and vice versa. The quantifier rule is the analogue — pushing a negation through a quantifier flips the quantifier:

Key Concept — Quantifier Duality (De Morgan)

Moving ¬ inward past a quantifier flips it: ¬∀x P(x) ≡ ∃x ¬P(x) and ¬∃x P(x) ≡ ∀x ¬P(x). A universal "all" is just "there's no exception"; an existential "some" is just "not everything fails."

Worked example — express "everybody likes ice cream" without using ∀:

1

Start with the universal form: ∀x Likes(x, IceCream).

2

Rephrase in English: "there is no person who does not like ice cream."

3

That becomes ¬∃x ¬Likes(x, IceCream) — the inner ∃x ¬Likes… says "someone dislikes ice cream," and the outer ¬ says "that's impossible."

4

To verify, push the outer ¬ inward: it flips ∃ to ∀, giving ∀x ¬¬Likes(…). The double negation cancels, returning ∀x Likes(x, IceCream). The two forms are provably equal.

The Big Idea

Unfolding turns quantifiers into ordinary conjunctions and disjunctions — and once they're conjunctions and disjunctions, the familiar laws of propositional logic (De Morgan, double negation, implication) apply directly. Almost every quantifier puzzle in this course can be cracked by unfolding, then reasoning propositionally.

Part V

Representing Knowledge in Practice

The real skill — and the maze the lecture said we must "pick up from today" — is translating natural-language statements into correct FOL. This is harder than it looks, and the only way to get good is to work examples. Let's do the lecture's running example: a dog named Spot and his sisters.

"Spot Has Two Sisters" — At Least Two

The vocabulary is given: one predicate Sister(a, b) meaning "a is a sister of b," and one constant Spot. A first attempt — without quantifiers — fails:

// WRONG — x and y are free variables, not quantified
Sister(x, Spot) ∧ Sister(y, Spot) ∧ ¬(x = y)

The idea is right — two distinct sisters of Spot — but x and y dangle with no quantifier. We need to introduce these objects existentially:

∃ x ∃ y   Sister(x, Spot) ∧ Sister(y, Spot) ∧ ¬(x = y)
// "there exist two different things, each a sister of Spot"

Note the use of equality — the special built-in predicate — to insist the two sisters are not the same object. Without ¬(x = y), x and y could refer to the very same sister, and we'd have proved only that Spot has one sister.

Subtle: This Says "At Least Two," Not "Exactly Two"

The sentence above is satisfied even if Spot has three or four sisters — it only asserts that two distinct sisters exist, saying nothing about whether there are more. The everyday phrase "has two sisters" is genuinely ambiguous between "at least two" and "exactly two," and FOL forces us to make the choice explicit.

Tightening to "Exactly Two"

To say exactly two, we keep the "at least two" core and add a clause forbidding any third, different sister. The clean way: any sister z of Spot must already be one of our two.

∃ x ∃ y   Sister(x, Spot) ∧ Sister(y, Spot) ∧ ¬(x = y)
        ∧  ∀ z ( Sister(z, Spot) → (z = x ∨ z = y) )

Read the new line as: "and for every z, if z is a sister of Spot, then z must be either x or y." Nobody outside our pair can be a sister, so there are exactly two.

Two Forms, Provably Equal

An equivalent way to forbid a third sister is to negate its existence: ¬∃z ( Sister(z,Spot) ∧ ¬(z=x) ∧ ¬(z=y) ) — "there is no sister who is neither x nor y." Pushing that negation inward (flip ∃ to ∀, then De Morgan and double-negation on the body) transforms it exactly into the ∀z (Sister(z,Spot) → z=x ∨ z=y) form above. Two different-looking sentences that the quantifier/De Morgan machinery proves identical.

Expand: the "uniqueness" quantifier ∃! and "there is exactly one king"

There is a convenient shorthand ∃!x P(x) meaning "there exists a unique x with property P." It is not strictly needed — like everything, it can be written with ordinary ∃. "There is exactly one king":

∃ x ( King(x) ∧ ∀ y ( King(y) → x = y ) )

The first conjunct says a king exists; the second says any king y must be that same x — so there can be only one. Watch the parentheses: the closing bracket must come after the ∀y … clause. If you close it too early, the x inside ∀y falls outside the scope of ∃x and becomes a stray free variable — a real bug the lecturer deliberately made and asked the class to catch.

Scope & Free Variables

The bracket mistake above points at a deeper idea: scope. A quantifier governs a particular region of the sentence — just like a variable's scope in a programming language. Every variable must lie inside the scope of a quantifier that introduces it.

Key Concept — Free Variable & Well-Formed Formula

A free variable is one that appears with no governing quantifier. A well-formed formula (WFF) is a sentence with no free variables — every variable is quantified over. Free variables make a formula meaningless (what would Sister(x, Spot) even claim if x refers to nothing in particular?), so a legitimate FOL sentence must have none.

The programming analogy is exact. Re-using a quantified variable name inside a nested quantifier (shadowing) is legal but poor style — just like shadowing a loop variable in code. But a variable with no quantifier at all is a genuine error, the FOL equivalent of using an undeclared variable.

Part VI

Inference & a Full-Scale Example

Representing knowledge is only half the story. The point of a knowledge base is to infer new facts from it. Picture an inference engine: a box that takes a knowledge base (a set of sentences, call it Δ) plus a query (call it α) and reports whether α follows from Δ.

An inference engine asks: does query α follow from knowledge base Δ? In FOL it returns more than yes/no — it returns a binding list.

The New Twist: Binding Lists

In propositional logic the answer was a flat yes or no. FOL inference does something richer. Consider a tiny knowledge base:

∀ x ( Cat(x) → Mammal(x) )    // all cats are mammals
Cat(Tuna)                       // Tuna is a cat
Cat(Spot)                       // Spot is a cat

Query: ∃x Mammal(x) — "is there a mammal?" The answer is yes. But the engine can also hand back a binding list: the values of x that make the query true.

x = Spot    // or
x = Tuna
Answering a Query Performs a Computation

By asking an existential query "is there an x with this property?", the engine doesn't merely say yes — it finds and reports the witnesses. The act of proving the query computes the answers. If that feels like programming, it is: this is exactly the idea behind Prolog, a logic programming language built on a restricted form of FOL. You state facts and rules, ask a question, and the inference engine computes the bindings that satisfy it.

A Full-Scale Example: Reasoning About a Circuit

The lecture closed with a substantial example from the textbook — modeling a digital logic circuit (a one-bit full adder, with XOR, AND, and OR gates) in FOL. We won't encode every line, but the shape of the example teaches the most important lesson about real knowledge engineering.

Why model a circuit in logic?

Plain simulation only goes one way: given inputs, compute outputs. Logic lets you run it backward — "I observe a 0 on this output and a 1 on that one; what inputs could produce that?" — and even diagnose faults ("the output is wrong, which gate is broken?"). Logical reasoning answers questions simulation can't.

The crucial structural lesson: two parts

A real FOL knowledge base splits into two pieces, and recognizing this split is the heart of the example.

1

The general (domain) part — facts true of any circuit, independent of this specific one. Connectivity is symmetric: ∀t₁ ∀t₂ Connected(t₁,t₂) ↔ Connected(t₂,t₁). Connected ports carry equal signals. The behavior of each gate type, e.g. an OR gate outputs 1 iff some input is 1: ∀g ( Type(g)=OR → ( Signal(Out(1,g))=1 ↔ ∃n Signal(In(n,g))=1 ) ). And housekeeping like ∀s ( Signal(s)=1 ∨ Signal(s)=0 ) with ¬(1 = 0).

2

The specific (instance) part — facts about this circuit. The five gates and their types: Type(X1)=XOR, Type(X2)=XOR, and so on. The wiring: Connected(Out(1,X1), In(1,A2)), one such fact per wire.

The Logic Knows Nothing You Don't Tell It

Why state that Connected is symmetric, when "obviously" a wire connects both ways? Because the logic has no built-in common sense. Likewise you must assert ¬(1 = 0) — that the constants 1 and 0 name different objects — since in general two different constant names could denote the same object (people have nicknames!). Drop that one statement and, as the textbook shows, the whole knowledge base collapses into nonsense. Knowledge engineering is largely the discipline of making the obvious explicit.

Running it backward

With both parts in place, the diagnostic query "what inputs give output (0,1)?" becomes an existential:

∃ i₁ ∃ i₂ ∃ i₃   Signal(In(1, C1)) = i₁ ∧ …
                  ∧ Signal(Out(1, C1)) = 0 ∧ Signal(Out(2, C1)) = 1

The engine answers yes and returns the binding lists — the input combinations producing that output: (1,1,0), (1,0,1), (0,1,1). Inference ran the circuit in reverse and computed the valid inputs, exactly the binding-list idea from the cat example, now doing real work.

The Big Idea

A serious FOL knowledge base separates the general domain theory (true of all circuits, all families, all worlds of this kind) from the specific instance (this circuit, this family). The general part is reusable; the instance part is swapped in per problem. This factoring — plus the discipline of stating everything the logic can't infer on its own — is the craft called knowledge engineering.

Putting It All Together

This lecture took one big step: from a logic of flat true/false flags to a logic of structured worlds. The thread running through all six parts is the trade-off between expressiveness and cost.

Part I motivated the leap — propositional logic drowns in repetition because it cannot say "for all," so first-order logic was invented to capture general patterns compactly. Part II rebuilt the notion of a world around four ingredients: objects, properties, relations, and functions, with the litmus test that functions return objects while relations return truth values. Part III turned that picture into precise syntax: choose a vocabulary, build terms that name objects, apply predicates to terms to get atomic sentences, and combine them with connectives.

Part IV delivered the real payoff — quantifiers — and gave us the master technique of unfolding: ∀ is a (possibly infinite) conjunction paired with implication, ∃ is a disjunction paired with conjunction, the order of mixed quantifiers encodes dependence, and the two are De-Morgan duals. Part V put it to work translating English ("at least two sisters" vs. "exactly two," uniqueness, scope, free variables, well-formed formulas), where the same unfolding-and-De-Morgan machinery proves two different-looking sentences identical. Part VI showed inference returning not just yes/no but binding lists — the seed of Prolog — and the discipline of knowledge engineering: split a knowledge base into a reusable general theory and a swappable instance, and state explicitly everything the logic can't figure out on its own.

The unifying insight: once you unfold quantifiers into conjunctions and disjunctions, all the propositional reasoning you already know comes flooding back. First-order logic isn't a replacement for propositional logic — it's propositional logic given the power to talk about objects and to quantify over them.
