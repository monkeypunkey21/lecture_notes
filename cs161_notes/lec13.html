From Logic to Belief: Reasoning Under Uncertainty

An interactive guide to Lecture 13 — why classical logic broke, how degrees of belief fixed it, and the probability toolkit that powers everything that follows.

Part I

The Crisis of Monotonic Logic

For the first few decades of artificial intelligence, the field ran almost entirely on logic — the propositional and first-order systems we have spent the past several lectures building. You write down facts and rules in a knowledge base, and a reasoning engine deduces new facts that must follow. It is clean, exact, and provably correct. So why are we now, in this lecture, walking away from it?

Because in the late 1970s and early 1980s, AI researchers hit a wall. They discovered a deep, structural mismatch between how classical logic behaves and how human common-sense reasoning behaves. The decade-long struggle to resolve that mismatch — McCarthy and others called it a genuine crisis — gave birth to a whole new era of AI, and ultimately to machine learning. To understand where we are going, we first have to understand exactly what broke.

What "Monotonicity" Means

The property that caused all the trouble is called monotonicity. Here is the precise statement. Suppose you have a knowledge base Δ (delta), and some sentence α (alpha) follows from it — written Δ ⊨ α, read "delta entails alpha." Monotonicity says: if you now learn additional information β (beta) and add it to your knowledge base, then α still follows. Nothing you learn can ever retract a conclusion you already drew.

Key Concept — Monotonicity

A logic is monotonic if conclusions only ever accumulate: once Δ ⊨ α holds, then Δ ∪ {β} ⊨ α holds for any new fact β. In plain words: what is believed remains believed in the presence of new information. Classical logic is monotonic.

Why is classical logic monotonic? It falls right out of the semantics of entailment we already know. Recall that Δ ⊨ α means exactly that the models of Δ (the worlds where Δ is true) are a subset of the models of α. Now add β. The worlds satisfying both Δ and β are Models(Δ) ∩ Models(β) — a subset of Models(Δ), which was already a subset of Models(α). A subset of a subset is still a subset, so α still follows. Pure set theory.

Adding β shrinks the model set inward. Since Models(Δ) already sat inside Models(α), the smaller Models(Δ ∧ β) does too — so α survives.

The Tweety Problem

Monotonicity sounds like a feature, not a bug — until you compare it with how people actually reason. Consider the famous example that circulated in the AI literature at the time.

1

"Tweety is a bird." Call this your knowledge base Δ. I ask: does Tweety fly? You answer yes — call the conclusion "flies(Tweety)" your α. Perfectly reasonable.

2

"Tweety is a penguin." Now I hand you a new fact, β. I ask again: does Tweety fly? You answer no. Penguins do not fly.

3

You just violated monotonicity. Adding β retracted a conclusion (α) you had already drawn. A monotonic logic is forbidden from ever doing this — yet human reasoning does it constantly.

This pattern is everywhere. You leave campus at 4:30 planning to be home by 5:00, and you build your whole evening around that conclusion. Then your car won't start. One extra fact — "the car won't start" — and a conclusion that firmly followed a moment ago no longer follows. Human reasoning is non-monotonic: new information can defeat old conclusions.

The Big Idea

Classical logic is monotonic; human common-sense reasoning is non-monotonic. Interestingly, although formal logic had been developed for centuries, AI researchers were the first to actually notice this mismatch — because they were the first to try to make a machine reason like a person. Closing this gap is the entire reason this lecture exists.

Patching Logic with an "Abnormality" Predicate

The natural first instinct is: maybe the rule was just written carelessly. Instead of the blunt ∀x. bird(x) → flies(x), write a more careful rule that admits exceptions:

∀x. bird(x) ∧ ¬abnormal(x) → flies(x)

Now the logic no longer contradicts itself. If you learn "Tweety is a bird" and separately "Tweety does not fly," the system simply concludes Tweety must be abnormal — no contradiction. So far so good. But watch what we lost.

The Patch Backfires

With the careful rule, if you tell the system only "bird(Tweety)" and ask "does Tweety fly?", the honest answer is "I don't know" — because the system can't confirm ¬abnormal(Tweety). But that is exactly wrong! Tell a person "here's a bird," and they say "it flies." Every rule has unconfirmable exceptions, so under this patch you can never draw any conclusion at all. The cure is as useless as the disease.

The original rule was too strong (one penguin causes a contradiction, and from a contradiction you can derive anything, since the model set of an inconsistent base is empty and the empty set is a subset of everything). The patched rule is too weak (it paralyzes you with "I don't know"). What humans actually do is something the patched logic only gestures at: we assume birds are normal by default, unless we see evidence to the contrary. We draw the conclusion "flies(Tweety)" not from facts alone, but from facts plus a default assumption. And reasoning from facts-plus-assumptions is no longer classical logic — it is something new.

A New Kind of Logic

The realization that everyday reasoning combines hard facts with retractable default assumptions launched an entire research program: non-monotonic logics (and more broadly, non-classical logics). The hope was to extend logic so it could gracefully handle defaults and exceptions.

When Assumptions Collide: The Nixon Diamond

Extending logic with defaults turned out to be much harder than it looked, and a second example shows exactly why. Suppose your knowledge base has two default rules (everything universally quantified):

∀x. quaker(x)     ∧ ¬ab₁(x) →  pacifist(x)
∀x. republican(x) ∧ ¬ab₂(x) → ¬pacifist(x)

Note we need two different abnormality predicates, one per rule. Informally: Quakers are typically pacifists; Republicans are typically not. Now you learn that Nixon is both a Quaker and a Republican.

The "Nixon diamond." One default path concludes pacifist, the other concludes ¬pacifist. You cannot adopt both default assumptions at once.

If you make both default assumptions — "Nixon is a normal Quaker" and "Nixon is a normal Republican" — you derive that Nixon is both a pacifist and not a pacifist. A flat contradiction. So you cannot make all your default assumptions simultaneously; they clash. Now you are in the business of managing assumptions: when a set of defaults is jointly inconsistent, which ones do you give up?

Connection — Belief Revision

This "which assumptions do I throw out?" question is exactly the problem of belief revision we touched on in the very first lecture — managing a body of beliefs when contradictory information arrives. It is a whole field in its own right in philosophy, and AI contributed heavily to it. The Nixon diamond is a clean instance: deciding which default to retract is belief revision.

Part II

Degrees of Belief

Going Beyond Logic Entirely

For about a decade, researchers tried to fix logic — to bolt defaults and assumption-management onto a classical foundation. As the Nixon diamond shows, that path is thorny. Meanwhile, a competing line of thought said: maybe the problem is the foundation. Maybe we should go beyond logic.

Classical logic offers only a crude, three-way verdict on any sentence α: it follows, its negation follows, or neither. True, false, or "I don't know." That is a very blunt instrument. The new idea: instead of asking "is α true?", assign α a degree of belief — a number, by convention in the interval [0, 1], representing how strongly you believe it. Now you have infinitely many shades between certainty and ignorance.

Key Concept — Degree of Belief

A degree of belief is a number in [0, 1] attached to a sentence, capturing the extent to which an agent believes it. 0 = certain it is false, 1 = certain it is true, and everything in between is graded confidence. This replaces the crude true/false/unknown trichotomy with a continuum.

Just as there were many proposed ways to extend logic, there were many proposed calculi for manipulating degrees of belief. But the community eventually settled on probability as the right framework — it is the calculus we will use for the rest of the course. And here is the lovely twist: probability does not throw logic away. As we will see, the entire machinery is built on top of the logical notions of worlds, satisfaction, and entailment we already developed.

From Possible Worlds to Numbers

We return to the running example from our logic lectures. Three Boolean variables describe a situation: E = earthquake, B = burglary, A = alarm. Three Booleans give 2³ = 8 possible worlds — complete assignments of true/false to every variable.

Logic's view of knowledgeProbability's view of knowledge
Each world is either possible or impossible (in or out)Each world gets a number — its degree of belief
Knowledge = the set of worlds left standingKnowledge = how the belief mass is distributed
Adding facts knocks worlds outAdding facts reshapes the numbers
Answers: yes / no / neitherAnswers: a number in [0, 1]

So instead of marking each of the 8 worlds "possible" or "impossible," we assign each one a number between 0 and 1. By convention these numbers sum to 1 across all worlds. Strictly speaking the sum-to-1 rule is not logically necessary — what truly matters is the ratios between worlds. But normalizing to 1 lets us meaningfully compare different people's belief tables (Jack's distribution versus Susan's) on a common scale.

Knowledge as Distributed Mass

In logic, learning meant throwing out possible worlds — the more you know, the fewer worlds remain, the less uncertain you are. Probability keeps that spirit but softens it: instead of deleting worlds, you redistribute a fixed unit of "belief mass" among them. A world you find implausible gets a tiny share, not outright deletion.

From Worlds to Sentences — The Fundamental Equation

The table gives numbers to worlds. But we rarely care about a single complete world; we care about sentences like "was there an earthquake or a burglary?" That is not a world — it is an α that many worlds satisfy. How do we get a belief in α out of beliefs in worlds? With the single equation everything else in this course rests on:

The belief in a sentence α is the sum of the belief masses of all worlds ω that satisfy α (written ω ⊨ α). Notice this definition reuses logical satisfaction directly.

To find the belief in α, walk through every world, keep the ones where α is true, and add up their numbers. That's it. Let's exercise it on our alarm example (using illustrative table values):

1

Pr(E), probability of earthquake. Find the worlds where E is true — worlds 1, 2, 3, 4. Add their numbers: the total comes to 0.1.

2

Pr(B), probability of burglary. The worlds with B true are 1, 2, 5, 6. Adding their numbers gives 0.2.

3

A compound sentence: Pr(B ∨ ¬E). Worlds with B true are {1,2,5,6}; worlds with ¬E true are {5,6,7,8}. Their union is {1,2,5,6,7,8} — add up exactly those six numbers, counting each world once.

The Big Idea

Probability theory in this course is logic with weights. The same possible worlds, the same satisfaction relation ω ⊨ α — we simply attach a number to each world and sum. Every property we derive next is a consequence of this one definition, not an axiom handed down from on high.

Part III

Properties of Beliefs

If you have seen probability in a statistics class, you were probably handed a list of "axioms" and told to accept them. In this course they are not axioms — they are theorems that fall straight out of the one summation equation. Let's derive the essential ones, because understanding why they hold is what makes them stick.

Bounds, Inconsistency, and Validity

1

0 ≤ Pr(α) ≤ 1. A belief is a sum of some of the world-numbers. Each world-number is at least 0, so the sum can't go below 0. And the most you could ever sum is all of them, which equals 1. So every belief lives in [0, 1].

2

If α is inconsistent, Pr(α) = 0. Inconsistent (unsatisfiable, contradictory) means no world satisfies α. The sum ranges over an empty set of worlds, and an empty sum is 0 by definition.

3

If α is valid, Pr(α) = 1. Valid means α is true at every world. The sum then covers all worlds, and we already know those add up to 1.

Belief in a Negation

Here is one you will use constantly: Pr(α) + Pr(¬α) = 1. Picture the set of all worlds as a rectangle, with a circle inside it for the worlds satisfying α.

Inside the circle: worlds satisfying α. Outside (but inside the rectangle): worlds satisfying ¬α. Together they tile the whole space, so their masses add to 1.

Add up the masses inside the circle — that's Pr(α). Add up the masses outside the circle — that's Pr(¬α). Together they cover every world exactly once, so they must total 1. (This is why Pr(¬T | ¬D) = 98% follows immediately from Pr(T | ¬D) = 2% in the disease example later.)

Disjunction and the Overlap Trap

Now a subtler question that matters enormously for computation: given Pr(α) and Pr(β), can we compute Pr(α ∨ β) just by combining those two numbers? The tempting move is to add them. But look at what that double-counts.

The red overlap satisfies both α and β. Adding Pr(α) + Pr(β) counts those worlds twice.

The worlds in the red intersection satisfy both α and β, so adding the two beliefs counts them twice. The fix is to subtract that overlap exactly once — the inclusion–exclusion rule:

Pr(α ∨ β) = Pr(α) + Pr(β) − Pr(α ∧ β)
The Special Case You'll Reuse

If α and β are mutually exclusive — their model sets do not intersect, so the red region is empty and Pr(α ∧ β) = 0 — the rule simplifies to plain addition: Pr(α ∨ β) = Pr(α) + Pr(β). This is the workhorse behind case analysis in Part V.

Correct — B. Plain addition works only when there is no overlap to double-count, i.e. Pr(α ∧ β) = 0, which is precisely mutual exclusivity. In general (A) you must subtract the intersection. Equal probabilities (D) say nothing about overlap, and two valid sentences (C) overlap completely — the worst possible case for naive addition.
Part IV

Belief Change & Independence

The whole point of degrees of belief is that they change as evidence arrives — gracefully, without the contradictions that broke classical logic. To make this vivid, the professor walked through a sequence of updates on the burglary example, where the belief table was synthesized from a causal graph: a burglary can set off the alarm, and so can an earthquake.

The causal structure: both burglary and earthquake can trigger the alarm. This graph is what the belief table was built to reflect.

Explaining Away

Follow the burglary belief as evidence piles on. It starts at Pr(B) = 0.2.

1

You learn the alarm went off. A burglary is now more plausible, so Pr(B | A) jumps to about 0.74. The alarm is evidence for burglary.

2

Then you learn there was also an earthquake. Now the alarm has an alternative explanation. Your belief in burglary drops — to about 0.25. The earthquake "explains away" the alarm.

3

Alternatively, you learn there was definitely no earthquake. Then burglary is the only remaining explanation for the alarm, so the belief climbs even higher — to about 0.96.

Plausible Reasoning, For Free

These intuitive shifts — belief rising and falling sensibly as competing explanations appear — are called patterns of plausible reasoning. They were extremely hard to capture in the non-monotonic-logic era. Probability produces them automatically: part from the math itself, part from the causal structure baked into the table. Judea Pearl's late-1980s book first laid this out comprehensively.

Independence as Unchanged Belief

In this same example, learning about a burglary did not change the belief in an earthquake: Pr(E | B) = Pr(E) = 0.1. When evidence about one thing leaves your belief in another untouched, the two are independent.

Key Concept — Independence

α is independent of β when Pr(α | β) = Pr(α) — knowing β does not shift your belief in α. This is symmetric: if β doesn't change your mind about α, then α doesn't change your mind about β. (You may have seen the equivalent product form Pr(α ∧ β) = Pr(α)·Pr(β); we prefer the belief-change form because it says what independence means.)

Why is it symmetric? Bayes conditioning gives Pr(α | β) = Pr(α ∧ β) / Pr(β). If that equals Pr(α), rearranging gives Pr(α ∧ β) = Pr(α)·Pr(β), which is symmetric in α and β — so the relationship runs both ways.

Independence Is Dynamic — Conditional Independence

Here is the crucial subtlety. Burglary and earthquake are independent on their own. But run the explaining-away story again, this time given that the alarm has triggered. Now learning about an earthquake very much changes your belief in burglary (it drops from 0.74 to about 0.25). Independence evaporated the moment we conditioned on the alarm.

Independence Depends on What You Know

Two things can be independent unconditionally yet become dependent once you learn something else. Whether B and E are independent is not a fixed fact about them — it depends on the rest of your knowledge. This is exactly why we need a conditional notion.

Key Concept — Conditional Independence

α is conditionally independent of β given γ when Pr(α | β, γ) = Pr(α | γ) — once you already know γ, additionally learning β tells you nothing new about α. Equivalently, Pr(α ∧ β | γ) = Pr(α | γ)·Pr(β | γ). Plain independence is the special case where γ is empty.

Connection — Bayesian Networks Ahead

Conditional independence is the hinge the rest of the course swings on. When we reach Bayesian networks, you'll see that by drawing a causal graph — just specifying what causes what — you implicitly declare a rich set of conditional independences. The graph lets us read off what an agent believes is independent of what, which is what makes large probabilistic models tractable.

Part V

The Inference Toolkit

We now collect three identities — all consequences of the fundamental equation plus Bayes conditioning — that we will combine to answer real questions. Each one is a tool for breaking a belief we don't directly know into beliefs we do.

The Chain Rule

The first decomposes the belief in a big conjunction into a product of conditional beliefs, peeling off one conjunct at a time:

Pr(α₁ ∧ α₂ ∧ … ∧ αₙ) =
   Pr(α₁ | α₂ … αₙ) · Pr(α₂ | α₃ … αₙ) · … · Pr(αₙ)

It is proved by repeatedly applying Bayes conditioning and rearranging — not hard, and in fact your homework asks you to do it. The pattern to remember: the belief in a conjunction equals the belief in each piece given everything that follows it. We won't lean on it heavily today, but it reappears when we explain the meaning of Bayesian networks.

Case Analysis (Law of Total Probability)

This is the one you'll reach for constantly. Suppose you can't compute Pr(α) directly, but you can split the world of possibilities into cases β₁, …, βₙ that are mutually exclusive (no overlap) and exhaustive (cover everything).

The cases β₁…β₅ partition all worlds. The sentence α (orange) is sliced by them, so Pr(α) is just the sum of its slices.

Because α is chopped into non-overlapping slices, one per case, you can add the slices up. Written with Bayes conditioning, the slice in case βᵢ is "belief in α if it were case βᵢ" weighted by "how likely case βᵢ is":

Pr(α) = ∑ᵢ Pr(α | βᵢ) · Pr(βᵢ)
// two-case version:
Pr(α) = Pr(α | β)·Pr(β) + Pr(α | ¬β)·Pr(¬β)
Why "Case Analysis" Is the Better Name

The textbook name is "law of total probability," but think of it as case analysis: "what would my belief in α be in each scenario, weighted by how likely that scenario is?" You enumerate exhaustive, non-overlapping scenarios and take a weighted average. That framing makes it obvious and tells you how to use it.

Bayes' Rule

Finally, the star of the show. Bayes conditioning says Pr(α | β) = Pr(α ∧ β)/Pr(β). Apply the same rule the other way for Pr(β | α), notice the numerators are the same conjunction, and rearrange. Out pops Bayes' rule:

Bayes' rule. Algebraically it is just Bayes conditioning rearranged — yet it earned its own name.

Why does a simple rearrangement deserve a famous name? Because of what the pieces mean. Let α be a cause and β an effect — say, α = disease, β = symptom. Then the left side, Pr(α | β), is the probability of the cause given the effect — the diagnostic question we actually want answered. The right side needs Pr(β | α): the probability of the effect given the cause.

The Big Idea

It is usually easy to know effect given cause (a doctor readily states "the chance of a headache given the flu") but hard to know cause given effect ("the chance of flu given a headache"). Bayes' rule lets you compute the hard, valuable direction from the easy, available one. When we build Bayesian networks, you'll supply the easy effect-given-cause numbers, and inference will run them backward for you.

Part VI

Worked Example: The Disease Test

Now we put every tool together on the canonical example. A patient takes a test for a disease, and the test comes back positive. What is the probability they actually have the disease? The answer is famously surprising.

Setting It Up

Two binary variables: D = has the disease, T = test is positive. We are told three facts:

1

Base rate. One in a thousand people have the disease: Pr(D) = 1/1000.

2

False positive rate 2%. The test reads positive on healthy people 2% of the time: Pr(T | ¬D) = 2/100. So Pr(¬T | ¬D) = 98/100 (they must sum to 1).

3

False negative rate 5%. The test reads negative on sick people 5% of the time: Pr(¬T | D) = 5/100. So Pr(T | D) = 95/100 — the true positive rate.

Notice every number we were handed is an effect-given-cause probability (test result given disease status). The causal story is Disease → Test. We want the reverse, diagnostic direction — Pr(D | T) — which is exactly the job Bayes' rule was built for.

Solving It

1

Write Bayes' rule. Pr(D | T) = Pr(T | D) · Pr(D) / Pr(T). We have Pr(T | D) = 95/100 and Pr(D) = 1/1000. We are missing the denominator Pr(T) — the chance a random person tests positive.

2

Get Pr(T) by case analysis. Split on whether the person has the disease: Pr(T) = Pr(T | D)·Pr(D) + Pr(T | ¬D)·Pr(¬D). Every one of those four numbers is already in hand.

3

Plug in. Pr(T) = (95/100)(1/1000) + (2/100)(999/1000) = 2093/100000. The positives are dominated by false positives from the huge healthy population.

4

Finish Bayes' rule. Pr(D | T) = (95/100)(1/1000) / (2093/100000) = 95/2093 ≈ 4.5%.

The Surprise

A positive result on a fairly accurate test still leaves only a ~4.5% chance of disease. Why? The disease is rare (1 in 1000), so the vast healthy majority generates far more false positives than the few sick people generate true positives. Ignoring the base rate — the "base rate fallacy" — is one of the most common mistakes in reasoning under uncertainty, and the math here is the cure.

What We Combined

This single answer used the whole toolkit: Bayes' rule to flip cause and effect, case analysis to compute the denominator Pr(T), and the negation rule to turn false-positive/negative rates into their complements. Three tools, one clean number.

Putting It All Together

This lecture is a hinge in the course. We began by exposing the fatal flaw in everything that came before it: classical logic is monotonic, but human reasoning is not. The Tweety problem, the abnormality-predicate patch that paralyzes you with "I don't know," and the Nixon diamond's clashing assumptions all showed that fixing logic from the inside is a quagmire — and that managing conflicting assumptions is really the old problem of belief revision in disguise.

So the field went beyond logic, replacing crude true/false/unknown verdicts with graded degrees of belief, and settled on probability as the calculus. Crucially, we did not abandon what we learned — we built on it. Probability here is logic with weights: the same possible worlds, the same satisfaction relation ω ⊨ α, with one number per world and a single summation equation, Pr(α) = Σω ⊨ α Pr(ω), from which everything else follows.

From that one equation we derived the properties of beliefs (bounds, negation, inclusion–exclusion), watched belief change gracefully under evidence — including "explaining away," the very plausible-reasoning patterns logic struggled with — and saw that independence is dynamic, which forced the richer notion of conditional independence. Then we assembled the inference toolkit: the chain rule, case analysis, and Bayes' rule, and combined all three to crack the disease-test example, where a positive result still meant only a 4.5% chance of illness.

The Through-Line

Logic asks "is α true?" Probability asks "how strongly should I believe α, and how should that shift as evidence arrives?" The first question shattered on the rocks of common-sense reasoning; the second, answered with probability built atop logic's worlds, is what makes modern, evidence-driven AI — including machine learning — possible.

What's Next

The closing observation pointed the way: so far our variables have been binary (D/¬D, T/¬T), inherited from propositional logic. Next we generalize to multi-valued variables — a variable that can be red, green, or blue — and then to Bayesian networks, where a causal graph encodes conditional independences, you specify only easy effect-given-cause numbers, and inference runs Bayes' rule backward at scale. From there the course bridges into machine learning: learning these belief models directly from data. Keep the summation equation and Bayes' rule close — everything ahead is built on them.
